# Grill Canon

This vault's contradictions didn't come from writing prose — they came from re-brainstorming the same fork in a fresh ChatGPT session without ever checking back against what got decided last time. This skill is the fix: sit down, resolve the fork on the record, edit the vault right then, and move on. One real decision beats another five brainstorm passes.

## The two things NOT to do

1. **Don't invent lore to fill a gap.** Almost every open item in this vault already has 2-3 drafted options sitting in it — someone brainstormed them months or years ago and Dave never picked. Your job is to surface those verbatim, not paper over the gap with something new that sounds plausible. Only write a genuinely new answer if Dave states one himself, or if you check and there really is no drafted option on record.

2. **Don't treat this like engineering.** There's no objectively correct answer to "did Mickey diablerize Kayla" — there's only what Dave actually means his story to be. Your questions should feel like helping him remember or discover his own intent, not defending which option is "best." If he answers, that's final. Don't relitigate it because a different option seemed cleaner to you.

## Where the open items live

- **Primary source, and where to start unless Dave says otherwise:** `docs/Questions/Development Notes & Unresolved Questions.md`. This is a flat list of flagged contradictions, unanswered questions, and unreconciled drafts, mostly organized by character/topic with a `[[wikilink]]` heading.
- **Secondary:** `docs/Questions/Out of Scope Ideas.md` — a different flavor of item. These are things that got deliberately cut or left as hypotheticals, not live forks. Don't treat "resurface a cut idea" the same as "resolve a contradiction" — ask Dave whether he actually wants to revisit it before pushing him to decide anything about it.
- **Also check the pages themselves.** Individual character/event/book pages often carry their own inline flags — "not reconciled," "flagged, not resolved," "X vs Y, worth confirming with Dave" — that never made it into the Development Notes list, or that elaborate on an item that IS listed there. When you pull up an item, open the pages it links to and skim for any other loose flag on the same topic before you start the interview, so you're not missing half the fork.

## Building the queue

At the start of a session, read the full Development Notes file and pull out the distinct open items — there are usually 20-30. Group items that are clearly the same underlying fork wearing two names (e.g. the Huntsman's two incompatible resolutions, Ashley's Awakening age drifting between 5/6/kindergarten across sources) so Dave resolves them once, not twice.

Present the queue and let Dave drive: pick where to start, skip around, defer things, batch a handful of small ones together. Don't force top-to-bottom order — he thinks in hyperfocus bursts, and a rigid checklist fights that rather than using it. It's fine if a session only clears three items and leaves the rest for next time, as long as the three that got touched are actually locked.

## The interview, per item

For each item you're working through:

1. **State the fork plainly.** One or two sentences: here's the contradiction, here's why it's a contradiction (which two pages/sources disagree, or which question was left open).
2. **Surface what's already drafted, verbatim.** Quote the actual competing versions instead of summarizing them into mush — if three mechanisms were brainstormed for how Mickey learned about the ritual, name all three specifically. Dave often recognizes "oh right, it's obviously the second one" the moment he sees his own past phrasing again.
3. **Ask a question that forces the choice to feel real, not abstract.** Not "which do you want?" — pressure-test it. "If the vendetta targets Mickey, why would Mickey bother manipulating someone who already hates him instead of just avoiding him? If it targets Rudy instead, why does Mickey orchestrate the kidnapping at all rather than letting the vendetta play out on its own?" Make him feel the downstream implications of each fork, because that's usually what actually surfaces which one he means.
4. **If he answers, that's final.** Don't keep arguing for the other option because it seemed tidier. Write it down.
5. **If he hesitates or says "I don't know," dig one level deeper once** — what scene does this actually need to serve? why does this one feel harder than the others? — but then let him explicitly defer if he wants to. Don't extract an answer he doesn't have yet just to close the item.
6. **If none of the drafted options fit,** that's a fine outcome — record whatever new answer he actually gives, in his own words where you can.

## Editing the vault, live — not at the end

The moment a decision lands, edit the page(s) it lives on before moving to the next item. Waiting to batch edits at the end is how things get lost.

- Rewrite the "flagged, not resolved" / "not reconciled" / "open question" language on the affected page(s) so it reads as settled canon, not a maybe.
- Add a Timeline entry noting this was decided live in a session today (use the actual date) — phrase it distinctly from the "per [chat name], full data export" citations used everywhere else in this vault, since this is a fresh decision Dave is making now, not something recovered from old ChatGPT history. Something like: `- **2026-07-15 (decided live, grill-canon session):** ...`
- Mark the item resolved on `Development Notes & Unresolved Questions.md` using the pattern already in use on that page — strike through the original question text with `~~...~~`, then `**Resolved:**` followed by a short summary and a page link. For a quick one-liner item, the simpler `Resolved as [[X]] — see her page.` form is fine too. Don't delete resolved items outright; the strikethrough is the record that this used to be open.

## The ripple check

Before moving to the next item, spend one beat checking whether the decision you just locked contradicts something else in the vault — a related character page, a different book's outline, an already-settled Timeline entry. This exact failure mode (a decision on one page silently not matching a related page) is how this vault's debt built up in the first place, and it's cheap to catch immediately and expensive to catch later.

If you find a new ripple:
- If it's trivial to fix in the same breath, fix it as part of this same edit.
- If it's a real fork of its own, don't silently leave it — say so out loud and either add it to the queue as a new item or ask Dave if he wants to knock it out right now while it's fresh.

## Wrapping up a session

When Dave says he's done, or the queue for this session is empty, give a short recap: what actually got decided, what got explicitly deferred and why, and what's still sitting open. This recap is the actual deliverable — it's the proof that today produced real, locked decisions instead of another round of talking that'll need re-litigating in six months.

## One more thing

This project is semi-autobiographical — Dave's said as much directly. Grill the lore hard. Don't grill the man. The pointed questions are in service of getting his actual intent on the page, not testing him.
