First, I'm writing a story set in King County, Colorado - a fictional county - that I'm calling "What Wasn't and What Was: A King County Chronicle." I do have other stories in the works featuring the same characters and location but is it too presumptive to add "A King County Chronicle" to the first published work, even if that's all that is ever published? 

Not at all — in fact, it’s a smart move.

Adding "A King County Chronicle" to your title suggests a few key things to potential readers and publishers:
✅ What It Communicates:

    Shared Universe: It signals that this story is part of a larger world, which appeals to readers who enjoy recurring characters, places, or themes.

    Potential for More: It creates a framework for future stories — even if you never publish them, you’ve left the door open.

    Cohesive Branding: It gives your fictional setting a proper name and identity, making it easier to build recognition over time.

🧠 Even If It’s the Only One Published…

It doesn’t feel presumptuous unless the subtitle is grandiose or misleading (e.g., "Volume 1 of the Epic Saga of King County"). “A Chronicle” is modest, almost humble — like you’re recording one of many possible stories from the place, not claiming a series or demanding sequels.

It’s no different than books like:

    The Ballad of Black Tom: A Lovecraftian Tale

    The Sisters Brothers: A Western

    The Secret Place: A Dublin Murder Squad Novel (even though it can be read standalone)

🔁 If You Later Publish More:

    You’ll be glad you started this way.

    You won’t have to rebrand your first book to match the others.

📘 Suggested Format

If you’re worried about it sounding too heavy, here are a few variations:

    What Wasn't and What Was (A King County Chronicle)

    What Wasn't and What Was: A Chronicle of King County

    What Wasn't and What Was – From the King County Files

Next, self-publishing. Beside Amazon and Drive-thru Fiction, are there any other respectable venues for self publishing? If I do self publish, does that eliminate the chance that the self-published work can be published by someone else, later? 

Great questions — let's break this into two parts: venues for self-publishing and what happens to your rights after self-publishing.
📚 PART 1: Where Else Can You Self-Publish?

Besides Amazon KDP and DriveThruFiction, here are some respectable self-publishing platforms for fiction writers:
1. Itch.io

    Originally for indie games, but increasingly home to experimental fiction, zines, RPG stories, and multimedia narratives.

    Great for cross-media or weird/genre-bending stories.

    Flexible pricing and community-focused.

2. Smashwords

    Focuses on eBooks, and distributes to Barnes & Noble, Kobo, Apple Books, and libraries.

    Now merged with Draft2Digital, which offers great formatting tools and wide distribution.

3. Draft2Digital

    One of the best alternatives to Amazon.

    Handles ebook distribution to multiple storefronts, including Apple, Kobo, and libraries.

    Clean dashboard, decent reporting, easy to use.

    Does not publish PDFs or print books, but integrates with print-on-demand via BookBaby and others.

4. Lulu

    Offers print-on-demand (paperback, hardcover) and eBook options.

    Can distribute to Amazon, B&N, and other retailers.

    Good for zines, photo books, and specialty formats.

5. IngramSpark

    Best for wide print distribution, especially to independent bookstores and libraries.

    More professional, but costs money (setup fees, though sometimes waivable with promo codes).

    Print quality often superior to Amazon's.

6. Gumroad or Ko-fi

    For selling direct to readers (no middleman).

    Great if you want full control of pricing and reader data.

    Works best if you already have an audience.

🛑 PART 2: Can You Still Get Traditionally Published Later?

Short answer: Yes, but it depends.
🧠 Self-Publishing Isn’t a Dead End

Many authors have self-published a book and later signed a traditional publishing deal for:

    A revised version of the same book

    A sequel or new work in the same world

    A new series entirely

Examples:

    Andy Weir self-published The Martian before Crown picked it up.

    Erin Morgenstern initially shared The Night Circus as short fiction and built interest.

📌 However:

Traditional publishers usually want unpublished rights. If a self-published book has:

    Sold poorly (e.g., <1,000 copies), publishers may pass unless you’re revising/rebranding it.

    Sold well (e.g., 10k+), that success can help land a deal, though they may want print/eBook rights separately.

💡 If You Might Pitch It Later:

    Consider publishing under a "soft imprint" (your own label or neutral branding).

    Use ISBNs you control (don’t let Amazon lock it with a free ISBN if you plan to republish).

    Be ready to rework the manuscript if a publisher wants a fresh take.

🧭 Summary
Topic	Recommendation

Avoid Dead Ends?	Yes — self-publishing doesn’t block future traditional deals, but tread mindfully
Want Trad Later?	Use your own ISBN, avoid KU exclusivity, and build a solid reader base
