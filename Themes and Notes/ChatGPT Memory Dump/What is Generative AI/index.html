    What Wasn't and What Was: A King County Chronicle - What is Generative AI¶

    Date: 2026-07-14 Source: chat.openai.com

    ChatGPT¶

    If you used AI specifically to help with editing — things like grammar, clarity, flow, or restructuring — you can disclose that in a straightforward, low-key way. It doesn’t need to be dramatic or overly detailed. Here are a few clear, professional examples tailored for different tones and formats:

    📘 Acknowledgments Page (Formal or Professional Tone)

    “Editing assistance provided in part by AI tools, including ChatGPT, for clarity and phrasing. All final edits were reviewed and approved by the author.”

    Or:

    “The author used generative AI tools during the editing process to improve flow, clarity, and structure. All decisions and final wording remain the author’s own.”

    ✍️ Author’s Note (Personal or Conversational Tone)

    “I used ChatGPT as a sounding board during the editing process — for rewording awkward sentences, checking tone, and tightening up the prose. Every choice was ultimately mine, but it helped to have a tireless editor at my side.”

    📜 Colophon or End Credit (Simple Statement)

    “Edited with the assistance of ChatGPT.”

    Or:

    “Revisions supported by generative AI tools.”

    ✅ When to disclose AI editing:

    If you’re publishing professionally or academically.

    If you’re entering a writing contest that has guidelines about AI use.

    If your audience values transparency and craft (especially in creative nonfiction or literary fiction circles).

    ❌ When you don’t necessarily need to disclose:

    If you're only using it for minor grammar checks (like Grammarly).

    If your edits were entirely rewritten in your voice and AI played a small role.

    If you’re sharing fiction informally (e.g., fanfic, blogs, personal sites), and transparency isn’t a concern for the context.

    Let me know your tone or genre, and I can write a polished version that fits your specific book or story.

    So, if I use AI as a sounding board, utilize ideas presented by ai, and then refer back to ai during the editing process - but everything suggested by AI was rewritten - or specifically NOT rewritten in some, but not most cases within a single body of work - by me...

    ChatGPT¶

    That use is still ethically sound, as long as you’re honest about it if disclosure is expected — and it sounds like you're being very thoughtful and intentional, which is excellent. What you’re describing is a hybrid creative process, where:

    You generate and refine ideas with AI help (like brainstorming with a collaborator),

    You write and rewrite in your own voice, keeping or reshaping suggestions,

    Occasionally, you retain an AI-generated phrase or sentence without rewriting — but only when it fits your intent and tone.

    That’s still authorship. It’s a modern form of collaboration — just one where the other "voice" isn't human.

    ✅ How to disclose that specific use (clear, honest, and nuanced): 📘 Acknowledgments (Professional or Literary)

    “This work was developed with the assistance of generative AI tools, including ChatGPT, used as a creative sounding board and editing aid. All text was written or adapted by the author, though select phrases or ideas were retained where they aligned with the author’s intent.”

    ✍️ Author’s Note (Personal, Transparent)

    “Throughout the writing of this piece, I used ChatGPT to bounce around ideas, explore structure, and polish the prose. Most AI-suggested language was rewritten in my own voice, though a few lines remain as originally suggested. Every inclusion was intentional, and the heart of the work — its meaning, purpose, and style — is mine.”

    📜 Minimalist Disclosure (Colophon-style)

    “Written and edited with the aid of ChatGPT. All final content was reviewed, rewritten, or approved by the author.”

    🔍 Key Considerations:

    Intent matters: You're using AI as a tool, not a substitute for authorship.

    Transparency matters in context: If you're submitting for publication, a grant, or a contest, this kind of statement may be appreciated or even required.

    Control matters: You remain the curator, the editor, the author. Keeping a few lines intact doesn’t change that — people quote other writers all the time, but it’s the way you use those lines that makes it yours.

    Would it be safe to include "No concept, character, or storyline is wholly the product of AI?"

    ChatGPT¶

    Yes — it is absolutely safe and very effective to include a line like:

    “No concept, character, or storyline is wholly the product of AI.”

    This phrasing clearly communicates that:

    You are the primary author.

    AI was a tool, not a source of full creative generation.

    Every major element — plot, worldbuilding, characters — originated from you, even if AI helped shape or refine them.

    💬 Why it works well:

    Direct: It draws a clear line between human and AI authorship.

    Defensible: If challenged, you can easily back it up by showing notes, drafts, or the extent of edits.

    Respectful of readers: It assures them the heart and structure of the work are yours, not machine-made.

    Optional Enhanced Version (adds clarity for publishers or readers):

    “No concept, character, or storyline is wholly the product of AI. Generative AI tools, including ChatGPT, were used as part of the creative and editorial process, but all narrative decisions and original ideas were authored by the writer.”

    ChatGPT¶

    Yes — unfortunately, yes, some publications would exclude your work even if:

    You wrote the entire story yourself,

    Only used ChatGPT for light editing suggestions, and

    Rewrote or approved every change.

    📚 Why would they exclude it? Because many literary journals, contests, and traditional publishers are adopting strict "no AI involvement" policies — and they often:

    Define any use of generative AI (even as a grammar or style assistant) as a disqualifier,

    Err on the side of caution to avoid legal or creative gray areas,

    Want to avoid any perception of AI-generated work slipping through under the radar.

    This is especially true in literary fiction, poetry, and contest submissions, where “original human authorship” is a core part of the judging or publishing ethos.

    🛑 Typical language you might see:

    “Submissions must be the sole work of the author, with no AI-generated assistance in any part of the writing or editing process.”

    This wording often includes brainstorming, structure, grammar suggestions, or style edits — even if the author made every final decision.

    ✅ However — many other publishers are okay with it if:

    You are transparent,

    AI was used as a tool, not a creator,

    You maintained full creative control.

    In fact, some publishers are starting to request a brief AI use disclosure rather than prohibit it outright.

    Always read the submission guidelines carefully. If they don’t mention AI, check their FAQ or email them directly.

    Keep a clean version of your story that was not edited with AI — just in case you want to submit to stricter publications.

    Disclose appropriately when needed. Some places now have checkboxes or optional disclosure sections about AI use.

    What if I use AI only for outlining and storyboarding -- every idea is mine, there's just a little polish on order and pacing -- and then I never touch AI for the writing and editing?

    ChatGPT¶

    That’s a smart, thoughtful workflow — and in most cases, it lands in a gray area that's becoming increasingly common. But here’s the hard truth: some publishers and contests will still exclude your work for any AI involvement — even if it's just for outlining or storyboarding.

    🔍 Let’s break it down: ✅ What you're doing:

    All ideas are your own.

    AI helps you organize or polish the pacing of a story outline.

    You do all the writing and editing yourself, without AI assistance.

    ⚖️ Ethically? This is completely ethical. You're the creative force, and you're using a tool to help with structure — just like using beat sheets, Save the Cat!, or a friend to bounce off ideas. 📚 Publishing-wise? It depends on the publisher's policy. Here's how it tends to break down: Publisher Policy TypeIs Your Approach Allowed?Strict “no AI in any part”❌ No“No AI writing/editing” only✅ YesDisclosure required✅ Yes (with a note)No clear policy🤷 Maybe — ask or err on the side of caution

    📘 Example Disclosure You Could Use: If required (or if you're being transparent in an author’s note), you could say:

    “AI tools were used only during the outlining phase to help organize and pace my prewritten ideas. The writing and editing of the story were entirely my own.”

    This makes it clear that:

    The creativity is yours.

    AI was a tool for planning, not creation.

    The prose and voice are 100% human-authored.

    ✅ Tip: If you want to keep doors open to strict publishers, you might:

    Do your outlining on paper or in a doc, based on ideas developed without AI.

    Save AI-based outlines for drafts that aren’t being submitted to no-AI markets.
