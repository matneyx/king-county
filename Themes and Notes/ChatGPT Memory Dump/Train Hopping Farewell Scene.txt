The sun wasn't up yet, but the yard was already humming—low and steady, like the sound of the earth chewing gravel. The rails clicked and groaned under slow-moving wheels. Nobody shouted. Nobody had to. You either knew the rhythm or you didn’t.

Moss crouched low behind a stack of busted pallets near the service road, their hoodie drawn tight, patched jeans crusted in dirt and oil. Their boots were split at the toes, taped up with strips of neon duct tape. You could still read “FUCK ICE” scribbled on the left one in sharpie, half-faded by rain.

Casey stood with them—hands jammed in the pockets of a too-big Carhartt, sleeves rolled twice. She looked like she hadn’t slept, and maybe she hadn’t. Her eyeliner was smudged like war paint, but her eyes stayed soft. She wasn't riding out—not this time.

“They're lining it up,” Moss whispered, eyes on the break in the trees where the mainline peeled off. “That’ll be the southbound.”

Casey nodded, chewing her bottom lip like it owed her something. She pulled a little bundle from inside her coat—wrapped in a bandana, tied up tight.

“What’s this?” Moss asked, taking it with a half-suspicious squint.

“Some protein bars. Couple smokes. My last lighter. And that little rabbit’s foot I found in Flagstaff.” She paused. “You said you liked it.”

“I said it was creepy.”

“Yeah. But you kept it.”

Moss tucked the bundle into their bag. Didn’t say thank you. Just gave a half-shrug, half-nod. The way people do when saying it out loud would make it too real.

The train clanked hard, then hissed—a brake test. That was the cue.

“You could still come,” Moss said, not quite looking at her. “I don’t mind. We got space.”

Casey shook her head. “My brother’s clean again. I said I’d be around. Might get that kitchen job at Donna’s.”

Moss nodded again. More serious this time. “That’s good.”

They hugged, fast and rough. No rocking, no whispers. Just arms and release.

As Moss jogged toward the service ladder of the grainer, Casey called out—not loud, just enough: “Hey!”

Moss turned.

“Don’t forget your name. Alright?”

Moss grinned, all teeth and smoke and too much road. “It’s spray-painted under a bridge in Omaha. I’ll find it again.”

Then they were gone, swallowed by the rumble and the wind and the weight of the next hundred miles.

The gravel crunched under her boots, sharp and mean. Casey kept her hood up even though there wasn’t anyone around. The yard dogs didn’t usually come this far out, but you never knew—especially with the summer kids stirring up heat and leaving trash behind. She stayed low by the pallet stacks near the fence line, watching the shadows move slow and heavy in the pre-dawn fog.

Moss was already there. Always early, always wound up before a hop. They were crouched down like a coiled spring, eyes locked on the ghostlights at the edge of the yard. Their backpack sat open beside them, everything crammed in: bungee cords, tarp, peanut butter jar with a rusted lid, someone else’s flannel shirt tied to the side.

Casey felt it before Moss saw her. That flicker—like nerves skipping over skin. Moss turned and gave her a grin that barely touched their face.

“You’re late,” they said, voice low and tight.

“It ain’t rolling yet,” she replied, settling beside them. “You’re the only person I know who shows up early for a crime.”

They both laughed, quiet and close to the ground.

For a while, they just watched. The yard moved like a tired animal, coughing out steel and breath. Some other riders were probably posted farther down, but this was their spot—half-covered, half-forgotten. She liked it that way. No goodbyes at the platform, no onlookers. Just freight and fog.

She pulled the bundle from her coat. It was stupid. Sentimental. But she'd packed it last night anyway.

“What’s this?” Moss asked, the grin fading as they took it.

“It’s nothing. Some food. Lighter. That rabbit’s foot.”

Moss narrowed their eyes. “The creepy one from Flagstaff?”

“You said it was lucky.”

“I said it was probably cursed.”

Casey shrugged. “Same difference.”

She waited for a thank you, but Moss just nodded and shoved it into the side pocket of their pack like it was just another bit of ballast. Which, she guessed, it was.

They didn’t hug right away. Moss shifted their weight, then leaned in, one arm loose around her shoulder. She pressed her forehead to their chest, just for a second, then let go. Any longer would’ve been too much. Too heavy.

“You could still come,” Moss mumbled. “You wouldn’t be in the way.”

Casey shook her head. “My brother’s out of detox. He needs me around. I think I can get a job at Donna’s again. Maybe keep a couch this time.”

Moss nodded. Not sad. Just accepting. They never held on too tight.

The train hissed, a long metal breath that made her chest ache.

Moss stood, slung the pack over one shoulder, and started moving toward the grainer. She watched their boots hit the ties, sure and steady.

“Hey!” she called. Not loud, not desperate.

They turned, a silhouette in the mist.

“Don’t forget your name,” she said. Her throat burned when she said it. It was stupid. It was all she had.

Moss flashed a grin over their shoulder. “It’s spray-painted under a bridge in Omaha.”

And then they were climbing.

And then they were gone.

She stood there a while, long after the steel was out of sight and the yard was still again. Watching the empty rails. Holding the quiet.

And then she walked home.

☀️ NAME: Rye

    Age: Maybe 17. Could’ve been 20. Moss never asked.

    Vibe: Road-worn, cocky, too clever for his own damn good.

    Wore: A neon orange hoodie with “SECURITY” ironed on the back. Total joke. Always wore it inside out.

    Talked like: A podcast host, all confidence and bullshit wisdom.

    Called Moss: “Cavekid.” Said it like a compliment. Like they were carved out of myth.

🛤️ THE STORY

Moss and Rye caught out together in Nebraska. Cold night, steel rails humming like lullabies. They’d made jungle under a half-collapsed boxcar, huddled around a crushed can of Sterno and a jar of peaches someone dropped in Topeka.

Moss had already said no.

    “We wait. Dawn’s bad luck.”

Rye just laughed. Tossed his bag over the chain-link fence and stretched like he was heading to brunch.

    “You and your faerie bedtime rules. It’s a goddamn sunrise, not a banshee.”

    “It’s when I came back,” Moss said. Not angry. Just... low.

    “Then maybe it’s when you start moving forward.”

He grinned like a cliff dared him to jump.
🚨 WHAT HAPPENED

The grainer rolled slow—perfect for a lazy hop. Rye jogged up, grabbed the ladder, and started to swing on.

Then everything went wrong.

The bull wasn’t supposed to be there. No lights, no warning. Just there, on foot, real old-school.

Moss watched it unfold in slow motion:

    A shout.

    A flashlight beam slicing across Rye’s back.

    A bang—something hit metal, not flesh.

    But then Rye slipped.

Landed hard.

One leg under the wheel.

Moss didn’t scream. Didn’t cry. Didn’t move until the bull turned their back.

Then they were beside Rye. Holding his hand. Rye’s hoodie inside-out and soaked in blood.

    “You were right,” he whispered.
    “Cavekid... you were right.”

He didn’t die. Not right then. But Moss ran before the ambulance came.
🕯️ THE AFTERMATH

Moss never saw Rye again. Maybe he’s alive, limping through Denver on a peg leg. Maybe not.

But since that day:

    Moss never rides at dawn.

    Won’t speak Rye’s name aloud.

    Leaves orange scraps tied to fenceposts near trainyards—torn hoodie bits, burnt caution tape. A warning for others. A shrine.

Sometimes, when the sky begins to warm and the steel starts to glow with morning light, Moss swears they hear him:

    “Just ride, Cavekid. Just go.”

And Moss waits. Because they remember.

Because dawn still means blood on the rails.
