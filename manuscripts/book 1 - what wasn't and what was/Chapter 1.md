The child stumbled quickly through the gap between two trees, bursting from the canopy-covered creekside into the open field ahead of them.  They held up their immaculately clean hands to shield their eyes from the morning sun as it crested a silhouette of trees, power lines, and single-wide trailers off to their right.  Everything about this was all wrong. The colors were muted, and the smells in the air were dull, but at the same time everything was more real than the child had ever experienced.

The child's attention was on the rising sun in the distance, and their steps were awkward and clumsy, like a newborn doe, and without warning the child tripped on an exposed root, slamming into the ground.  Like the sights and smells, everything about the pain was all wrong.  Their entire body was numb, as if feeling the world through a soft blanket, but the feeling of the rough dirt against their palms and their knees was intense; for the first time ever, they could feel every pebble in the soil, and every blade of grass, as their tiny body crushed the flora beneath their weight.

There wasn't time for this -- the new senses, the new feelings. The child had escaped, and though they hadn't seen or heard their pursuer, they knew they were being hunted.  Everyone who escapes is hunted. Everyone who escapes is caught. Everyone who escapes is returned.

The child scanned the edge of the field -- the trailer homes were too far, and the field was too open; they would be seen and they would be caught.  Directly across from them, though, was safety: an old barn, sagging at one end, with a clear hole in the outer wall just large enough for something the child's size to fit through.

The child scrambled to their feet and tried to run toward the barn, struggling to coordinate their limbs in a way that would propel them forward, and they fell.  Again. And again. And again and again, over and over.

But the child was determined, and they were making progress.  It took a lifetime, but the child made it to the hole in the wall and crawled through, then crawled beneath an old trailer and huddled behind the flat rubber tire between themself and the hole.

The barn door facing the trailer homes was open, and the view was clear. There was a dirt road running from the trailer homes toward the barn, then past it, and though it was rutted it wasn't covered in weeds.  The grass of the field was tall enough to hide behind. Maybe. If the child ran, they could make it, and where there were trailers there were people. Or at least better hiding places.

Just one more breath, and the child would run.

That breath caught in their throat as a shadow crossed in front of the door -- tall, thin, with a broad-brimmed hat and a long coat.  The hunter.

The hunter was closer than the child expected. Too close, and blocking the path between the child and the trailer homes.

The child watched, their breath held tight in their chest, as the hunter entered the barn and began searching for the child, looking behind old barrels and crates, and under threadbare tarps.  The hunter didn't know where the child was!

The child waited only three heartbeats before moving, rushing back through the hole in the wall they had come in through, then away from the safety of the trailer homes.

Behind them, the hunter tried to squeeze through the hole.
