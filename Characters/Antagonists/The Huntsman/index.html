    The Huntsman

    Pursuing Jamie, who fled through The Hedge from Arcadia (Changeling: The Lost concepts).

    Kids' nickname, confirmed (2026-07-15, decided live, grill-canon session): The local creek kids call him "the Hollowman" — distinct from the unrelated, now-retired "the Hollow" place-name from the non-canon Mystery Crime Thriller alternate outline. See Ben for the scene where this comes up.

    Bound by a rule it didn't design, confirmed (2026-07-18, decided live, grill-canon session): The creek kids' tooth-nailing charm (see Ben) — a baby tooth nailed to the tree near the Hedge opening by someone other than the protected child, as a ward — isn't an Arcadia-designed binding. It's folk superstition the kids made up themselves. And yet the Huntsman is genuinely, "weirdly" bound by it anyway, the same way it was bound by the "Steven" name a scared kid made up in a dream. Belief creating real, binding rules on the Huntsman — not just on Caleb's existence — is a deliberate thematic echo, not a coincidence.

    Wants Moss to know, confirmed (2026-07-18, decided live, grill-canon session): There's no ambiguity or misdirection about whether the Huntsman took Ben — it wants Jamie/Moss to know it did, since the kidnapping only works as leverage if Moss is certain. The misdirection in the A/B plot only comes from what Ben's disappearance implies about Caleb (see below).

    Possible confirmation/mockery about Caleb, drafted (2026-07-18, decided live, grill-canon session): When Rudy and Jamie reach Ben in Arcadia, the Huntsman may confirm — possibly mockingly — that Caleb "wasn't his to take," or say something similarly suggestive of Caleb's belief-based nature without spelling the reveal out directly. Not scripted as final dialogue yet, but confirmed as the intended shape of the beat. Alternate drafted version (per "Making What Wasn't Scooby-Doo," full data export): rather than mockery, the Huntsman could react to a direct question about Caleb ("What did you do with him?") with blank, dismissive incomprehension — a head-tilt, as if asked about a total stranger — which would land as its own kind of confirmation that Caleb was never its concern at all. Both versions available; neither locked over the other.

    Terminology is local, confirmed (per "Making What Wasn't Scooby-Doo," full data export): Nobody in this setting uses a single agreed-upon name for it. The creek kids call it "the Hollowman." Goblin's dream-name "Steven" gets adopted by Jamie as a taunt. Jamie's own private term is just "the Hunter" — not "Huntsman," a word Jamie may or may not even know is the "correct" one. Rudy never hears or uses "fae" either — his hunter-network contacts, at least one degree removed from any actual firsthand fae knowledge, would say "fairy-folk," "the Fair Folk," or similar; "fae" itself reads to both Rudy and Jamie as a modern-pagan/head-shop word that doesn't carry investigative weight. Dave's explicit framing: Jamie actively dislikes the term "fae," not because it's wrong, but because it's imprecise — the same bad taste as someone insisting "a pentacle isn't a pentagram." Rudy's case notes should carry this as an aliases list (Hollowman; "the Hunter," Jamie's term; possibly a "Huntsman" per some traditions) rather than one settled name. See Jamie.

    How Rudy comes to try iron at all, confirmed (per "Making What Wasn't Scooby-Doo," full data export): Rudy doesn't own a cold iron weapon at the story's start and has no pre-existing history with the fae that would give him one "from his war days" (already retired as non-canon, see Rudy Williams). The idea comes from a hunter-network contact who tells him old iron is supposed to hurt "fairy-folk" — secondhand, untested, low on Rudy's own confidence scale (rumor, below trusted testimony, personal observation, or repeated confirmation — see Rudy Williams). That's the only reason he tries it on the Huntsman in the first place — not because he already suspected iron would work, but because he had a lead worth testing and nothing better. See Rudy Williams for the actual knife he builds and tests it with.

    Near miss, location and staging confirmed (2026-07-22, decided live): Happens on the Shepherd-Jackson Highway, on Moss's walk back from a drug run at an old motel in Red Bridge — a small, run-down pass-through town based on Olathe, CO, the kind of place nobody stops in on purpose. Reached earlier that night by hitching a ride from a trucker out of LeGrande Station, the anonymous departure point nobody remembers a face at. No ride waiting at 4am, so Moss just starts walking the highway back alone. It drags close to dawn — triggering the established dawn curse for real, on the page, for the first time: Moss knows exactly what dawn means for them, and this time might not make it off the road before the sky lightens. Satisfies the original constraint below (separate from the barn/tree line/field) without inventing a new location — this is Moss's own already-established nightlife (can't sleep, needs drugs, wouldn't loop Rudy into that specific errand) taking them somewhere real. Moss doesn't tell Rudy about this, confirmed same session: it happened on a drug run, and Moss knows Rudy disapproves and wants no part of that side of things — so this stays private, meaning Rudy doesn't get this as direct evidence the Huntsman is active again. Whatever makes him start believing has to come from elsewhere (see the animal-carnage scene below).

    The dealer, confirmed (2026-07-22, decided live): Moss's source in Red Bridge is Christie Reese, reused from Dave's own past Hunter: the Vigil tabletop game (the fence NPC from the published adventure The Harvesters, whom his players actually met). See her own page.

    Original constraint (per "Making What Wasn't Scooby-Doo," full data export — superseded above, kept for the reasoning): Dave's own call: the Near Miss should happen somewhere other than the barn, the tree line, or the field where Jamie meets Ben — deliberate narrative separation so the reader doesn't mentally file it as "the same scene" once Ben disappears and the tooth turns up. This doesn't reopen the already-confirmed sequencing (tree line → Near Miss → Ben missing, see Ben) — it only pins down that the Near Miss needs its own location.

    The coyote and the squirrels, confirmed (2026-07-22, decided live): While investigating the already-established "something in the woods" lead for Caleb's case (see Development Notes & Unresolved Questions — resolved as a real but mundane predator, not connected to Caleb), Rudy finds a dead coyote surrounded by several dead squirrels — a kill by attrition, not power, echoing the capybara-and-zebra approach sign above. This is Rudy's own version of the animal-aggression sign, discovered independently of Moss's Near Miss, in the same stretch of woods near the RV — his own reason to start believing something is genuinely wrong, without needing Moss to tell him anything.

    Not reintroduced later in the series, confirmed (2026-07-22, decided live, grill-canon session): A planning spreadsheet had floated the Huntsman "reintroduced" in a much later book (Book 7, "Abyss Reopens"). Dave's call: no — the Book 1 kill stays the end of this thread entirely, consistent with the hidden-heart mechanic already being skipped below.

    Hidden-heart/Keeper's-Title canon, confirmed skipped (2026-07-21, decided live): Real Changeling: The Lost canon says killing a Huntsman's body isn't permanent unless its hidden heart (held in the Bastion of a sleeping mortal) is also destroyed — otherwise the Keeper's Title just reforms it in a new Huntsman. Dave's call: ignore this mechanic entirely. The reader never learns CtL rules, so Rudy's kill stays permanent on the page with no in-world explanation needed, consistent with the existing "Hunter story, not a Changeling story" framing above.

    Huntsman-shaped absences, floated (per "Making What Wasn't Scooby-Doo," full data export): Rather than more direct Huntsman appearances, Dave and the chat discussed smaller, unconfirmed "signs" that build dread between the Prologue and Ben's disappearance without ever fully resolving into a sighting — a boot print where no one should have walked, a crow watching too long, unnatural silence in the woods, a single horn blast at dawn from miles off, an animal carcass positioned strangely, someone mentioning "a man in an old coat." Not locked as specific scenes — available atmosphere/clue material for the stretch before the Near Miss.

    Approach signs, confirmed (per "Moss and the Huntsman," full data export — Dave's own idea, distinct from the floated absences above): Recurring, Moss-tracked omens that build before it actually arrives — ecological wrongness with a mundane explanation everyone else reaches for, not obvious evil signs. Frogs and toads start turning up in numbers people notice and comment on, short of a plague. Animals get stupidly aggressive — Moss has seen a docile old carriage-horse get attacked by a squirrel downtown, and there was a zoo incident where a family of capybara killed a mother zebra but left her calf alone. And people stop having sex — subtle, only noticeable to someone who's spent time around people who are normally sexually open (a campground, a commune, a bar where people usually leave together — all gone quiet). See Jamie for how Moss keeps track of these.

    First confirmed kill — Rye, confirmed (2026-07-22, decided live): Before Ben, before the story even starts, the Huntsman has a real, successful kill: Rye, a fellow Changeling and Moss's road companion, killed protecting Moss during a train-yard encounter years before the book opens (see Rye for the full scene — the "railroad bull" that appeared that night was the Huntsman in its established man-in-a-hat disguise). Deliberately meant to give the Huntsman weight as a genuine, already-proven threat rather than a pure red herring that only ever fails or gets thwarted — it's killed before, and the reader should feel that going in.

    Hearing-radius mechanic, confirmed (per "Moss and the Huntsman," full data export — Dave's own idea): As the Huntsman closes in, the audible world shrinks around whoever it's hunting — not silence, but a kind of selective culling, the way a video game engine stops rendering sound past a shrinking radius. A train can still be seen — wheels moving, someone on the platform on their phone — but goes inaudible; ambient sound drops out the same way, farthest first; eventually the only things left audible are whatever's closest, ending with footsteps that don't belong to the person hearing them. Moss's own rule, per Dave: when you can still see something that should be making noise but can't hear it, that's the warning; when you start hearing footsteps of people nearby that shouldn't be that close, run. (Precise foot-by-foot distance tiers were floated in the chat as flavor, not locked as an exact, reliable mechanic.)

    Confirmed core defining traits (per "Clue Evaluation Assistance," full data export — Dave's own original stated description, missed in an earlier pass): "Two defining traits: always walks but can skip through space, ala Jason Voorhees; and, his shadow is larger than it should be and kindof resembles 'a bear with antlers.'" This is the foundational visual/locomotion signature underlying every later depiction below — the Gothic version's "shadow" glimpses and the antlered-shadow imagery in What Wasn't and What Was's red-herring photo-album scenes both trace back to this.

    Mechanical basis for a mortal defeating one (background, from a general rules-discussion chat, not a specific confirmed scene): Huntsmen are tied to their hunt via a token/compact granted by the Gentry — a mortal (or a Hunter: The Vigil-style hunter) could plausibly stop one by breaking/stealing that token, exploiting an oath the Huntsman relies on, offering a substitution/sacrifice the Huntsman accepts in place of its target, or exploiting a folkloric bane (iron, salt, true names) tied to its specific origin story. This is general Changeling: The Lost lore Dave asked about, likely the underlying rationale for how Rudy could plausibly have saved young Jamie from a Huntsman — not a description of how that specific rescue actually played out.

    Gothic depiction (current direction for What Wasn't and What Was): Never shown in full — only glimpsed as a hat, a shadow, a fingertip brushing the edge of a mirror. Dread over spectacle.

    Discarded pulp depiction: A more violent, visceral hunter — willing to mutilate a secondary character (e.g. ripping out their eyes) just to provoke a reaction from Rudy. Dropped when the book's tone shifted toward Gothic horror.

    Origin: Comes from the crook-path (part of The Hedge), a tangle of time-rotted hedge-thorns wrapped in glinting iron — jaw unhinged from years of quiet service.

    Confirmed nature (per the Detective Noir alternate outline): Not just a creature but a function — "a servant of doubt, a predator of belief." Shape-shifts between a man, a beast, Leanne's face, and Rudy's own face. This directly parallels the "villain is disbelief itself" theme already established for the Caleb plot — the Huntsman may be a literalization of that same force, rather than a separate threat. Vulnerable to iron and sage. Its taunt: "You don't believe in yourself. You never did. That's why they die."

    Note on conflict, resolved (2026-07-15, decided live, grill-canon session): The full-contact, shape-shifting depiction shown fully in the climactic fight came from the Detective Noir alternate outline, now confirmed non-canon (see Development Notes & Unresolved Questions). The Gothic "never shown in full" depiction above is the canonical one.

    Confirmed resolution (per the main outline): The kid character Goblin claims the Huntsman came to him in a dream and gave his name as "Steven" ("my name is Steven, and I eat liars" — "Goblin lies a lot," per the kid's own deadpan aside). Jamie carries the joke-name into the real confrontation: "Hi, Steven." ... "Kid back home said that's your name... It's dumb. I like it." ... "Bye, Steven." This also sets up Dave's confirmed intent for Jamie to permanently close the Hedge scar afterward, framed as "like Loial closing the Waygate" — concept confirmed, specific ritual mechanics not yet settled. See Jamie.

    Who actually lands the kill, resolved (2026-07-18, decided live, grill-canon session): The "Steven" naming and the cold iron dagger both stay, split between the two characters rather than one replacing the other — but not played as a joke landing alongside a killing blow. Jamie says the "Bye, Steven" line as the taunt; Rudy is the one who actually drives the cold iron dagger home. Dave was explicit about why it can't be Jamie's hand on the blade: Moss isn't a killer — they've never willingly killed anything (Dave's own framing: they'd go out of their way not to step on a spider) — and if that ever changes on the page, it can't be framed lightly; it has to land as genuinely heavy, not a punchline moment. Rudy, in sharp contrast, already is a killer, and carries real unresolved weight about it — see the new note on Rudy Williams. So this scene isn't "Jamie's kill, witnessed by Rudy" — it's Rudy doing the killing, with Jamie's taunt as the only piece of levity the scene gets, and even that shouldn't undercut how much this specific act costs Rudy internally.

    Location of the final kill, resolved (2026-07-16, decided live — from "Westernizing What Wasn't"): Arcadia, not the Hedge. Dave stated this directly while planning the scene ("Moss and Rudy enter Arcadia to save a child the Huntsman has kidnapped... that is probably where the Huntsman has to die") and confirmed it again later in the same session while discussing pacing ("The reader won't see Arcadia until Rudy and Moss return there to save Ben"). This retires the earlier "inside the Hedge" wording from "Clue Evaluation Assistance" as imprecise phrasing rather than a real competing version — Arcadia and the Hedge remain distinct locations in this cosmology (see The Hedge), and the deliberate choice to withhold Arcadia from the page until this scene is itself a confirmed craft decision (see What Wasn't and What Was's Reusable Craft Lines / Western Style Notes).

    Refined, same location note (2026-07-18, decided live, grill-canon session): Specifically the edge of Arcadia, not deep within it. Dave's own reasoning (explicitly speculative, since neither Changeling canon nor this vault has ever mapped Arcadia's geography): the Huntsman likely wouldn't set bait meant to lure Moss too close to Arcadian "civilization" — wherever exactly that is — so the kidnapped-Ben trap sits right at the boundary, not deep inside fae territory. Arcadia's interior geography remains deliberately unmapped beyond this.

    Timing, resolved (2026-07-16, decided live — from "Westernizing What Wasn't"): This confrontation — and the Huntsman's death — happens at the end of Act II, not simultaneously with the Act III Leanne/Caleb resolution. Dave's own reasoning: the Huntsman thread is a red herring investigation that has to be closed out before Rudy can pursue the real answer, and the two locations are logistically "dozens of miles apart," making a simultaneous climax impossible without forcing a coincidence. The Huntsman is a genuinely dangerous, worth-investigating threat — not a wasted detour — but its purpose is to be the false climax, resolved so Rudy can devote himself fully to the true mystery in Act III.

    The full arc, confirmed (2026-07-16, decided live — from "Westernizing What Wasn't"): Reframed in Changeling terms as not really a monster to kill but a function — a piece of Arcadia's machinery sent to correct an anomaly (Jamie's escape), not an individual with grudges of its own. The truck-ramming in the Prologue did not kill it — it got back up. Over the roughly eight years since, it's pursued Jamie directly, with Jamie glimpsing it "a dozen or so times." Eventually it changes tactics: rather than continuing to chase Jamie (who's gotten very good at evading it), it kidnaps Ben instead — exploiting the one thing Jamie won't just run from: someone else being threatened. This forces Rudy and Moss to go into Arcadia to get Ben back, which is also the first and only time Arcadia itself appears on the page (the Hedge is shown earlier; Arcadia is deliberately withheld until this point). The weapon is a cold iron dagger (Rudy has cold rounds for lycanthropes, per his established gear, but no cold iron ammunition) — cold iron is anathema to the fae in a way ordinary violence isn't, "the one thing that can make a creature of Arcadia vulnerable." Moss's exact contribution to the kill mechanically is still open, but narrowed (2026-07-18, decided live): Dave is thinking of it in explicit RPG-party terms — Moss as support/controller, Rudy as striker — and plans to check the actual Changeling: The Lost Contract/power list for something that fits that role cleanly, purely so the scene stays mechanically consistent behind the scenes even though the reader will never know Moss is a Changeling character in game terms. No specific power chosen yet. Who swings the blade is now settled — see the resolution above: Rudy delivers the kill, not Jamie. Dave was explicit that this stays a Hunter-genre kill by violence, not a purely metaphysical Changeling-style banishment, specifically because the reader hasn't been taught Changeling rules and wouldn't find a non-violent resolution satisfying in a book playing as a Hunter story.

    Genre-blend framing, confirmed (same session): Dave's own summary is the cleanest statement of what the Huntsman actually is on the page: "this isn't TECHNICALLY a Changeling story and the reader doesn't know the rules. It's a Hunter story and, in those, monsters are usually killed with violence." Mechanically it's Changeling — a function of Arcadia, stopped by cold iron and Moss's fae-knowledge — but the reader only needs the Hunter-story stakes: something escaped something terrible, it never stopped looking, and it's finally found a way to force a confrontation. The method (cold iron, Moss's help) is what keeps the kill thematically Changeling even though the genre it's dressed in is Hunter.

    Red herring mechanics, confirmed (same session): The misdirection isn't just "Rudy investigates the wrong monster" — it's that the Huntsman primes both Rudy and the reader to misread Ben's disappearance as more of the same pattern ("the Huntsman took another one"), when the Huntsman never touched Caleb at all. Caleb's fading is a wholly separate, non-predatory tragedy (see Caleb's doubt-vs-disbelief mechanic) — the reveal isn't just "wrong culprit," it's "wrong category of problem entirely."

    This also resolves the vault's previously-unnamed "second missing kid": The kid Rudy and Jamie rescue during the climax (see What Wasn't and What Was) — whose rescue is how they learn Caleb was never really there — is confirmed as Ben, and it's the Huntsman, not some other threat, that took him.

    Retired alternate resolutions (2026-07-15, decided live, grill-canon session): Dave confirmed the main Act 1.1–3.6 outline as the book's sole canonical plan (see Development Notes & Unresolved Questions) — the four alternate outlines were a drafting exercise, not competing endings. Three of them had independently landed on a "defanged/starved by belief, not killed" resolution instead: the Detective Noir alternate outline (power cut away via a town-wide belief-in-Caleb mechanic Rudy engineers by re-planting Caleb's flyers, "you can't destroy doubt"); the Character-Driven alternate outline (it "dissolves, not killed, but starved," its story rewritten when Rudy speaks loving, specific memory of Caleb into being rather than fighting); and the Mystery Crime Thriller alternate outline (a fifth physical description — "a towering, faceless entity with antlers like dead trees and a cloak made of rotting leaves and stolen names" — plus a stakes rule that killing it outright would let "the Hollow eat what it has," resolved instead by naming truth rather than fighting). All three are retired along with the outlines they came from; the kill-and-name-him-Steven resolution above is the only one that's canon. The three alternates agreeing with each other that belief/memory (not violence) neutralizes the Huntsman was a coincidence of drafting the same climax through different templates, not a signal about the real ending.

    • 2025 ("Moss and the Huntsman," full data export): Confirmed a distinct set of approach signs (frogs/toads, aggressive animals, people losing interest in sex) as ecological omens Moss tracks; confirmed the hearing-radius mechanic — the audible world shrinking around whoever it's hunting as it closes in.
    • 2026-07-22 (decided live, grill-canon session): Confirmed a real kill predating the book — Rye, killed protecting Moss in a train-yard encounter, with the "railroad bull" that appeared that night revealed as the Huntsman itself. See Rye.
    • 2026-07-22 (decided live, grill-canon session, later same day): Near Miss location and staging locked — the Shepherd-Jackson Highway, on Moss's walk back from a drug run, timed near dawn. Confirmed Moss doesn't tell Rudy, since it happened on a drug run. Also confirmed Rudy's own independent animal-aggression discovery (the coyote killed by attrition, surrounded by dead squirrels) while investigating the unrelated "something in the woods" lead — his own reason to start believing, separate from Moss's Near Miss.
    • 2026-07-22 (decided live, grill-canon session, later still): Corrected the drug-run destination — not Jensen (too big, Montrose-based), but a small run-down pass-through town. Confirmed Christie Reese as Moss's dealer there, reused from Dave's own past tabletop game.
    • 2026-07-22 (decided live, grill-canon session, later still): Town named — Red Bridge, per a Google Docs town-naming reference Dave rediscovered. Resolves the "drug-run town" item on Development Notes & Unresolved Questions.
    • 2025-06-05 (Pulpify your Story): Introduced pursuing Jamie; pulp depiction floated first, then dropped in favor of the Gothic hat/shadow/mirror depiction within the same session.
    • 2025-06-05 (Omniscient POV vs Alienation): Crook-path origin and jaw-unhinged physical detail established, offered as an example of naming Hedge concepts without exposition.
    • 2025-06-03 (Detective Noir Setup): Doubt/belief nature, shape-shifting forms, iron/sage vulnerability, and the "cut away its power" resolution established via the alternate noir outline — not reconciled with the "never shown in full" Gothic depiction.
    • 2025-06-03 (Character Driven Plot Outline): A second, independently-drafted alternate outline arrives at a similar belief-not-violence resolution, reinforcing that mechanic as a likely keeper.
    • 2025-06-03 (Mystery Plot Breakdown): A third alternate outline adds a fifth physical description (faceless, antlered, cloak of rotting leaves and stolen names) and the "kill it and the Hollow eats what it has" stakes rule; again resolved via truth/belief rather than violence.
    • 2025 ("Hunter Saves Changeling," full data export): General Changeling: The Lost / Hunter: The Vigil rules discussion on how a mortal could save a Changeling from a Huntsman — background context, not a specific scene.
    • 2026-07-15 (decided live, grill-canon session): Main outline confirmed as canon; the "Steven"/kill resolution is the real ending, and the three belief-not-violence alternate resolutions are retired along with the outlines they came from.
    • 2026-07-16 (decided live, from "Westernizing What Wasn't"): Full 8-year arc confirmed — survives the Prologue's truck-ramming, hunts Jamie a dozen-ish times, then shifts tactics and kidnaps Ben, forcing an Arcadia rescue where it's finally killed with a cold iron dagger. Location resolved as Arcadia (not the Hedge), and timing resolved as the end of Act II, functioning as the book's red herring/false climax before Act III's real mystery.
    • 2026-07-18 (decided live, grill-canon session): Who lands the kill resolved — Jamie says "Bye, Steven" as the taunt, but Rudy is the one who actually kills him with the cold iron dagger, since Moss isn't a killer and Rudy already is. See Jamie and Rudy Williams for the new characterization this surfaced. Location refined to the edge of Arcadia specifically, not its interior. Confirmed the Huntsman is bound by the kids' own tooth-nailing superstition despite not designing it, that it wants Moss to know it took Ben, and drafted a possible mocking confirmation about Caleb "not being his to take." Exact integration with the pre-existing 2.6 beat left deliberately unresolved for now — see What Wasn't and What Was. Moss's mechanical contribution narrowed to a support/controller role (Rudy as striker) pending Dave checking actual Changeling Contracts for a fitting power — invisible to the reader, but kept internally consistent.
