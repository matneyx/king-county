    Mickey Two Dice

    Type: Vampire (Malkavian Clan)

    Background: - Embraced around/shortly after WWI by Missy Chamberlain and has lived through multiple identities, accumulating a fractured perception of his past due to his memory lapses. - Diablerized both Harv and Missy, though he’s forgotten these acts, and now they linger in his mind as spirits with their own autonomy. - Mickey has a complex and colorful past, with multiple aliases and a recently issued Blood Hunt against him in Castle Dell, Georgia. He relocated to King County and currently operates out of Stanton City

    Human identity — Michael Turino, corrected timeline (2026-07-22, decided live, grill-canon session — per "Vampire Population Ratio" and "King County Chronicles ChatGPT consolidatation"): Born in Italy, family emigrated to Argentina. There, out of envy over a friend's ease and confidence with women he himself lacked despite his own wealth and accomplishments, Michael killed that friend — a household servant named Angel — and drank his blood hoping to gain his qualities via occult practice found in his own parents' literature. Nothing supernatural came of it; Michael was not yet a vampire. He went on to become a renowned alienist, contributing to the era's medical literature on memory and the concept of the soul, and was invited to Castle Dell, Georgia by a doctor opening a new psychiatric hospital who respected his work — arriving there around, or shortly after, WWI at the latest (fixing an earlier, now-retired "early-to-mid-1800s/precognitive insanity" version of this origin, which no longer fits the timeline once Missy's Victorian/Wild-West-era human background and Harv's turn-of-the-century one are accounted for). Missy sought him out and embraced him specifically for his knowledge of the mind — not realizing "there was evil inside of him." Castle Dell is therefore the shared origin point of both Mickey's own backstory and the earliest "Spanish Dolls" cases (see below) — likely no coincidence.

    The diablerie chain and the Blood Hunt (confirmed, same source): The same night Missy embraced him, Mickey diablerized her first childe, Harv — and immediately forgot having done it, the first instance of what became his signature clan-curse behavior (see below). He went on to diablerize Kayla next, then eventually his own sire. A Blood Hunt was called on him once his diablerie of Missy was discovered. His escape: he took an innocent bystander, broke their mind with Dementation, forced them to watch as he and Kayla fleshcrafted them into an exact physical copy of himself, then Embraced that copy — creating what Malkavians call a "Gnawed" (the same mechanism behind the Spanish Dolls — a mind shattered before Embrace, with no coherent self left for a soul to anchor to — just aimed at copying himself instead of Kayla) — and released it as a feral decoy. The Camarilla hunted and killed the decoy, ending the Blood Hunt. It was during this period that Mickey first became "Angel" as a disguise, gradually forgetting it was a mask and coming to believe he was a real, separate person (see Angel de la Vega).

    Explicitly rejected survival mechanisms (2026-07-22, per "King County Chronicles ChatGPT consolidatation" — logged so these don't get reintroduced later): Dave explicitly ruled out Mickey possessing Rudy's body Superior-Spider-Man-style ("I don't want to go the 'Superior Spider-Man' route"), and Mickey diablerizing himself to survive as a disembodied spirit ("There are only so many levels of bullshit a reader will accept"). Also ruled out "Rudy still alive at the end" framings outright. Whatever the final survival/death mechanics turn out to be, they shouldn't be either of these.

    Post-fire survival, still genuinely unresolved (same source): Dave considered Dementation 6 options (Shattered Mirror, Devil in the Mind, Lingering Malaise, Kiss of the Moon) as possible explanations for surviving what should be lethal aggravated damage, explicitly ruling out Dementation 8 ("Deny") as too big a leap. None of these were locked as the actual mechanism — this is still open.

    Clan curse and diablerie addiction, confirmed (same source): Eidetic memory paired with massive, unpredictable memory lapses — he can forget small things (how to drive) or enormous things (that "Michael" or "Angel" ever existed at all), and anyone who references what he's forgotten is, as far as he's concerned, "making shit up." He's a diablerie addict, dangerously close to losing his Humanity to the Beast — he's Embraced people purely to diablerize and drink their soul. His Book 2-era arc is about seeking immortality without becoming a monster: he wants power and hedonism without the cost of losing himself, and explicitly does not want redemption.

    Confirmed cosmology-specific mechanic (per "Civilian Rifle Equivalents," full data export): Uses Changeling-Dreaming-style terms for his own limits — extremely banal, with no awareness of the Hedge at all, but he does know the Astral plane exists. Mechanically he has almost enough Auspex to Astral project already, and is confirmed to gain that Discipline later in the series — using it specifically to harass and manipulate Phoebe, who is herself an unwitting master of Astral projection (see her own page). See King County Cosmology Framework for how the Astral plane maps onto this setting's Umbra/Abyss/Arcadia structure.

    Auspex level, grounded (2026-07-22, decided live): Confirmed Auspex 3 back when Mickey was actually played as a PC in a play-by-post game — Auspex 4 (full Astral projection) and even 5 are within reach for where he ends up by the time he dies. Potential scene, floated same session: Phoebe and Mickey actually meeting and interacting on the Astral plane itself, not just Mickey using it one-directionally against her — not locked as a specific scene yet, but a real candidate. See Phoebe.

    Confirmed clan mechanic — The Passion (Dementation 1): Mickey can dial a target's emotional state up or down — one direction flattens/numbs/depresses, the other produces mania and overwhelming intensity. He can control intensity and direction but not which emotion gets affected; the effect always manifests like a real psychiatric condition, not an obviously supernatural one. He never uses it on himself.

    Discipline levels, confirmed (2026-07-22, decided live): At least Dementation 3 (The Passion above is only Dementation 1). Vicissitude 3, capped — no higher, which is the actual limit behind his fleshcraft work (Angel's disguise, the Dolls) rather than an unlimited power. Auspex 3 currently, 4-5 plausible by the time he dies (see above). Obfuscate 5 — confirmed via a specific combo, Mask of a Thousand Faces plus Cloak the Gathering, used to make an entire room look like him (his established cowardice/preference for not being where the danger actually is, see the drafted final-fight material, now has a concrete mechanism). Dave's standing note, worth stating plainly since it applies to all of this: none of these numbers or Discipline names are ever meant to surface on the page — this is purely bookkeeping to keep Mickey's power level internally honest and consistent, not lore the reader is meant to learn.

    Angel de la Vega — Mickey's alternate identity (confirmed, major): Surfaces when Mickey's Memory Lapses derangement erases the very concept of "being Mickey." Angel is tied to a real person from Mickey's human life — a servant Michael Turino murdered out of jealousy in Argentina — and is everything Mickey isn't: kind, empathetic, well-liked by Missy, Harv, and Kayla. The awareness mostly runs one direction — Angel knows about Mickey; Mickey has no idea Angel exists — but this isn't an absolute rule; Dave's own hedge: "They don't share memories, except when they do." Kayla and Angel have an established, almost intimate ritual of re-crafting Angel's face together via fleshcraft. Confirmed mechanic for how an Angel episode ends (per "Vampire Population Ratio," full data export): the fleshcraft disguise heals away on its own during ordinary vampire daysleep/torpor, since it was self-inflicted — Mickey wakes up "Mickey all over again" without any deliberate reversal needed. See Angel de la Vega.

    Kayla (confirmed, major — see her own page): Not a childe, not prey, not an ally — Mickey thinks of her as "property he didn't choose but now possesses," with reluctant obligation rather than any real affection. The earlier vault framing of her as his "girlfriend" is the cover story, not the reality. The Dolls he has her victims made into are described as things he does to her stand-ins because he can't (yet) do them to her directly. Kayla ultimately conspires with Angel to destroy Mickey, accepting that this destroys Angel too; Mickey remembers the betrayal and kills her for it (whether he diablerizes her is undecided by Dave).

    Appearance: Tall and lanky — 6'2" but only about 150 lbs, giving him an almost gaunt frame (not skeletal). Dark, deeply sunken eyes that look perpetually tired. Sharp, pronounced bone structure and thin lips, softened only slightly by a bit of fullness in the face. Permanent 5-o'clock shadow, often two days' stubble, with salt-and-pepper creeping in at the temples. Dark hair, cut short and slicked back lazily. Pronounced worry lines on the forehead and from nose to mouth. Favors cheap, wrinkled suits with no tie; chain-smokes, cigarette usually held in his long, thin fingers. Late 40s to early 50s in appearance. Visual references cited: a mix of Steve Buscemi, Benicio Del Toro, Christopher Walken, and Quentin Tarantino.

    Before the Embrace: Mickey was the kind of person who'd obsess over something and lose all track of days — specifically described as diving into the newest medical journals, hinting he may have had some background in medicine or science before being Embraced (consistent with the "forgot he was a doctor" example used to illustrate his memory-lapse derangement). Confirmed directly in The Prodigal Son: he was a neurologist. Also Latin-literate (a skill Missy needles him about, given his memory lapses — "The only words I'd expect you to recognize would be 'brain' and 'hysteria'").

    Current project (Book 2, in progress): Working out of an abandoned shipping warehouse, Mickey and Harv have built an unsettling construct — three stainless-steel autopsy tables suspended at angles by scaffolding, drain spouts connected via copper piping to a plastic-lined claw-foot bathtub. Still leak-prone; Harv isn't impressed with Mickey's soldering. Mickey spends his time in a small claimed office poring over old, handwritten Latin manuscripts and diagrams, looking for something specific. Almost certainly connected to the Western Slope Nursing Care body-snatching in the Prologue — Allen and Frank, two elderly patients, are set up for comparison by Mickey for an unstated purpose. Not yet explained on the page.

    Likely goal — Golconda, via a blood ritual (see The Three Generations Ritual): A separate brainstorm establishes Mickey seeks Golconda, or at minimum immortality without risk of losing himself to his Beast, and that he's pursuing a specific ritual requiring three firstborns across three generations of one bloodline, drained and combined to grant three times their remaining lifespan. The three-autopsy-table/one-bathtub warehouse construct matches this ritual's mechanics almost exactly — very likely the same thing, though not explicitly confirmed as such on the page yet. Mickey learned of the ritual (and a prior failed attempt at it, 20 years earlier, stopped by Rudy) through means still undecided by Dave. A survivor of that earlier attempt holds a vendetta against Mickey.

    "Blood-mage" vs. Malkavian, resolved (2026-07-16, decided live, grill-canon session): He's a Malkavian, full stop — not a blood-mage in any formal sense. But "straightforward" is the wrong word for him regardless: he'll dabble in anything that might grant him true immortality, or even just get him closer to it than he currently is. In The Prodigal Son's warehouse ritual specifically, he's playing with magicks he doesn't actually understand, working entirely from what's written in the old Latin manuscripts he's collected — competent enough to attempt it, not a trained practitioner of it.

    Afraid of fire: Explicitly stated (Mickey jokes it's "a miracle" he managed to solder the warehouse plumbing at all, "given how [he] feels about fire"). Not elaborated on further — could be an ordinary vampire vulnerability, or something more specific to him.

    "The kid," identified (2026-07-16, decided live, grill-canon session): Mickey references an unnamed young assistant twice — once in the Prologue, explaining he's doing the nursing-home job himself rather than sending "the kid" because "he can't get in here... and he's got another job," and again in Chapter 3, waiting on the kid to "get back" before starting the next phase of the warehouse project. This is the now-grown survivor of The Three Generations Ritual's ~20-years-ago case — the son whose pregnant girlfriend and unborn child died in the failed attempt. Mickey is manipulating his grief, using it as fuel both to help recreate the ritual and to go after Rudy. The two have different motives working the same scheme: the kid genuinely wants revenge on Rudy for failing to save his girlfriend and child; Mickey personally doesn't care about Rudy at all at this point — the kid is simply useful. Still unnamed on the page.

    Personality:

    • Exudes charm, but his perception of reality is deeply skewed by his memory issues, which makes him eccentric even by Malkavian standards.
    • Convinced of his own mental stability and reliability, he interacts with others confidently, unaware of his many missing memories or the distortions this creates in his understanding.

    Derangement: Memory Lapses and “Infallible” Memory¶

    • Memory Lapses: Mickey’s Malkavian derangement causes episodic memory lapses, erasing specific knowledge, skills, or entire spans of history from his mind. These gaps can last decades or longer. Notably:
      • Era Amnesia: During the 1960s or 70s, Mickey forgot all prior decades, genuinely believing that history only began then, a belief that continued until the early 2000s. During that period, he adapted to modern settings with ease but had no concept of events, people, or inventions from before the 60s.
      • Disconnection from Past Identities: Any skills, professions, or knowledge Mickey once held can disappear without a trace. When these lapses happen, his eidetic memory reorganizes itself to create an internally consistent reality in which those skills or events have simply never existed. For instance, if he forgets he was ever a doctor, the concept of “doctor” becomes entirely foreign to him.
    • “Infallible” Memory Belief: Mickey is entirely unaware that he has memory issues, believing his memory is flawless due to his eidetic recall. He can’t conceive of himself as someone who forgets, even in the face of contradictory evidence. If someone tries to confront him about a memory gap, he dismisses it with certainty, rationalizing that they must be mistaken or simply lying.
      • This belief results in a deep-seated irony: Mickey remembers everything he’s allowed to remember in painstaking detail, yet he’s unaware of vast swathes of lost information. His confidence in his own memory adds to his eccentricity, as he often dismisses or belittles others for being “less observant” or “more forgetful.”

    Relationships with Harv and Missy¶

    • Blinded by Memory Gaps: His inability to recall significant moments with Harv and Missy, such as their diableries, creates an ironic twist. He views them as present, active figures in his life, unaware that they exist as fragments of his past. To Harv and Missy, his inability to remember his violence toward them is darkly amusing, as it means Mickey treats them as family or allies without acknowledging his own betrayal.

    • Correction (per Dave directly, 2026-07-14; refined 2026-07-16, decided live, grill-canon session): Harv and Missy are disembodied spirits with full free will and autonomy, unable to physically interact with the real world in any meaningful way — which is why Harv never lifts a finger to help with physical tasks (he can't, not that he won't; see his page). They can perceive each other, as can Mickey, and Kayla shared this same state during the window between her diablerization and her fleshcrafted body — but as far as anyone knows, no one outside that small circle has ever perceived Harv or Missy. Mickey has no idea they aren't "real" in the ordinary sense. Rather than a strategic "unstated agreement" to preserve his delusions for their own advantage, Harv and Missy genuinely tried, at some point, to convince him of the truth — and gave up. Mickey remains unknowingly reliant on them for context he can no longer retain, but the dynamic is one of resignation on their end, not manipulation. They aren't leashed to his physical proximity, either — they can wander off freely; the only real tether is existential (if Mickey dies, they may cease to exist permanently along with him), not physical distance.

    • The soul-anchoring rule (confirmed, per "Vampire Population Ratio," full data export): A diablerie victim's soul only survives as one of Mickey's "passengers" if the victim had a coherent, strong sense of identity to anchor to at the moment of death. This is why Missy, Harv, and Kayla all persist in his head, while the many victims of his "Dolls" scheme (deliberately psychologically shattered before being embraced and diablerized — "Gnawed," with no coherent self left) dissolve completely instead. Mickey diablerized Kayla too, and her soul got stuck the same way — but unlike with Harv and Missy, Mickey actually remembers diablerizing her.

    Roleplaying Themes and Dynamics¶

    • Anachronism and Confusion: Mickey’s incomplete memory often makes him anachronistic, with missing knowledge leading to misinterpretations of the modern world. For example, if he forgets an essential skill or cultural concept, he might struggle to navigate situations others find simple.

    • Persistent Denial of Forgetfulness: Mickey’s denial that he could ever forget anything adds a layer of tension and tragic irony to his interactions. This belief makes him unknowingly vulnerable, as his confidence in his memory prevents him from preparing for or acknowledging his own lapses.

    • Evolving Relationships with Harv and Missy: The shifting memories complicate Mickey’s view of his “companions.” Harv and Missy, who remember everything, have developed a wary respect for his distorted worldview — finding dark humor in his obliviousness more than exploiting it, per Dave's correction above.

    Blood Magic¶

    • Mickey's blood magic draws on unusual ingredients: animal tongues, mirror shards, gasoline-soaked prayer books.

    Current Activity: Mickey keeps a low profile but is one of the few vampires active in the area. He may be on the run from hunters or other vampires seeking to fulfill the Blood Hunt order.

    Confirmed relationship with Rudy (per "Vampire Population Ratio," full data export): Deliberately not designed as mirror-image foils — that role belongs to Mickey and Angel. Rudy fights Mickey only because he's a threat to King County broadly; it becomes personal only once Mickey threatens Rudy's found family. Mickey doesn't even notice Rudy exists until Rudy foils one of his immortality schemes.

    Confirmed series endpoint (Dave: "I already have that written down"): The Abyss-touched vampire and Promethean from The California Incident eventually return to the world (exact circumstances undecided). The Promethean joins Rudy's circle. Mickey eventually diablerizes the returned vampire, inheriting some of its age, power, and Abyssal taint — becoming the single most powerful vampire Rudy will ever face. Mickey kills Rudy. This is confirmed as the series' long-term destination, not just a brainstormed option. Mickey himself does not survive the same battle (see the finale below) — Angel ends him shortly after, so Mickey "wins" against Rudy but doesn't outlive that win by long.

    The finale, corrected and drafted in full (2026-07-22, decided live, grill-canon session — the version below supersedes an earlier same-day pass that omitted Angel entirely; see Book Order and Series Structure for the full breakdown): Confirmed Dave directly: Angel is real, still canon, and the elaborate ensemble version below — not the earlier stripped-down "Mickey alone, tortures Rudy" draft — is the one to build from.

    Mickey arrives with shadow minions and an Abyss-corrupted "shadow-self," inherited from diablerizing the Abyss-touched vampire — separate from his own will and separate from Angel. Rudy's already thwarted him at least twice by this point. Phoebe is the bait, offered deliberately: "Jesus Christ, Rudy, don't be chicken shit" (a direct Monster Squad callback) is her own line pushing Rudy past his reluctance to use her that way. She tells Mickey about the "surrender" herself, via astral projection — not leaked by Angel. Mickey knows it's a trap and comes anyway; his own planning around it fails once the actual fight starts going off the rails.

    The fight, full team, all at once (not sequential "one hero at a time"): Rudy, Amber, Doc (a werewolf and field medic), a Promethean, Phoebe, and Moss all commit simultaneously. The Promethean is unmade/torn apart early — whether that kills him isn't the point, just that it removes him from the fight. Shadow minions swarm and tie up the werewolf. Phoebe pivots to occupy Mickey's shadow-self specifically (it negates/denies her magic rather than just resisting it, so she has to distract it, not beat it), leaving Mickey himself for Rudy. Amber runs out of ammunition and steps up with a silver dagger dipped in Rudy's own blood — it isn't enough; Mickey overpowers her. Rudy calls Doc off the fight to treat her. Rudy then faces Mickey alone — fights well, stubborn enough to shrug off Mickey's Dominate, but Mickey is still superhuman. Phoebe burns herself out fighting the shadow-self, which returns to Mickey's side. Rudy is killed — not tortured at length in this version, but dying to buy the others the room to finish this. Amber and Doc both rush Mickey again anyway and are both battered aside. Moss makes the call and pulls Phoebe into the Hedge to save her.

    The sunrise trick fails, confirmed: The plan counted on daylight to finish Mickey, but an old barn casts shade exactly where he's standing — the sun comes up, but not on him. Amber, still standing: "She's gone. You can't have her... That was our only objective." Mickey, having just lost both his win condition (Phoebe) and Kayla (killed earlier for the betrayal with Angel), loses his composure completely — and that grief is what finally brings Angel forward at full strength. Angel steps into the sun himself, ending Mickey along with himself. Neither Rudy nor any of Rudy's allies land the actual killing blow on Mickey — Angel does, at the cost of his own existence.

    Connection to The Prodigal Son's present-day case, confirmed (2026-07-16, decided live, grill-canon session): The same chats laid out a detailed kidnapping case — a nursing-home body-swap discovered via a missing shrapnel scar/tattoo, a kidnapped grandson with a severed finger sent as proof, and a kidnapper who turns out to be "the kid" — Executive Dawes's son, the surviving son from The Three Generations Ritual, manipulated by Mickey into the copycat kidnapping, targeting Rudy specifically (the vendetta-target inconsistency previously flagged on that page is resolved: it's Rudy, not Mickey). This is confirmed as the same underlying case as the established Dawes/Speno kidnapping MO and the Western Slope Nursing Care body-swap (Allen and Frank; see below for the swap's now-explained purpose) — Speno's family is a new, unrelated bloodline Mickey chose for the recreated ritual, not a reopening of Dawes's own case. This case's climax is also Angel's first on-page appearance, with Missy pointing Rudy out to Angel as "the man that stopped it" (deliberately left unexplained on the page, per Dave).

    Confirmed staging of that Passion manipulation, per its final revised version (per "Bridging Story Gaps," full data export — Dave explicitly rejected an earlier draft of this scene as "a cliche cop out," and this is the version he prefers instead, 2026-07-16 decided live): The manipulated kidnapper ("the kid") holds a gun on Rudy; Rudy is only trying to disarm him non-lethally; Mickey dials Rudy's emotions down via The Passion — not to make him a more efficient killer, but to make him stop caring whether he survives. Rudy walks toward the gun without flinching, takes a hit, and grapples the kidnapper down; the gun discharges in the struggle, killing him — not a deliberate execution. Mickey, furious, uncloaks for the first time on the page, raging that his "blood key" is ruined; Rudy, still numb, tells him something to the effect that he was never the point. Sunrise forces Mickey to flee before Rudy can react. Only afterward — triggered by a human sound (a child crying, or the rescued captive sobbing) — does the numbness break, and Rudy is left nauseated and shaken by what he's capable of when he stops fearing his own death, not proud of it. This is a distinct, separate event from the vault's "Rudy once killed a human, not a monster" open thread (see Rudy Williams) — that connection was explored and explicitly retired. Rudy's confirmed epilogue line to the grandfather, deliberately hiding the full truth: "He got mixed up in something and I couldn't save him." This revised staging supersedes an earlier same-day pass that still had the deliberate-execution/forced-trigger-finger version Dave explicitly rejected.

    The nursing-home body-swap's purpose, resolved (2026-07-16, decided live, grill-canon session): Mickey abducts a real, unnamed comatose patient from elsewhere and brings him in as "Allen," fleshcrafting him in real time into a copy of the actual target, "Frank" — so staff won't notice Frank is gone. The Prologue's nurse mistakenly addressing the sitting figure (Allen) as "Frank" isn't a manuscript slip — it's the disguise visibly working, caught mid-process. See The Prodigal Son (A King County Chronicle) and Development Notes & Unresolved Questions.

    Frank confirmed as Jeff Speno's own father (2026-07-22, per "King County Chronicles 1 prewriting"): The three-generations structure for the present-day case — grandfather, father, child — is Frank, Jeff Speno, and Speno's kidnapped 12-year-old son. Speno confirms it's his own father when he sees him at the nursing home. This was already implied by Mickey's own page calling the kidnapped child "a grandson" above, just not previously spelled out.

    Fleshcrafting sensory detail, drafted (same source): Mickey licks his thumb — it comes away bloody, not covered in saliva — and rubs it into Allen's forehead; the skin goes pliable, "a similar consistency to silly putty," as the disguise takes hold. The nurse who walks in mid-process barely gets a glance from Mickey before her eyes "sag like she couldn't care any less" — an effortless, near-total compulsion, not a struggle.
