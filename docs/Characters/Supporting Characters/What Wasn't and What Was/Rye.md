**How Rye found Moss, confirmed (per "Moss and the Huntsman," full data export):** Rye caught Moss "staring" — Moss's own term for shutting down and going unblinking-still for hours — more than a few times, on top of a handful of other tells, and put it together before Moss ever said a word about it. **"Time went backwards," confirmed as Rye's own phrase, not literal (per "Moss and the Huntsman," full data export):** Rye's own words for what happened to him. Not actual reversed causality — Arcadia's time and the mortal world's were never coupled to begin with. He lived out a childhood-to-adulthood's worth of years; his family lost a month or two. "Backwards" is just the only word he had for something that inverted what should've been true: he should be the one who lost the least time, and instead he's the one who lost everything.

**Draft dialogue, not locked as final prose (per "Moss and the Huntsman," full data export):** "You know you don't have to do that, right?" ... "The staring." A pause. "I'm not staring." "Yeah, you are." "No. I'm listening." That's the exchange where Rye realizes what Moss actually is.

**The dawn rule and what happened:** Moss and Rye caught out together in Nebraska, camped under a half-collapsed boxcar with a can of Sterno and a jar of peaches. Moss refused to hop a train at dawn ("We wait. Dawn's bad luck" / "It's when I came back"). **Revised, not locked as final prose (per "Moss and the Huntsman," full data export):** Rye doesn't mock the rule as superstition — he's lived his own version of Arcadia's cruelty and knows better than to doubt it outright. What he doubts is that it applies to him: "That's your ghost, Cavekid. Not mine." He hops the grainer anyway, reckless more than genuinely convinced, leaning on the invincibility of someone who already survived the worst thing he could imagine. A railroad bull appeared without warning; in the chaos (a shout, a flashlight, a bang that hit metal not flesh) Rye slipped and his leg went under the wheel. His last words to Moss: "You were right. Cavekid... you were right." He didn't die immediately, but Moss fled before the ambulance arrived.

**Aftermath:** His hoodie was soaked in blood in the immediate aftermath. Moss never saw Rye again — fate unknown; "maybe he's alive, limping through Denver on a peg leg. Maybe not." This is the origin of [[Jamie|Moss]]'s dawn curse. Moss won't speak Rye's name aloud, and leaves orange scraps (torn hoodie bits, burnt caution tape) tied to fenceposts near trainyards — a warning for others, a shrine. At dawn, Moss sometimes hears his voice: "Just ride, Cavekid. Just go." Thematic closing line: "Dawn still means blood on the rails."
