No known last name. Physically appears around 16; chronologically unknowable, possibly centuries old. Non-binary, they/them. Ethnically ambiguous, often described as androgynous.

**Assigned sex at birth, confirmed intentionally unknown (per "Moss and the Huntsman," full data export):** Not just unaddressed in-story — Dave has deliberately never decided it, even for himself. It isn't a mystery to be solved later.

**Aliases:** "Jamie the Lost" (informal), "that street kid" (how locals refer to them), "Witness" (what Huntsmen call them), "Moss" and "Cavekid" (road names, see below).

**Appearance:** Tall, slender, graceful, with an uncanny presence — "elvish in form and manner, like an eerie fairytale illustration." Long, straight dark hair; big, expressive dark eyes, always a mix of curious and haunted. Pale skin with faint scars, especially on the forearms. Usually dressed in layers of secondhand goth-punk clothing: a long coat, patched-up boots. Voice is smooth and low, ambiguously between soft-masculine and husky-feminine; speaks deliberately and often cryptically, but rarely unkindly.

**Personality:** Old-soul wisdom in a childlike shell. Grounded but otherworldly — doesn't always react to things "normally." Resilient, skeptical of institutions, fiercely loyal to those who show them kindness. Deeply traumatized but rarely speaks directly about Arcadia. Dry, poetic sense of humor.

Taken into [[Arcadia]] early enough in life that they don't remember life before becoming a Changeling — and has been there for centuries, making them far older than they appear. Their Keeper (unnamed, undeveloped — see [[Keeper]]) held a domain that included twisted reflections of modern towns. Fled Arcadia through [[The Hedge]] and is being pursued by [[The Huntsman]] (Changeling: The Lost concepts). Brought something back from the Hedge with them — a voice, a look in their eyes that isn't always theirs.

**Speculative Changeling mechanics (implied, not confirmed):** Mask (what mortals see) is their human form; Mien (true appearance) might be even more alien and striking. Likely a strong Wyrd rating — the Hedge still clings to them. Likely Seeming: Darkling or Fairest (shadowy, elegant, elusive). Possible Kith: Mirrorskin, Reflections, or Palewraith (fitting their obsession with memory and loss).

**Mien, confirmed physical description (2026-07-16, decided live — from Chapter 1 of the manuscript):** Nearly eight feet tall, still bipedal and humanoid, but with comically short legs and arms long enough to touch the ground — impossibly lanky, double-jointed. This is why Jamie's first minutes in a human-shaped body (the Prologue's exit from the Hedge) read as newborn-doe clumsy, falling over and over just to run: it isn't about age, it's that Jamie has never operated a body with these proportions before and has to relearn locomotion from scratch.

**Self-harm and drug use:** Cuts themselves occasionally to feel real, to test the boundaries of Mask and Mien. Can't sleep naturally; relies on drugs to shut their body down, which provide no real rest or dreams — the drugs also suppress memories of Arcadia. [[Rudy Williams|Rudy]] knows about the drug use and hates it, but understands.

**Confirmed detail on the cutting (per "Astrology Birthday Guess," full data export):** It's explicitly grounding, not escape — the pain and the blood feel and look different on this side of the Hedge (heavy, red, ordinary), and that difference is the point: proof the body is real and theirs. Precise, not reckless — Jamie knows how to avoid major veins. Dozens of scars, old and fresh, on arms, thighs, and calves (kept hidden by sleeves/socks, not out of shame, just not displayed). Rudy has always known and never made it a confrontation — he keeps the bathroom stocked with rubbing alcohol, bandages, and a single sealed blade at a time, and has said, once: *"If you're gonna do it, do it clean. I'm not gonna stop you. But I'm not gonna let you rot, either."* He's also said he'd worry *more* if Jamie stopped cold, since that would read as giving up rather than healing — his real ask is just that it keeps meaning something: *"Just... don't go silent on me. If it stops meaning something, I wanna know."* Confirmed line about medical escalation: Rudy has field-medic experience and keeps a trauma kit, and knows a retired vet, an off-the-books nurse, and a former surgeon who'll do house calls before ever risking a hospital's questions about an unregistered teen's fresh cuts — but he'll take Jamie to the ER without hesitation if it's genuinely life-threatening.

**Confirmed physiology quirk:** Jamie can't get sick — no colds, flu, or ordinary human ailments — but is still a carrier: they can pass on an illness they're personally immune to (explicitly including something as serious as HIV via shared needles), without ever testing positive themselves. They're aware of this and deliberately avoid sharing needles or getting too physically close for exactly that reason. Can eat literally anything and draw sustenance from it, but has a strong preference for sugar/candy. Also prone to non-medical, ambient "supernatural" afflictions tied to place and proximity — a restlessness/cabin-fever "Still Sickness" from staying too long somewhere emotionally heavy, and a "Grief Fever" (chills, light sensitivity, going quiet for days) triggered by proximity to death or emotional collapse — explicitly NOT visibly supernatural (no weeping blood, no glowing eyes); the point is that Jamie's wrongness should always read as dismissible, never as obvious proof of the supernatural.

**Confirmed care-package scene (unconfirmed as final prose, but the beat itself is confirmed):** Rudy drops off supplies without asking questions or waiting for a reaction — food, tampons *and* pads (covering bases without assuming which Jamie needs), condoms, cheap razors, ibuprofen. Line: *"Razors are shit. Don't use 'em on your legs, you'll bleed out and make me mop."* Followed by: *"Give away what you don't need. I'd rather they get used than forgotten under a sink somewhere."* And: *"If you need help with any of that, let me know. Need more? Just leave the empty box somewhere I'll find it."* No follow-up, no gratitude demanded — confirmed by Dave as a small moment meant to never necessarily come up again, just something that lodges in Jamie without being explained.

**Confirmed real-world touchstone — Sox Place:** A real Denver youth drop-in (est. 2002, run by Doyle Robinson, slogan "Bring the Father's love to the fatherless," explicitly bans volunteers from proselytizing) that Jamie used as a kid — hot meals, clean socks, no questions, no sermons. Aged out of eligibility once a volunteer asked how old they were and Jamie just shrugged; they stopped letting Jamie in afterward, though they'd still hand over socks "if I asked nice." Confirmed as deliberately included even knowing the real place might close before the book is finished or read — the point is that it *was* real and *mattered*, not that it currently exists. Confirmed line: *"They didn't kick me out. They just stopped letting me back in... That was the first time I realized I was aging out of something I wasn't even counting. To suddenly be too much of something I wasn't even keeping track of."* Explicitly framed by Dave as not about the Hedge at all — just the ordinary experience of an unplaceable kid slipping through every system built to "help." Dave's own gloss: "If anything, Jamie's one of the lucky ones."

**Confirmed 8-years-since-the-Hedge life sketch (per the same chat, loose, not scene-by-scene canon):** Roughly age 12 when they came out. Early years barely verbal, flinching at metal doors and the smell of sage/bleach. Learned trains felt right ("going away"), started riding the rails within a couple years, eventually running with a loose train-hopping/crust-punk crew (names floated: Cinder, Bark, Stix, Ghost Dog — not confirmed as actually appearing on-page). Picked up field medicine, bike repair, and busking-adjacent skills on the road. Shepherd's Inn is the one fixed point they always circle back to, even though they never fully unpack there — by years 7–8, the stretches away have been getting shorter.

**Smoking, confirmed as distinct from the drug use:** Jamie loves candy and uses drugs specifically to dissociate/force sleep — smoking doesn't serve either function, and it's unclear whether Jamie can even experience addiction, so the habit is likely a deliberate choice rather than dependency. **Confirmed motive: mimicking Rudy.** Jamie picked it up from him and mirrors his exact mannerisms — cigarette hanging from the lips more than held, talking around it, first and last drag exhaled through the nose, forgetting it's there for full minutes at a stretch. Jenn notices and names it directly: *"Even a blind man could see you learned to smoke by watching him, but it's more than that... You didn't just learn, you assimilated it and mirror it. I guess there are worse habits you could have picked up from him."* Other candidate readings floated alongside the mimicry one (not mutually exclusive): smoking as a small, boringly mundane ritual that anchors Jamie in the real world ("Monsters don't smoke menthols. People do."); as a way to mark time when day/night otherwise feels unreliable; as reclaimed autonomy after Arcadia took every choice from them ("maybe I like the idea of poisoning myself on my own goddamn terms"); as a way to keep people at a slight distance.

**Draft scene — the porch outside Shepherd's Inn, late night (not confirmed as final prose):** Rudy and Jamie smoke in wordless unison — same brand, same lighter, every drag and exhale mirrored without either commenting on it — ending with both taking one final, long drag and flicking their butts into the dark at the same moment, "like they'd practiced it."

**Prologue scene (opens [[What Wasn't and What Was]]):** Jamie exits the Hedge for the first time in their known life — barefoot, trembling, wearing rags that served function over anything else. Crosses a field, sees both a barn and the lights of a trailer park, ducks into the barn, and eventually climbs into an idling Ford F-150 (one door open) without hesitation.

**Captivity mechanism (2026-07-15, decided live, grill-canon session — fleshes out the existing "taken early enough in life... has been there for centuries" detail above):** Jamie was almost certainly stolen as an infant or toddler — they remember nothing of life before Arcadia. Held for what was subjectively hundreds of years, their captor aged them only as far as was needed to be physically useful for whatever tasks were demanded of them — which is why they emerged looking like an adolescent despite the centuries. Not kept in total isolation: other Changelings passed through over the years, and it was their outside perspective — naming what was happening to Jamie as abuse, if not outright torture — that first put the idea of escape in Jamie's head. Finding the actual opening in the Hedge took decades more; committing to it, once found, took additional time to work up the nerve. **Escaped alone** — no group, no other Changelings dying alongside them; an earlier "small group of fellow Changelings, the only survivor" detail is retired.

**The exit itself (2026-07-15, decided live, grill-canon session):** Stepping out of the Hedge was a full-body shock — the real world felt somehow duller and more muted while simultaneously realer and brighter than anything Arcadia had shown them. Jamie tripped on the way out and felt dirt, rock, and grass against their hands and knees for the first time as anything but an abstraction — pain and sensation they'd thought they already understood, but hadn't, not really.

**Staging and the Huntsman (2026-07-15, decided live, grill-canon session — supersedes the earlier drafted version below):** From the exit point, Jamie could see both the barn and the trailer park. They went to the barn first, already aware the Huntsman was hunting them, and were working up the nerve to make a break for the trailer park — more walls, more places to hide — when the Huntsman found them and blocked that path outright. Jamie ran the opposite direction instead.

**Site geography (2026-07-16, decided live):** The barn, the trailer park, and the Hedge opening form roughly an isosceles triangle — about 200 yards between the barn and the trailer park, and about 100 yards between the barn and the Hedge opening.

**Rudy's arrival (2026-07-15, decided live, grill-canon session):** Rudy was driving away from the trailer park at sunup, having quietly left [[Tina]]'s place while she still slept, and drove right past Jamie running from the barn without immediately clocking that they were in danger. It was catching the Huntsman's "man in a hat" form in his rearview mirror that made him do a hard U-turn, lean across the bench seat, throw the passenger door open, and shout "Get in!" The dust his U-turn kicked up, combined with the sunrise backlighting the Huntsman from behind, is what let Rudy see past the human disguise to the thing's true shadow — the bear-like, antlered shape already established as the Huntsman's core visual signature (see [[The Huntsman]]).

**Confirmed resolution (2026-07-15, decided live, grill-canon session):** Rudy rams the Huntsman with the truck. A gun was explicitly ruled out — Rudy would have been firing in the direction of the trailer park, risking hitting a resident, so ramming (or just fleeing) were his only real options once Jamie was in the truck. This locks in the previously-drafted action beat below as the actual confirmed scene, not just a candidate.

**Confirmed beat-by-beat action (per "Clue Evaluation Assistance," full data export, now locked in above):** Jamie clocks the trailer park lights but the Huntsman is between them and it, forcing a run instead. Rudy sees Jamie running and then sees the Huntsman's shadow in his own headlights. He throws the passenger door open — "Get in!" — tries to reverse away, finds no traction, then throws the truck into gear and rams the Huntsman outright. He feels two distinct bumps under the tires and doesn't see the Huntsman in the rearview afterward.

**Rudy's rescue and aftermath (confirmed):** Rudy saved young Jamie from the Huntsman that dawn, whatever the exact mechanism turns out to be — cementing Jamie's loyalty to him. Repeated attempts to place Jamie in foster care failed; they kept running back to Rudy. Eventually Jamie disappeared into the wider world — hopping trains, living off-grid — but returns occasionally to Shepherd Junction and Rudy.

**Name origin, confirmed (per "Moss and the Huntsman," full data export — Dave's own idea):** In the immediate aftermath of the rescue, Rudy asks the kid their name. Jamie isn't in a place where the question means anything yet — centuries in Arcadia, the Keeper, the Huntsman, then suddenly a stranger's truck — and doesn't answer with a name. They answer with the only fact that matters: *"…saved me…"* Rudy only catches a fragment. He hears "Jamie." The kid doesn't correct him, and he takes the silence as confirmation. Jamie is named within about a week of the rescue, before entering the foster system at all — "Moss" comes much later, on the road (see below). Jamie may never know whether "Jamie" was ever meant as a name; what it actually was, was the first word of freedom.

**Draft scene, not locked as final prose (per "Moss and the Huntsman," full data export):**
> Rudy pulls the truck off the road. The kid is shaking, bare feet, mud, eyes fixed on the tree line. Rudy keeps his voice low: "Hey. Hey, look at me. You're safe." No response. "What's your name?" A long pause. The kid's mouth moves, barely audible: "…saved me…" Rudy doesn't catch the first word, doesn't catch the context. "Jamie?" The kid looks at him — not understanding, not agreeing, just looking at the person who came back for them. Rudy takes the silence as confirmation. "Okay. Jamie."

**Draft callback, not locked as final prose (per "Moss and the Huntsman," full data export):** Years later, someone asks Rudy why he picked the name Jamie. "I didn't." "What?" "They did." Then the truth: "First thing they ever said to me was that I saved them."

**Never given a name by the Keeper, confirmed (per "Moss and the Huntsman," full data export):** The Keeper never called Jamie by a name — only by whatever role or function Jamie served (unspecified — see [[Keeper]]). A title implies ownership; a name implies personhood, which the Keeper had no use for granting.

**No missing-persons record, confirmed (per "Moss and the Huntsman," full data export):** There's no report, no family, no old photograph. If Jamie exited the Hedge at the same physical point they entered, that predates the first recorded white presence in what became Colorado. More likely, Jamie came from somewhere else entirely. Nobody is out there remembering Jamie as Jamie — from the mortal world's perspective, Jamie didn't go missing so much as never arrive.

**"Moss," name origin confirmed (per "Moss and the Huntsman," full data export):** Given later, on the road, by another homeless/traveling kid who caught them mid-stare: *"So still you could grow moss."* Jamie also smells faintly of wet soil — not something they can smell on themself, but something others notice ("You smell like rain." / "No I don't." / "Yeah, you do."). This is a separate naming, by a separate person, from [[Rye]]'s "Cavekid" nickname.

**Warning before the Arcadia rescue, confirmed (per "Moss and the Huntsman," full data export):** Before going in after Caleb and Ben, Jamie warns Rudy directly: *"When we get Caleb and Ben, they might not be the same... Time is different in there. We might not be the same when we come out."* Rudy hears a warning about trauma; Jamie means it literally — time in Arcadia doesn't track with the outside world, and whoever comes back might be older, younger, missing pieces, or carrying memories that aren't theirs. Jamie knows this first-hand from their own centuries inside (see the captivity mechanism above).

**Keeping records, confirmed (per "Moss and the Huntsman," full data export):** Moss tracks the Huntsman's approach signs (see [[The Huntsman]]) like a private weather report — dated notes on missing birds, an aggressive dog, a week nobody at a commune touched each other, footsteps outside a door. To anyone else who found it, it would read as a paranoid person's journal. Moss hates being right about it every time.

**Draft scene — "the only one who ever believed in me" (2026-07-18, offered live, not yet locked as final prose):** Jamie tells Rudy he's the only person who ever really believed in Jamie — not belief in the *body*, with all the baggage of assuming you know someone because you can see them, but belief that Jamie is, and has always been, exactly what Jamie says they are. **Primary read, confirmed same session:** this is meant to land first and foremost as LGBTQIA+ allyship — a weird non-binary kid telling someone they've never had to "come out" to *them*, specifically because that person accepted Jamie as exactly who they said they were from the start, no negotiation required. The parallel to the book's belief mechanic (Caleb exists because Leanne believes in him despite having no body to point to, while Jamie has a body everyone can see and still spent their life having it disbelieved or "corrected") is deliberately kept as a subtextual hint underneath that, not the point of the scene. **Placement, confirmed same session:** right after the gas station gender scene (see below) — specifically so the coming-out framing reads first, and the belief-mechanic echo stays buried for a re-read rather than announcing itself.

**Handedness, confirmed (2026-07-18, decided live, grill-canon session):** Jamie is ambidextrous, but only as long as they aren't thinking about it. The concept of handedness itself is foreign to them — a very human idea Arcadia never taught them — so the moment they become consciously aware of which hand they're using, they lose all dexterity in *both* hands at once and can't reliably do fine-motor tasks like writing with either one. Fluent and unthinking one moment, fumbling and helpless the next, purely based on whether they're paying attention to their own hands.

**The imprint on Rudy, confirmed (2026-07-18, decided live, grill-canon session):** Jamie has a supernatural, one-directional bond with [[Rudy Williams|Rudy]] — Rudy is Jamie's "person." Jamie always knows what direction Rudy is in relative to them, and roughly how far away, the same way an Aes Sedai always knows the direction and rough distance to her own bonded Warder (*Wheel of Time* reference — a deliberate craft touchstone, not an in-world explanation). This is confirmed as one-directional: Rudy does not have the reverse sense for Jamie. Not necessarily a Book 1 element — Dave flagged this as something that will "absolutely come up later" in the series even if it doesn't surface in this first book.

**Not a killer, confirmed (2026-07-18, decided live, grill-canon session):** Jamie has never willingly killed anything — Dave's own framing is that they'd go out of their way not to step on a spider. This is why the Huntsman's death (see [[The Huntsman]]) is confirmed as Rudy's kill, not Jamie's — Jamie delivers the "Bye, Steven" taunt, but doesn't hold the blade. If Jamie ever does kill something on the page in a later book, it can't be played light or victorious; it has to land as genuinely heavy, a real violation of who they are, not a triumphant beat.

**The ongoing hunt, confirmed (2026-07-16, decided live — from "Westernizing What Wasn't"):** The Prologue's truck-ramming didn't actually kill the Huntsman — it got back up and has spent the roughly eight years since directly pursuing Jamie, who's glimpsed it "a dozen or so times" in that span (part of why Jamie never fully settles anywhere, including at [[Shepherd's Inn]]). Jamie has gotten good enough at evading it that the Huntsman eventually changes tactics — kidnapping [[Ben]] instead, to exploit the one thing Jamie won't just run from. This is what finally forces Rudy and Jamie into **Arcadia** (confirmed, not the Hedge) for the confrontation where it's actually killed with a cold iron dagger, at the **end of Act II** — functioning as the book's red herring/false climax, resolved before Rudy turns fully to the real Act III mystery. See [[The Huntsman]] for the full arc.

**The dawn curse:** Because the Hedge-exit happened at dawn and went badly, dawn itself became dangerous for Moss. They refuse to hop trains during dawn — they'll wait cold and hungry all night rather than catch out once the sky lightens. Their line when asked why: "Things crawl back in at night. But in the morning? That's when they get found."

Draft prose for the exit (Omniscient POV vs Alienation, 2025-06-05):
> Jamie stepped out of the Hedge and into a chill wind that tugged at their stolen coat. A field sloped gently toward a trailer park lit in dull orange. They crossed it barefoot, trembling, and ducked into the nearest barn. A pickup truck idled nearby, door open, engine rumbling. They climbed inside without hesitation.

> They didn't know why they trusted the shape of the truck, the hard plastic of the dashboard, or the rattle of keys left dangling from the ignition. But they did. Somewhere deep inside the years of thorns and lies, this place still made sense.

**Arcadia's twisted mirror of the mundane:** Jamie recognizes ordinary real-world objects (a truck, an engine, a steering wheel) not because they remember pre-Arcadia life, but because Arcadia's Gentry steal and repurpose real-world things for their own cruel aesthetics. Possible in-world explanations floated (not mutually exclusive): Jamie was made to build trucks out of bones and smoke for a cruel reenactment; was told stories about what trucks used to be, filtered through fae nonsense; their Keeper's domain held twisted reflections of modern towns; they overheard other Changelings or captured mortals reminiscing; time is warped in Arcadia, so they may have glimpsed the real world through a cracked mirror or a bleeding television set.

Jamie's Arcadia-version of a "truck": twenty feet tall, pulled by glass-eyed deer with jet-engine antlers, used to haul weeping statues between screaming orchards — called a truck, had headlights, rumbled, "but it wasn't right." This raises an open, eerie question: what did Arcadia give back to Jamie that it didn't take? When Jamie climbs into the real truck, they aren't afraid it won't start — they're afraid it'll whisper. They don't trust that anything will stay as it is.

Returns from the Hedge bleeding, whispering "it found me again."

Can't sleep — attempts to rest bring on withdrawal-like shaking after too many days of forced consciousness. When sleep is forced, it manifests as waking dreams: murmured lullabies, phantom bruises.

Hides in a barn with carvings in the beams — names, sigils, and dates that haven't happened yet.

A recurring gut-punch beat: Jamie says, "I remember who I was. I think he's still in there."

Townsfolk sense something is wrong but won't say it aloud — some spit on the ground when Jamie walks by.

**Won't say [[Caleb]]'s name at first:** calls him "the boy," "the missing kid," or "your kid." When pressed, claims not to remember, or to have never heard it. Reads as a Changeling-touched quirk, but it's really because Caleb's name is fluid and unstable — like a dream you almost forget on waking. Jamie seems more certain than anyone else that Caleb isn't real, but says nothing.

**Postcard motif:** writes postcards — a quiet gut-punch, expressing a longing for magic that doesn't come with a cost. Tied to stepping into places that feel like Arcadia but don't ask them to stay. Written at weird/kitschy Americana roadside stops, with [[Rudy Williams|Rudy]] quietly reacting to witnessing it (thread flagged for further brainstorming, not yet developed).

**Road life (train-hopping era, presumably before/around King County):** Goes by "Moss" among fellow travelers ("Oogles"); also called "Cavekid" by [[Rye]] — a nickname said like a compliment, "like they were carved out of myth." Gear/appearance: hoodie drawn tight, patched jeans crusted in dirt and oil, boots split at the toes and taped with neon duct tape ("FUCK ICE" scrawled on the left one, half-faded), backpack packed with bungee cords, a tarp, a peanut-butter jar with a rusted lid, and someone else's flannel shirt tied to the side.

**The name search:** When told "Don't forget your name," Moss replies: "It's spray-painted under a bridge in Omaha. I'll find it again." Implies their real/true name is physically inscribed somewhere in Omaha — an open thread on reclaiming their identity.

**[[Casey]]:** A road companion who didn't ride out with Moss this time — see her page for the farewell scene.

**[[Rye]]:** A road companion who died or was maimed defying Moss's dawn rule — the origin of the dawn curse above. See his page for the full story.

**Emotional style:** Doesn't say thank you — shows gratitude through action, not words (e.g. tucking a farewell gift away with only a half-shrug, half-nod). Never holds on too tight, even in a goodbye hug; affection reads as fast and rough rather than lingering.

**Wordplay note:** "Moss carries that weight like moss carries rain: quiet, slow, inevitable" — a bit of thematic wordplay on the chosen name itself, worth keeping in mind for prose about how Jamie/Moss processes grief.

**Role in the story (B-plot):** Provides thematic contrast to [[Caleb]] — someone who was lost, forgotten, and returned changed. Deliberately functions as a red herring: their story invites the question "what if Caleb was taken the same way?" before the true reveal (Caleb never existed at all). Reminds Rudy and the reader that belief shapes reality — Jamie's existence is proof of that. May confront the Huntsman again if it starts hunting Rudy or threatens Caleb.

**The stillness, confirmed image (per "Making What Wasn't Scooby-Doo," full data export):** The closest Moss comes to sleep is sitting absolutely still, staring into space, unblinking, for hours at a stretch. Rudy has witnessed this repeatedly — getting up at 3:30am to find Jamie already on the porch, coffee gone cold, in the exact same position hours later — and has stopped asking whether Jamie slept, because he already knows the answer. The drought goes back further than the 8 years since the Hedge-exit — Jamie hasn't truly slept for however long the Arcadia captivity actually lasted, possibly hundreds of years (see the captivity mechanism above), which is part of why the loss reads as bottomless rather than merely traumatic.

**Arcadia doesn't believe in coincidences, confirmed worldview (per "Making What Wasn't Scooby-Doo," full data export — Dave's own words):** Jamie doesn't believe in coincidences, or more precisely, believes Arcadia itself doesn't allow them. When Caleb goes missing around the same time Jamie returns to King County, that's not two unrelated events to Jamie — it's evidence something arranged it. This is a different failure mode than Rudy's investigative caution (see [[Rudy Williams]]): Rudy resists connecting things that aren't necessarily connected; Jamie's survival has depended on recognizing patterns instantly. Neither read is irrational — they just start from different priors. Jamie doesn't admit any of this to Rudy until after Ben disappears.

**Checking the crossing, confirmed behavior (per "Making What Wasn't Scooby-Doo," full data export):** Jamie keeps returning to the edge of the field/Hedge opening even before anything happens — not because they expect to find something, but the same way someone repeatedly checks a lock they already know is fastened. Asked what they're doing out there, Jamie's answer is some version of "making sure nothing's looking back."

**Still Sickness vs. psychological conditioning, reconciled (2026-07-21, decided live):** The literal "Still Sickness" (cabin-fever-like restlessness from staying somewhere emotionally heavy, see the confirmed physiology quirk above) and the psychological "running has worked, staying = danger" conditioning from years of evading the Huntsman (see the ongoing hunt above) both stand as true, compatible reasons Jamie never settles — a real physical nudge layered under a deeper, earned psychological one, not competing explanations.

**Dislike of the word "fae," confirmed (per "Making What Wasn't Scooby-Doo," full data export):** Jamie's own vocabulary is "the Hunter," "Arcadia," "the Gentry" — experiential terms, not a taxonomy. Jamie actively dislikes "fae" specifically as a word, not because it's wrong, but because it's imprecise — the same energy as someone insisting a pentacle isn't a pentagram. See [[The Huntsman]] for the fuller terminology note.

**Public nickname, confirmed (per "Clue Evaluation Assistance," full data export):** A creek kid asks, "You're him, aren't you. The kid who came out." Jamie answers simply, "I'm Moss." The kid's next line, missed in an earlier pass: "He doesn't like it when someone makes it back."

**An earlier, purely personal visit (per "Making What Wasn't Scooby-Doo," full data export):** Before any of the guilt-driven watching below, and before anyone has any reason to suspect the Huntsman is involved in anything, Jamie visits the crossing once just to look at it — possibly the first time since coming back to King County — less investigation than closure, or facing down an old fear. That visit is what starts the "checking the crossing" habit noted above; the guiltier, more surveillance-flavored version below builds on top of it once Caleb's disappearance gives Jamie something to actually suspect.

**Act 2.1 subplot beat ("The Root Cellar and the Thorn"):** Jamie has been staying close to where they exited the Hedge — near the barn where Caleb would later "vanish" — watching the barn, the dirt road, and the trailer park kids, wondering if they know something or even caused something ("someone else could have crossed back in"). Guilt is creeping in. They weigh whether to run again or stay and help Rudy, since Rudy once helped them when they didn't deserve it. Meanwhile Rudy starts noticing signs someone with supernatural knowledge is watching the same areas he's investigating: a still-warm cigarette by a chalked hunter-marked sign (see [[Hunter Network]] for the sign system), and a note reading "Good enough" in charcoal tucked into a book Caleb loved — another cross-character deployment of the catchphrase, and a sign Jamie's been near Caleb's things. At this point in the story Rudy and Jamie are circling each other but haven't reconnected yet, despite their established history — likely by design, saving the reunion for later. By Act 2.7, Rudy is described as "reeling from the Hedge" and Jamie as "quiet, altered" — this may be the resolution of their reunion/confrontation, and may also be what "it" refers to in the epilogue line "if it starts again" above.

**Second epilogue draft ("The Postmark"):** An alternate, simpler take on the same train-departure beat (same day as the thermos/tea version above). Rudy, having just confirmed Leanne chose to believe in Caleb again, walks outside to find Jamie waiting with a pack on their back:

> Rudy: "You sure about this one?"
> Jamie: "No. But it's good enough."
No big goodbye, just understanding.

**Epilogue scene (confirmed):** After Caleb's case resolves, Jamie leaves King County by train — closure without permanence. Setting: a rail siding outside Shepherd Junction at dawn, smelling of creosote and diesel, an idling freight train "groaning low as if it hates to move." Jamie crouches on the gravel tying a rag around one wrist and tucking loose hair under their hood — looks smaller and younger in the morning light, though the scars on their arms are visible in the growing sun, "pale like old spiderwebs." Rudy pulls up in his truck and doesn't get out right away; when he does, he offers a beat-up thermos and a paper sack. Key exchange:
> Jamie: "Tell her thank you. For believing."
> Rudy: "She'd thank you, too. If she knew."
> Jamie: "She doesn't need to know."

(The "her" here is unconfirmed but almost certainly [[Leanne]], given the belief/Caleb context.) Rudy asks if Jamie will call "if it… starts again" — left ambiguous whether "it" means the Huntsman returning, another crisis/relapse, or something else entirely; Jamie says he'll hear from her before that. Parting line: "Not everyone who's forgotten disappears. Not forever." Rudy: "Let's hope so." Confirmed catchphrase crossover: when Rudy says the tea "isn't good," Jamie answers "It's good enough" — deliberately reinforcing Rudy's "Not perfect. Just good enough." line across characters (see [[Rudy Williams]]).

**Fourth ending draft (per the [[Character-Driven Alternate Outline (What Wasn't and What Was)|Character-Driven alternate outline]]):** Jamie ends the book whole — still Moss, still changed, but no longer lost — and sleeps sometimes now, "not much, but enough." **Further ending drafts (per the [[Save the Cat Alternate Outline (What Wasn't and What Was)|Save the Cat alternate outline]]):** In "Stage 12: The Fallout," Jamie leaves the day after Caleb returns — bed made, a single train ticket left on the nightstand, a candy wrapper in the sink, and "Good enough" smudged onto the mirror. In the same outline's romance-structure reframing of Rudy and Jamie's bond: after a mid-story failed confrontation with the Huntsman (not otherwise detailed), the two have a falling-out — Jamie blaming themself for the Monster, Rudy blaming himself for believing he could fix it, neither saying goodbye, each thinking the other better off without them — before Jamie ultimately returns "not for the town, not even for Caleb, but for Rudy," telling him "If you believe in him, I believe in you." Rudy: "I believe in both of you." Jamie is willing to give up their last memory of Arcadia as part of the sacrifice to bring Caleb back. Rudy keeps their candy wrapper in a drawer afterward.

**Gas station scene (confirmed):** Jamie takes a while in the candy aisle buying a lot of candy while Rudy orders cigarettes at the counter. The clerk asks Rudy, low-key, "Are they a boy or a girl?" — Rudy: "I don't know... It never came up." The clerk presses about which bathroom Jamie's "supposed" to use; Rudy shuts it down (the station only has one bathroom anyway) with a look that says "drop it or I'll drop you." An alternate, softer phrasing for the clerk's opening line was floated ("Hey, not trying to be rude, just curious — is your friend a boy or a girl?") but not confirmed as final. Authorial note: Jamie's gender and LGBTQIA+ themes generally are intentionally treated as a fact of their existence, not a plot theme — this scene is likely the only place it's addressed directly in the book. Optional, unconfirmed embellishment: Jamie quietly clocking the whole exchange from the candy aisle without reacting overtly — choosing a candy bar like it doesn't matter, with just a hint of tension that fades once Rudy handles it.

**Candy motif:** Jamie has a private, wordless sense of humor around candy with gender-adjacent subtext:
- Airheads — "I hear something dumb and keep smiling"; a snide joke at the expense of dumb questions.
- Now and Later — "what I am now, what I was then, what I'll be later — doesn't matter, still sweet"; references transition/change/identity.
- Sour Patch Kids — "first they're sour, then they're sweet"; people misreading Jamie at first, or Jamie being prickly but loveable.
- Twix — two of a kind, identical but marketed as different; if Jamie grabs two, a deadpan "I'm both."
- 3 Musketeers — "all for one and one for all"; fluidity in identity, unity in diversity.
- Smarties — "I know exactly what's going on, and I'm not going to explain it to you"; extra fitting if held while Rudy fends off the clerk.
- Mike and Ike — "you want binaries? Have two!"; nods to the real-world "Mike and Ike broke up" marketing stunt that queer communities joked about.

Drafted version of the gas station beat: Jamie grabs an Airheads and a Now and Later, and when Rudy rejoins them, offers him a piece: "Thought it fit the vibe." No explanation, just a small grin — a moment only Jamie fully gets.

**Rudy sees Jamie's Mein (confirmed scene, in the Hedge):** At some point before the book's climax, Rudy actually crosses into the Hedge with Jamie — **confirmed (per "Project Chronicle Summary," full data export, found on a fresh-eyes pass) as Act 2.6's "The Hunter in the Thorns" disaster beat**, where they go in looking for Caleb, find only an empty clearing (Caleb was never in the Hedge at all), and are ambushed by the returning Huntsman from the Prologue — a retreat, not a victory, that leaves Jamie's Mien only partially receded afterward (one eye stays inhuman, one finger stays too long). This is the same event referenced as "the disaster" behind Act 2.7's "reeling" state (see [[What Wasn't and What Was]]). **Resolved (2026-07-15, decided live, grill-canon session):** The Huntsman-abduction/rescue crossing in the [[Detective Noir Alternate Outline (What Wasn't and What Was)|Detective Noir alternate outline]] — where the Huntsman takes Jamie because Jamie's belief in Rudy sustains him too — is retired along with that outline (see [[Development Notes & Unresolved Questions]]). The retreat-not-victory version above is the canonical scene. Rudy already loves Jamie and knows they're different, but doesn't know the half of it; the reveal of their true Mien lands as shocking, but Jamie's underlying familiarity and the urgency of whatever task is at hand come first for him — the shock isn't in the monstrousness, it's in how much stays the same ("seeing Jamie's Mein isn't just witnessing something monstrous — it's realizing that monsters can be kin," a thematic line worth keeping given Rudy's whole Monster Squad/hunter identity). **Resolved (2026-07-16, decided live, grill-canon session):** It doesn't reshape his view of past cases at all — Rudy's never dealt with fae/Changelings in any meaningful way before this, and he already knew Jamie was different long before ever seeing the Mien. The reveal confirms what he'd already accepted rather than recontextualizing anything from his history. Drafted scene: they cross the threshold "like it's nothing — a blink, a breath. One foot on red dirt, the next in something too green, too wet, too watching." The Hedge smells of copper, cut grass, and "something sticky and sweet, like rotted candy" (echoing the candy motif); the light isn't dark exactly, but bleeds sideways through a thousand cracks. "Then he sees Jamie. Not Jamie. Still Jamie." — stretched, sharp, old; skin humming like a bubble's surface, translucent and mossy-tinted; fingers too long; hair moving like it's underwater; eyes still big and dark but unblinking; mouth not quite moving right when they speak, "like a puppet trying to remember how lips work." A beat — the rustle of something in the brush, the sound of breath that isn't theirs (something else may be present, unconfirmed). Rudy doesn't reach for a weapon or flinch. Key exchange:
> Rudy: "Am I gonna come out looking like that?"
> Jamie: "Only if you're here too long."
> Rudy: "How long were you here?"
> Jamie: "I don't know. It got really hard to tell time after they took sleep from me. But I was here for at least a lifetime. Maybe two or three."
Jamie pulls out a piece of candy (red, bright, glinting) mid-scene. Whatever Jamie is, whatever the Hedge is, Rudy follows — "they're in it together," with no time to fall apart.

**Travel route breadcrumbs:** Not always solo — travels with a loose community of fellow riders (offers Casey "we got space"; other unnamed riders share train yards). Considered seasoned rather than an amateur "summer kid." Route fragments mentioned: a southbound train out of the farewell-scene yard; Nebraska (site of the Rye incident), with supplies scavenged as far as Topeka, Kansas — a loose Midwestern route consistent with eventually working toward [[King County, Colorado|King County]].
