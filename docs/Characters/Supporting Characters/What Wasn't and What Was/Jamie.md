No known last name. Physically appears around 16; chronologically unknowable, possibly centuries old. Non-binary, they/them. Ethnically ambiguous, often described as androgynous.

**Aliases:** "Jamie the Lost" (informal), "that street kid" (how locals refer to them), "Witness" (what Huntsmen call them), "Moss" and "Cavekid" (road names, see below).

**Appearance:** Tall, slender, graceful, with an uncanny presence — "elvish in form and manner, like an eerie fairytale illustration." Long, straight dark hair; big, expressive dark eyes, always a mix of curious and haunted. Pale skin with faint scars, especially on the forearms. Usually dressed in layers of secondhand goth-punk clothing: a long coat, patched-up boots. Voice is smooth and low, ambiguously between soft-masculine and husky-feminine; speaks deliberately and often cryptically, but rarely unkindly.

**Personality:** Old-soul wisdom in a childlike shell. Grounded but otherworldly — doesn't always react to things "normally." Resilient, skeptical of institutions, fiercely loyal to those who show them kindness. Deeply traumatized but rarely speaks directly about Arcadia. Dry, poetic sense of humor.

Taken into [[Arcadia]] early enough in life that they don't remember life before becoming a Changeling — and has been there for centuries, making them far older than they appear. Their Keeper (unnamed, undeveloped — see [[Keeper]]) held a domain that included twisted reflections of modern towns. Fled Arcadia through [[The Hedge]] and is being pursued by [[The Huntsman]] (Changeling: The Lost concepts). Brought something back from the Hedge with them — a voice, a look in their eyes that isn't always theirs.

**Speculative Changeling mechanics (implied, not confirmed):** Mask (what mortals see) is their human form; Mien (true appearance) might be even more alien and striking. Likely a strong Wyrd rating — the Hedge still clings to them. Likely Seeming: Darkling or Fairest (shadowy, elegant, elusive). Possible Kith: Mirrorskin, Reflections, or Palewraith (fitting their obsession with memory and loss).

**Self-harm and drug use:** Cuts themselves occasionally to feel real, to test the boundaries of Mask and Mien. Can't sleep naturally; relies on drugs to shut their body down, which provide no real rest or dreams — the drugs also suppress memories of Arcadia. [[Rudy Williams|Rudy]] knows about the drug use and hates it, but understands.

**Prologue scene (opens [[What Wasn't and What Was]]):** Jamie exits the Hedge for the first time in their known life — barefoot, trembling, wearing a stolen coat. Crosses a field, sees the lights of a trailer park, ducks into a barn, and climbs into an idling Ford F-150 (one door open) without hesitation.

**Continuity note (unreconciled — three different accounts exist):**
1. A quiet, solo escape: crosses a field, sees a trailer park, hides in a barn, climbs into an idling truck (the "Omniscient POV vs Alienation" prologue draft, below).
2. A chaotic dawn exit into "mortal chaos — sirens, shouts, maybe even someone bleeding," with "the sun touched them, and it hurt" (the "Train Hopping Farewell Scene" chat, origin of the dawn curse).
3. Escaped the Hedge around age 12 with a small group of fellow Changelings, the only survivor — found alone and terrified on a King County back road, bleeding and running from a Huntsman, and rescued by [[Rudy Williams|Rudy]] himself (this chat's full character profile).
These may be the same event told differently across brainstorming sessions, different moments in Jamie's life, or genuine contradictions to reconcile later — not resolved here.

**Rudy's rescue and aftermath (per the full-profile account):** Rudy found young Jamie bleeding and terrified on a King County back road, running from a Huntsman, and saved them — possibly killing or banishing the Huntsman — cementing Jamie's loyalty to him. Repeated attempts to place Jamie in foster care failed; they kept running back to Rudy. Eventually Jamie disappeared into the wider world — hopping trains, living off-grid — but returns occasionally to Shepherd Junction and Rudy.

**The dawn curse:** Because the Hedge-exit happened at dawn and went badly, dawn itself became dangerous for Moss. They refuse to hop trains during dawn — they'll wait cold and hungry all night rather than catch out once the sky lightens. Their line when asked why: "Things crawl back in at night. But in the morning? That's when they get found."

Draft prose for the exit (Omniscient POV vs Alienation, 2025-06-05):
> Jamie stepped out of the Hedge and into a chill wind that tugged at their stolen coat. A field sloped gently toward a trailer park lit in dull orange. They crossed it barefoot, trembling, and ducked into the nearest barn. A pickup truck idled nearby, door open, engine rumbling. They climbed inside without hesitation.

> They didn't know why they trusted the shape of the truck, the hard plastic of the dashboard, or the rattle of keys left dangling from the ignition. But they did. Somewhere deep inside the years of thorns and lies, this place still made sense.

**Arcadia's twisted mirror of the mundane:** Jamie recognizes ordinary real-world objects (a truck, an engine, a steering wheel) not because they remember pre-Arcadia life, but because Arcadia's Gentry steal and repurpose real-world things for their own cruel aesthetics. Possible in-world explanations floated (not mutually exclusive): Jamie was made to build trucks out of bones and smoke for a cruel reenactment; was told stories about what trucks used to be, filtered through fae nonsense; their Keeper's domain held twisted reflections of modern towns; they overheard other Changelings or captured mortals reminiscing; time is warped in Arcadia, so they may have glimpsed the real world through a cracked mirror or a bleeding television set.

Jamie's Arcadia-version of a "truck": twenty feet tall, pulled by glass-eyed deer with jet-engine antlers, used to haul weeping statues between screaming orchards — called a truck, had headlights, rumbled, "but it wasn't right." This raises an open, eerie question: what did Arcadia give back to Jamie that it didn't take? When Jamie climbs into the real truck, they aren't afraid it won't start — they're afraid it'll whisper. They don't trust that anything will stay as it is.

Returns from the Hedge bleeding, whispering "it found me again."

Can't sleep — attempts to rest bring on withdrawal-like shaking after too many days of forced consciousness. When sleep is forced, it manifests as waking dreams: murmured lullabies, phantom bruises.

Hides in a barn with carvings in the beams — names, sigils, and dates that haven't happened yet.

A recurring gut-punch beat: Jamie says, "I remember who I was. I think he's still in there."

Townsfolk sense something is wrong but won't say it aloud — some spit on the ground when Jamie walks by.

**Won't say [[Caleb]]'s name at first:** calls him "the boy," "the missing kid," or "your kid." When pressed, claims not to remember, or to have never heard it. Reads as a Changeling-touched quirk, but it's really because Caleb's name is fluid and unstable — like a dream you almost forget on waking. Jamie seems more certain than anyone else that Caleb isn't real, but says nothing.

**Postcard motif:** writes postcards — a quiet gut-punch, expressing a longing for magic that doesn't come with a cost. Tied to stepping into places that feel like Arcadia but don't ask them to stay. Written at weird/kitschy Americana roadside stops, with [[Rudy Williams|Rudy]] quietly reacting to witnessing it (thread flagged for further brainstorming, not yet developed).

**Road life (train-hopping era, presumably before/around King County):** Goes by "Moss" among fellow travelers ("Oogles"); also called "Cavekid" by [[Rye]] — a nickname said like a compliment, "like they were carved out of myth." Gear/appearance: hoodie drawn tight, patched jeans crusted in dirt and oil, boots split at the toes and taped with neon duct tape ("FUCK ICE" scrawled on the left one, half-faded), backpack packed with bungee cords, a tarp, a peanut-butter jar with a rusted lid, and someone else's flannel shirt tied to the side.

**The name search:** When told "Don't forget your name," Moss replies: "It's spray-painted under a bridge in Omaha. I'll find it again." Implies their real/true name is physically inscribed somewhere in Omaha — an open thread on reclaiming their identity.

**[[Casey]]:** A road companion who didn't ride out with Moss this time — see her page for the farewell scene.

**[[Rye]]:** A road companion who died or was maimed defying Moss's dawn rule — the origin of the dawn curse above. See his page for the full story.

**Emotional style:** Doesn't say thank you — shows gratitude through action, not words (e.g. tucking a farewell gift away with only a half-shrug, half-nod). Never holds on too tight, even in a goodbye hug; affection reads as fast and rough rather than lingering.

**Wordplay note:** "Moss carries that weight like moss carries rain: quiet, slow, inevitable" — a bit of thematic wordplay on the chosen name itself, worth keeping in mind for prose about how Jamie/Moss processes grief.

**Role in the story (B-plot):** Provides thematic contrast to [[Caleb]] — someone who was lost, forgotten, and returned changed. Deliberately functions as a red herring: their story invites the question "what if Caleb was taken the same way?" before the true reveal (Caleb never existed at all). Reminds Rudy and the reader that belief shapes reality — Jamie's existence is proof of that. May confront the Huntsman again if it starts hunting Rudy or threatens Caleb.

**Act 2.1 subplot beat ("The Root Cellar and the Thorn"):** Jamie has been staying close to where they exited the Hedge — near the barn where Caleb would later "vanish" — watching the barn, the dirt road, and the trailer park kids, wondering if they know something or even caused something ("someone else could have crossed back in"). Guilt is creeping in. They weigh whether to run again or stay and help Rudy, since Rudy once helped them when they didn't deserve it. Meanwhile Rudy starts noticing signs someone with supernatural knowledge is watching the same areas he's investigating: a still-warm cigarette by a chalked hunter-marked sign (see [[Hunter Network]] for the sign system), and a note reading "Good enough" in charcoal tucked into a book Caleb loved — another cross-character deployment of the catchphrase, and a sign Jamie's been near Caleb's things. At this point in the story Rudy and Jamie are circling each other but haven't reconnected yet, despite their established history — likely by design, saving the reunion for later. By Act 2.7, Rudy is described as "reeling from the Hedge" and Jamie as "quiet, altered" — this may be the resolution of their reunion/confrontation, and may also be what "it" refers to in the epilogue line "if it starts again" above.

**Second epilogue draft ("The Postmark"):** An alternate, simpler take on the same train-departure beat (same day as the thermos/tea version above). Rudy, having just confirmed Leanne chose to believe in Caleb again, walks outside to find Jamie waiting with a pack on their back:
> Jamie: "Train's leaving."
> Rudy: "You sure about this one?"
> Jamie: "No. But it's good enough."
No big goodbye, just understanding.

**Epilogue scene (confirmed):** After Caleb's case resolves, Jamie leaves King County by train — closure without permanence. Setting: a rail siding outside Shepherd Junction at dawn, smelling of creosote and diesel, an idling freight train "groaning low as if it hates to move." Jamie crouches on the gravel tying a rag around one wrist and tucking loose hair under their hood — looks smaller and younger in the morning light, though the scars on their arms are visible in the growing sun, "pale like old spiderwebs." Rudy pulls up in his truck and doesn't get out right away; when he does, he offers a beat-up thermos and a paper sack. Key exchange:
> Jamie: "Tell her thank you. For believing."
> Rudy: "She'd thank you, too. If she knew."
> Jamie: "She doesn't need to know."

(The "her" here is unconfirmed but almost certainly [[Leanne]], given the belief/Caleb context.) Rudy asks if Jamie will call "if it… starts again" — left ambiguous whether "it" means the Huntsman returning, another crisis/relapse, or something else entirely; Jamie says he'll hear from her before that. Parting line: "Not everyone who's forgotten disappears. Not forever." Rudy: "Let's hope so." Confirmed catchphrase crossover: when Rudy says the tea "isn't good," Jamie answers "It's good enough" — deliberately reinforcing Rudy's "Not perfect. Just good enough." line across characters (see [[Rudy Williams]]).

**Fourth ending draft (per the [[Character-Driven Alternate Outline (What Wasn't and What Was)|Character-Driven alternate outline]]):** Jamie ends the book whole — still Moss, still changed, but no longer lost — and sleeps sometimes now, "not much, but enough." Stays a little while longer before hopping trains again; may or may not leave a note. A porch/coffee scene with Rudy: "You sure you're leaving?" / "Don't belong anywhere too long." / "You could." / [no answer, just a smile] / "You're not who I thought you were." / "Neither are you." This is a fourth distinct ending draft for Jamie, alongside the thermos/tea epilogue, "The Postmark," and the bleaker Detective Noir ending — not reconciled with any of them.

**Further ending drafts (per the [[Save the Cat Alternate Outline (What Wasn't and What Was)|Save the Cat alternate outline]]):** In "Stage 12: The Fallout," Jamie leaves the day after Caleb returns — bed made, a single train ticket left on the nightstand, a candy wrapper in the sink, and "Good enough" smudged onto the mirror. No one else remembers them clearly, but Rudy does: "They kept the door open, so we didn't have to." In the same outline's romance-structure reframing of Rudy and Jamie's bond: after a mid-story failed confrontation with the Huntsman (not otherwise detailed), the two have a falling-out — Jamie blaming themself for the Monster, Rudy blaming himself for believing he could fix it, neither saying goodbye, each thinking the other better off without them — before Jamie ultimately returns "not for the town, not even for Caleb, but for Rudy," telling him "If you believe in him, I believe in you." Rudy: "I believe in both of you." Jamie is willing to give up their last memory of Arcadia as part of the sacrifice to bring Caleb back. Rudy keeps their candy wrapper in a drawer afterward.

**Gas station scene (confirmed):** Jamie takes a while in the candy aisle buying a lot of candy while Rudy orders cigarettes at the counter. The clerk asks Rudy, low-key, "Are they a boy or a girl?" — Rudy: "I don't know... It never came up." The clerk presses about which bathroom Jamie's "supposed" to use; Rudy shuts it down (the station only has one bathroom anyway) with a look that says "drop it or I'll drop you." An alternate, softer phrasing for the clerk's opening line was floated ("Hey, not trying to be rude, just curious — is your friend a boy or a girl?") but not confirmed as final. Authorial note: Jamie's gender and LGBTQIA+ themes generally are intentionally treated as a fact of their existence, not a plot theme — this scene is likely the only place it's addressed directly in the book. Optional, unconfirmed embellishment: Jamie quietly clocking the whole exchange from the candy aisle without reacting overtly — choosing a candy bar like it doesn't matter, with just a hint of tension that fades once Rudy handles it.

**Candy motif:** Jamie has a private, wordless sense of humor around candy with gender-adjacent subtext:
- Airheads — "I hear something dumb and keep smiling"; a snide joke at the expense of dumb questions.
- Now and Later — "what I am now, what I was then, what I'll be later — doesn't matter, still sweet"; references transition/change/identity.
- Sour Patch Kids — "first they're sour, then they're sweet"; people misreading Jamie at first, or Jamie being prickly but loveable.
- Twix — two of a kind, identical but marketed as different; if Jamie grabs two, a deadpan "I'm both."
- 3 Musketeers — "all for one and one for all"; fluidity in identity, unity in diversity.
- Smarties — "I know exactly what's going on, and I'm not going to explain it to you"; extra fitting if held while Rudy fends off the clerk.
- Mike and Ike — "you want binaries? Have two!"; nods to the real-world "Mike and Ike broke up" marketing stunt that queer communities joked about.

Drafted version of the gas station beat: Jamie grabs an Airheads and a Now and Later, and when Rudy rejoins them, offers him a piece: "Thought it fit the vibe." No explanation, just a small grin — a moment only Jamie fully gets.

**Rudy sees Jamie's Mein (confirmed scene, in the Hedge):** At some point before the book's climax, Rudy actually crosses into the Hedge with Jamie — likely the "reeling from the Hedge" event referenced in Act 2.7 (see [[What Wasn't and What Was]]). Possibly the same event as (or a calmer counterpart to) the Huntsman-abduction/rescue crossing in the [[Detective Noir Alternate Outline (What Wasn't and What Was)|Detective Noir alternate outline]], where the Huntsman takes Jamie specifically because Jamie's belief in Rudy sustains something in him too ("if Jamie stops believing in Rudy, Rudy won't come back") — not reconciled with this calmer version. Rudy already loves Jamie and knows they're different, but doesn't know the half of it; the reveal of their true Mien lands as shocking, but Jamie's underlying familiarity and the urgency of whatever task is at hand come first for him — the shock isn't in the monstrousness, it's in how much stays the same ("seeing Jamie's Mein isn't just witnessing something monstrous — it's realizing that monsters can be kin," a thematic line worth keeping given Rudy's whole Monster Squad/hunter identity). Open thread, not developed further here: how this shift affects Rudy's view of his past cases, and what it might cost him later. Drafted scene: they cross the threshold "like it's nothing — a blink, a breath. One foot on red dirt, the next in something too green, too wet, too watching." The Hedge smells of copper, cut grass, and "something sticky and sweet, like rotted candy" (echoing the candy motif); the light isn't dark exactly, but bleeds sideways through a thousand cracks. "Then he sees Jamie. Not Jamie. Still Jamie." — stretched, sharp, old; skin humming like a bubble's surface, translucent and mossy-tinted; fingers too long; hair moving like it's underwater; eyes still big and dark but unblinking; mouth not quite moving right when they speak, "like a puppet trying to remember how lips work." A beat — the rustle of something in the brush, the sound of breath that isn't theirs (something else may be present, unconfirmed). Rudy doesn't reach for a weapon or flinch. Key exchange:
> Rudy: "Am I gonna come out looking like that?"
> Jamie: "Only if you're here too long."
> Rudy: "How long were you here?"
> Jamie: "I don't know. It got really hard to tell time after they took sleep from me. But I was here for at least a lifetime. Maybe two or three."
Jamie pulls out a piece of candy (red, bright, glinting) mid-scene. Whatever Jamie is, whatever the Hedge is, Rudy follows — "they're in it together," with no time to fall apart.

**Travel route breadcrumbs:** Not always solo — travels with a loose community of fellow riders (offers Casey "we got space"; other unnamed riders share train yards). Considered seasoned rather than an amateur "summer kid." Route fragments mentioned: a southbound train out of the farewell-scene yard; Nebraska (site of the Rye incident), with supplies scavenged as far as Topeka, Kansas — a loose Midwestern route consistent with eventually working toward [[King County, Colorado|King County]].
