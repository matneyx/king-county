The community's default suspect in Caleb's disappearance — a man living in an RV near the woods on the other side of the creek from [[Leanne]]'s trailer park. A deliberate, fully-cleared red herring in [[What Wasn't and What Was]], not a hidden villain.

**Backstory:** Formerly a defense attorney in a two-man firm — his partner handled custody cases, he handled DUI defense. Falsely accused of molesting his partner's own child (age 9 at the time of testimony). Per the partner's own deathbed confession letter, the partner bribed the child to testify against him to protect himself: *"Said he panicked. Said his wife would've left if she thought it was him. So he bought the kid a dirt bike and said, 'Tell the court what I tell you to.'"* Convicted on testimony alone, no physical evidence — sentenced to four years, served eighteen months. His appeal was initially denied. His former partner died of pancreatic cancer roughly three years later and confessed in writing; the victim, by then a teenager, recanted. His conviction was **vacated** via post-conviction relief — legally exonerated, not merely acquitted, pardoned, or expunged.

**Prison left him permanently disabled** — beaten/assaulted while incarcerated, now needs a four-point cane (a walker would be more appropriate but doesn't fit inside his RV). This is a deliberate, unstated clue: kids describe the real watcher in the woods as tall and thin; the lawyer is short and heavyset, confirming — without anyone in-story stating it outright — that he isn't who the kids have actually been seeing.

**Present day:** Town opinion never caught up to his legal exoneration. Someone slashed his tires the month before the story's events, "preemptively protecting" a five-year-old daughter. Kids dare each other to sneak up and touch his RV — notably, **it's always the older kids who do it, never the younger ones**, an unexplained detail meant as a real clue (see [[Development Notes & Unresolved Questions]] — no payoff decided yet).

**How Rudy actually clears him:** Rudy physically walks the terrain and determines the lawyer could not have reached the creek where the kids have been seeing something — the two sides are separated by a "Detroit riprap" wall of five stacked junked cars, essentially unknown to adults, passable only via a garden-hose "rope" tied to a fence post and a dirt slide — kid-only infrastructure a man with a cane could never use. The police never verified this themselves; they built their case on "proximity, opportunity, history. Done." His own alibi for the night in question — that he was with a woman — turns out to be true, corroborated by undated (deliberately, jokingly timestamped "Jan 1, 2000 12:00 AM") kinky Polaroids/video, which is enough to satisfy Rudy without needing the terrain evidence at all.

**Key line:** *"You think I'm your monster, detective? I'm not. I'm just what you find when you're looking too hard in the wrong places."*

**In the book's front matter ("Welcome to King County" blurb):** Referenced without a name — "They say the lawyer in the RV used to be someone. Made the news. Made a list, too. Now he's not allowed near schools."
