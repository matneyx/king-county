Owns [[LeGrande Station]] in [[Shepherd Junction, Colorado|Shepherd Junction]].

**Physical:** Over six feet tall, around 300 pounds, blonde hair in a boring cut. Most often seen in a red polo with "LeGrande Station" embroidered on the left breast, khakis "stiff from starch." A former state champion wrestler — his size and athletic background are played straight against [[Rudy Williams|Rudy]]'s 5'7" frame, though Rudy is never the one who backs down. Confirmed independently in [[The Prodigal Son (A King County Chronicle)|The Prodigal Son]]: "over six foot, over three hundred pounds, three chins on a good day, and a full head of dirty blond hair," red polo stretched over his gut like "a hammock for his girth," grease stains across his khakis.

**A third variant of the public blowup scene (Book 2, LeGrande Station):** In addition to the two drafted confrontations in "Escalating Tensions Rewrite," The Prodigal Son manuscript contains an actual written version of this same beat — Hale needling Rudy in front of [[Jenn-with-two-Ns|Jenn]] ("Your very presence is losing me business"), Rudy firing back that Hale's business sense is worse than his own ("your shit-hole... sees less money in a month than I see in a single night" / "Oh, fuck you" / "I was here before you, and I'll be here long after your wife leaves you and takes the Station as her well-deserved alimony"). Hale immediately does damage control with the customers who overheard by offering free meals — a nice bit of business savvy layered onto the antagonism. Rudy calms down once back at the Inn, more bothered that Hale got to him than by what was actually said.

**A recurring, toothless bit:** Hale opens this scene with "I thought I said you weren't welcome here, Rudy" — implying he's tried to ban Rudy from LeGrande Station before, more than once, and it's never once stuck. Played as an ongoing, low-stakes ritual in the feud rather than a real threat.

**Personality:** Actually a pretty good guy — decent boss to [[Jenn-with-two-Ns|Jenn]], has a wife and a couple of kids, holds an MBA. Knows nothing about the supernatural; would probably piss himself if he found out, and then tell everyone about it — most notably, he'd tell everyone about Rudy. A gossip and a loose-lips risk, not a threat by strength or knowledge.

**Confirmed long-term series fate (per "Astrology Birthday Guess," full data export):** Explicitly confirmed by Dave as one of three named characters — alongside [[Jamie|Jamie/Moss]] and [[Jenn-with-two-Ns|Jenn]] — who survive all the way through the confirmed series finale (Book 6), in which [[Mickey Two-Dice|Mickey]] kills [[Rudy Williams|Rudy]]. See [[Book Order and Series Structure]] for the fuller confirmed 6-book arc this comes from.

**Relationship with Jenn (surrogate-father dynamic, not romantic):** Hired her at 16. Genuinely proud of her — she's put herself through nursing school (CNA track) while working his registers, and he's lined up a CNA job lead for her at a home in Jensen (possibly [[Western Slope Nursing Care]] — see the parallel note on [[Out of Scope Ideas]]). He warns her away from Rudy not out of jealousy but real (if overprotective) concern, and his private read on Rudy is more nuanced than his public complaints: "I think he's dangerous in ways he doesn't even see anymore. But no — not bad. Just broken." When Jenn once asked him outright whether Rudy used to be a cop or a criminal, he deflected with "Does it matter?" — protecting her curiosity rather than answering it. Publicly, when caught staring down Rudy in front of onlookers, he plays it off with "He's not dangerous. He's just lonely" — a performance that doesn't match his private read, and his hands are shaking as he says it. He's the one who tells Jenn the unconfirmed "Hollow Creek" rumor about Rudy (see [[Rudy Williams]]) — meant to protect her, though it reveals more about his own fear than about Rudy's guilt.

**Feud with Rudy:** Views [[Shepherd's Inn]] as a blight on Shepherd Junction bringing down property values, even though the Inn predates his gas station by at least 30 years. Wants to buy Shepherd's Inn and bulldoze it to expand LeGrande Station into something like a Buccee's — motivated in part by a county tourism grant for a "neon cowboy" welcome sign that Rudy's "blight" keeps jeopardizing. He wants Shepherd Junction to become a notable stop on I-70. Rudy wants the opposite: a little nowhere you grab gas and a drink before moving on. Rudy, for his part, sees LeGrande Station's chrome-and-neon 50s theme (and its milkshake machine/branded-hoodie merch) as an eyesore — but still buys his cigarettes there.

**"Never escalates" vs. blowup drafts, resolved (2026-07-16, decided live, grill-canon session):** The day-to-day feud really is mostly passive-aggressive — but Rudy does let his temper flare during a case sometimes, specifically because he knows it's safe: he's certain Hale would never physically retaliate to anything Rudy says, no matter how far he pushes. The three existing drafted blowup scenes (the two property-dispute versions from "Escalating Tensions Rewrite," plus [[The Prodigal Son (A King County Chronicle)|The Prodigal Son]]'s LeGrande Station version) are all this smaller, safer kind of flare-up — the Prodigal Son scene specifically needs a rewrite pass to actually read as lesser than currently drafted, since right now it plays too big. The real, one-time explosion is a future beat, not yet written: it happens later, during a case where things seem genuinely impossible, and it's bad enough that [[Jenn-with-two-Ns|Jenn]] calls Rudy out on it afterward — telling him Hale is a good guy, a good boss, and a good friend, even if he doesn't know how to show it.

**Roots of the feud, resolved (2026-07-16, decided live, grill-canon session):** No deeper hidden history — roughly six years (see the timeline resolution on [[LeGrande Station]]) of property disputes really is mostly it, compounded by a genuine philosophical disagreement about what Shepherd Junction should even be: Hale wants a thriving I-70 tourist stop, Rudy wants a sleepy nowhere town people pass through without stopping. Deliberately mundane and boring on purpose, not a hidden secret being withheld. **Added same day:** a low-key layer of jealousy over [[Jenn-with-two-Ns|Jenn]] — nothing specific or acted on, just that each man is her friend and it quietly bothers the other that she's close to him too.

**A specific early moment in the feud:** Rudy once made Hale cry, back when Hale first bought and fixed up the station — Rudy told him his new chrome diner "looked like a jukebox threw up." Hale was proud of it at the time.

**A future twist on the feud — the will, resolved (2026-07-16, decided live, grill-canon session):** Rudy's will apparently names Hale somehow — possibly a bitter technicality — and in one framing Rudy wants to replace it with an unnamed "she" if she ever "earns" [[Shepherd's Inn]]. That "she" is confirmed as **[[Amber]]**, from the "Ghosts of King County" actual-play campaign (now confirmed **Book 3** in the same numbered series as this book) — the earlier Jenn theory below is superseded, rougher brainstorming, not a live alternative. The mechanism: the Clara McKenzie case proves Amber's capability to Rudy, and combined with her bar experience, he hires her on to cover Inn shifts. See [[Development Notes & Unresolved Questions]] and [[Amber]]. Separately, in a fully drafted brainstormed "Book 4" ending, Rudy actually dies or nearly dies protecting Hale and Jenn, and leaves Hale the Inn outright in a letter (full text on [[Rudy Williams]]) — with instructions to give Jenn half if he ever sells, and to keep her away from an unnamed cache of proof of the supernatural world. Hale doesn't fully understand what Rudy fought, but "he already knows what Rudy protected" — this is likely the moment (per the brainstormed triangle summary) where Hale's arc shifts from "disgust, respect, resentment" toward genuine understanding of Rudy. Whether this Book 4 letter-scenario and Amber's confirmed Book 3 succession both happen, or one supersedes the other, isn't decided — a question for a future session.

**A glimpse of the supernatural (brainstormed, not confirmed adopted):** In one drafted beat, Hale witnesses something unnamed and monstrous behind the diner at night, and Rudy steps between them ("Go back inside, Nathan" / "You want someone to gnaw on, you ugly sonofabitch, you go through me.") — in tension with the otherwise-stated intent that Jenn (and by extension, presumably, Hale) never learns about the supernatural. Unclear if this is meant to be a real future beat or just an illustration of how far Rudy's protectiveness extends, even to people he can't stand.

**Note on a discarded earlier draft:** An early rewrite attempt portrayed Hale as a hunter/Fed with a dark shared past with Rudy (a "Cairo" incident, threats about blood-bonds, having let a compromised child burn). This was explicitly rejected as wrong for this character — Hale has no connection to the supernatural at all. See [[Out of Scope Ideas]].
