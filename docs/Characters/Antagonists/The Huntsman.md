**Near miss, location and staging confirmed (2026-07-22, decided live):** Happens on the Shepherd-Jackson Highway, on Moss's walk back from a drug run at an old motel in [[Red Bridge, Colorado|Red Bridge]] — a small, run-down pass-through town based on Olathe, CO, the kind of place nobody stops in on purpose. No ride waiting at 4am, so Moss just starts walking the highway back alone. It drags close to dawn — triggering the established dawn curse for real, on the page, for the first time: Moss knows exactly what dawn means for them, and this time might not make it off the road before the sky lightens. Satisfies the original constraint below (separate from the barn/tree line/field) without inventing a new location — this is Moss's own already-established nightlife (can't sleep, needs drugs, wouldn't loop Rudy into that specific errand) taking them somewhere real. **Moss doesn't tell Rudy about this, confirmed same session:** it happened on a drug run, and Moss knows Rudy disapproves and wants no part of that side of things — so this stays private, meaning Rudy doesn't get this as direct evidence the Huntsman is active again. Whatever makes him start believing has to come from elsewhere (see the animal-carnage scene below).

**The coyote and the squirrels, confirmed (2026-07-22, decided live):** While investigating the already-established "something in the woods" lead for Caleb's case (see [[Development Notes & Unresolved Questions]] — resolved as a real but mundane predator, not connected to Caleb), Rudy finds a dead coyote surrounded by several dead squirrels — a kill by attrition, not power, echoing the capybara-and-zebra approach sign above. **Hidden-heart/Keeper's-Title canon, confirmed skipped (2026-07-21, decided live):** Real *Changeling: The Lost* canon says killing a Huntsman's body isn't permanent unless its hidden heart (held in the Bastion of a sleeping mortal) is also destroyed — otherwise the Keeper's Title just reforms it in a new Huntsman. Dave's call: ignore this mechanic entirely. The reader never learns CtL rules, so Rudy's kill stays permanent on the page with no in-world explanation needed, consistent with the existing "Hunter story, not a Changeling story" framing above.

**Huntsman-shaped absences, floated (per "Making What Wasn't Scooby-Doo," full data export):** Rather than more direct Huntsman appearances, Dave and the chat discussed smaller, unconfirmed "signs" that build dread between the Prologue and Ben's disappearance without ever fully resolving into a sighting — a boot print where no one should have walked, a crow watching too long, unnatural silence in the woods, a single horn blast at dawn from miles off, an animal carcass positioned strangely, someone mentioning "a man in an old coat." Not locked as specific scenes — available atmosphere/clue material for the stretch before the Near Miss.

**Approach signs, confirmed (per "Moss and the Huntsman," full data export — Dave's own idea, distinct from the floated absences above):** Recurring, Moss-tracked omens that build before it actually arrives — ecological wrongness with a mundane explanation everyone else reaches for, not obvious evil signs. Frogs and toads start turning up in numbers people notice and comment on, short of a plague. Animals get stupidly aggressive — Moss has seen a docile old carriage-horse get attacked by a squirrel downtown, and there was a zoo incident where a family of capybara killed a mother zebra but left her calf alone. And people stop having sex — subtle, only noticeable to someone who's spent time around people who are normally sexually open (a campground, a commune, a bar where people usually leave together — all gone quiet). See [[Jamie]] for how Moss keeps track of these.

**First confirmed kill — Rye, confirmed (2026-07-22, decided live):** Before Ben, before the story even starts, the Huntsman has a real, successful kill: [[Rye]], a fellow Changeling and Moss's road companion, killed protecting Moss during a train-yard encounter years before the book opens (see [[Rye]] for the full scene — the "railroad bull" that appeared that night was the Huntsman in its established man-in-a-hat disguise). Deliberately meant to give the Huntsman weight as a genuine, already-proven threat rather than a pure red herring that only ever fails or gets thwarted — it's killed before, and the reader should feel that going in.

**Hearing-radius mechanic, confirmed (per "Moss and the Huntsman," full data export — Dave's own idea):** As the Huntsman closes in, the audible world shrinks around whoever it's hunting — not silence, but a kind of selective culling, the way a video game engine stops rendering sound past a shrinking radius. A train can still be seen — wheels moving, someone on the platform on their phone — but goes inaudible; ambient sound drops out the same way, farthest first; eventually the only things left audible are whatever's closest, ending with footsteps that don't belong to the person hearing them. Moss's own rule, per Dave: when you can still see something that should be making noise but can't hear it, that's the warning; when you start hearing footsteps of people nearby that shouldn't be that close, run. (Precise foot-by-foot distance tiers were floated in the chat as flavor, not locked as an exact, reliable mechanic.)

**Confirmed core defining traits (per "Clue Evaluation Assistance," full data export — Dave's own original stated description, missed in an earlier pass):** "Two defining traits: always walks but can skip through space, ala Jason Voorhees; and, his shadow is larger than it should be and kindof resembles 'a bear with antlers.'" This is the foundational visual/locomotion signature underlying every later depiction below — the Gothic version's "shadow" glimpses and the antlered-shadow imagery in [[What Wasn't and What Was]]'s red-herring photo-album scenes both trace back to this.

**Mechanical basis for a mortal defeating one (background, from a general rules-discussion chat, not a specific confirmed scene):** Huntsmen are tied to their hunt via a token/compact granted by the Gentry — a mortal (or a Hunter: The Vigil-style hunter) could plausibly stop one by breaking/stealing that token, exploiting an oath the Huntsman relies on, offering a substitution/sacrifice the Huntsman accepts in place of its target, or exploiting a folkloric bane (iron, salt, true names) tied to its specific origin story. This is general Changeling: The Lost lore Dave asked about, likely the underlying rationale for how [[Rudy Williams|Rudy]] could plausibly have saved young Jamie from a Huntsman — not a description of how that specific rescue actually played out.

**Gothic depiction (current direction for [[What Wasn't and What Was]]):** Never shown in full — only glimpsed as a hat, a shadow, a fingertip brushing the edge of a mirror. Dread over spectacle.

**Discarded pulp depiction:** A more violent, visceral hunter — willing to mutilate a secondary character (e.g. ripping out their eyes) just to provoke a reaction from [[Rudy Williams|Rudy]]. Dropped when the book's tone shifted toward Gothic horror.

**Origin:** Comes from the crook-path (part of [[The Hedge]]), a tangle of time-rotted hedge-thorns wrapped in glinting iron — jaw unhinged from years of quiet service.

**Confirmed nature (per the [[Detective Noir Alternate Outline (What Wasn't and What Was)|Detective Noir alternate outline]]):** Not just a creature but a function — "a servant of doubt, a predator of belief." Shape-shifts between a man, a beast, Leanne's face, and Rudy's own face. This directly parallels the "villain is disbelief itself" theme already established for the Caleb plot — the Huntsman may be a literalization of that same force, rather than a separate threat. Vulnerable to iron and sage. Its taunt: "You don't believe in yourself. You never did. That's why they die."

**Who actually lands the kill, resolved (2026-07-18, decided live, grill-canon session):** The "Steven" naming and the cold iron dagger both stay, split between the two characters rather than one replacing the other — but *not* played as a joke landing alongside a killing blow. Jamie says the "Bye, Steven" line as the taunt; **Rudy** is the one who actually drives the cold iron dagger home. Dave was explicit about why it can't be Jamie's hand on the blade: Moss isn't a killer — they've never willingly killed anything (Dave's own framing: they'd go out of their way not to step on a spider) — and if that ever changes on the page, it can't be framed lightly; it has to land as genuinely heavy, not a punchline moment. Rudy, in sharp contrast, already is a killer, and carries real unresolved weight about it — see the new note on [[Rudy Williams]]. So this scene isn't "Jamie's kill, witnessed by Rudy" — it's Rudy doing the killing, with Jamie's taunt as the only piece of levity the scene gets, and even that shouldn't undercut how much this specific act costs Rudy internally.

**Location of the final kill, resolved (2026-07-16, decided live — from "Westernizing What Wasn't"):** **Arcadia**, not the Hedge. Dave stated this directly while planning the scene ("Moss and Rudy enter Arcadia to save a child the Huntsman has kidnapped... that is probably where the Huntsman has to die") and confirmed it again later in the same session while discussing pacing ("The reader won't see Arcadia until Rudy and Moss return there to save Ben"). This retires the earlier "inside the Hedge" wording from "Clue Evaluation Assistance" as imprecise phrasing rather than a real competing version — Arcadia and the Hedge remain distinct locations in this cosmology (see [[The Hedge]]), and the deliberate choice to withhold Arcadia from the page until this scene is itself a confirmed craft decision (see [[What Wasn't and What Was]]'s Reusable Craft Lines / Western Style Notes).

**Refined, same location note (2026-07-18, decided live, grill-canon session):** Specifically the **edge** of Arcadia, not deep within it. Dave's own reasoning (explicitly speculative, since neither Changeling canon nor this vault has ever mapped Arcadia's geography): the Huntsman likely wouldn't set bait meant to lure Moss too close to Arcadian "civilization" — wherever exactly that is — so the kidnapped-Ben trap sits right at the boundary, not deep inside fae territory. Arcadia's interior geography remains deliberately unmapped beyond this.

**Timing, resolved (2026-07-16, decided live — from "Westernizing What Wasn't"):** This confrontation — and the Huntsman's death — happens at the **end of Act II**, not simultaneously with the Act III Leanne/Caleb resolution. Dave's own reasoning: the Huntsman thread is a red herring investigation that has to be closed out before Rudy can pursue the real answer, and the two locations are logistically "dozens of miles apart," making a simultaneous climax impossible without forcing a coincidence. The Huntsman is a genuinely dangerous, worth-investigating threat — not a wasted detour — but its purpose is to be *the false climax*, resolved so Rudy can devote himself fully to the true mystery in Act III.

**The full arc, confirmed (2026-07-16, decided live — from "Westernizing What Wasn't"):** Reframed in Changeling terms as not really a monster to kill but **a function** — a piece of Arcadia's machinery sent to correct an anomaly (Jamie's escape), not an individual with grudges of its own. The truck-ramming in the Prologue did **not** kill it — it got back up. Over the roughly eight years since, it's pursued Jamie directly, with Jamie glimpsing it "a dozen or so times." Eventually it changes tactics: rather than continuing to chase Jamie (who's gotten very good at evading it), it kidnaps **[[Ben]]** instead — exploiting the one thing Jamie won't just run from: someone else being threatened. This forces Rudy and Moss to go into Arcadia to get Ben back, which is also the first and only time Arcadia itself appears on the page (the Hedge is shown earlier; Arcadia is deliberately withheld until this point). The weapon is a **cold iron dagger** (Rudy has cold *rounds* for lycanthropes, per his established gear, but no cold iron ammunition) — cold iron is anathema to the fae in a way ordinary violence isn't, "the one thing that can make a creature of Arcadia vulnerable." Moss's exact contribution to the kill mechanically is still open, but narrowed (2026-07-18, decided live): Dave is thinking of it in explicit RPG-party terms — Moss as **support/controller**, Rudy as **striker** — and plans to check the actual *Changeling: The Lost* Contract/power list for something that fits that role cleanly, purely so the scene stays mechanically consistent behind the scenes even though the reader will never know Moss is a Changeling character in game terms. No specific power chosen yet. *Who swings the blade* is now settled — see the resolution above: Rudy delivers the kill, not Jamie. Dave was explicit that this stays a *Hunter*-genre kill by violence, not a purely metaphysical Changeling-style banishment, specifically because the reader hasn't been taught Changeling rules and wouldn't find a non-violent resolution satisfying in a book playing as a Hunter story.

**Genre-blend framing, confirmed (same session):** Dave's own summary is the cleanest statement of what the Huntsman actually is on the page: "this isn't TECHNICALLY a Changeling story and the reader doesn't know the rules. It's a Hunter story and, in those, monsters are usually killed with violence." Mechanically it's Changeling — a function of Arcadia, stopped by cold iron and Moss's fae-knowledge — but the reader only needs the Hunter-story stakes: something escaped something terrible, it never stopped looking, and it's finally found a way to force a confrontation. The method (cold iron, Moss's help) is what keeps the kill thematically Changeling even though the genre it's dressed in is Hunter.

**Red herring mechanics, confirmed (same session):** The misdirection isn't just "Rudy investigates the wrong monster" — it's that the Huntsman primes both Rudy and the reader to misread Ben's disappearance as more of the same pattern ("the Huntsman took another one"), when the Huntsman never touched Caleb at all. Caleb's fading is a wholly separate, non-predatory tragedy (see [[Caleb]]'s doubt-vs-disbelief mechanic) — the reveal isn't just "wrong culprit," it's "wrong *category* of problem entirely."

**This also resolves the vault's previously-unnamed "second missing kid":** The kid Rudy and Jamie rescue during the climax (see [[What Wasn't and What Was]]) — whose rescue is how they learn Caleb was never really there — is confirmed as [[Ben]], and it's the Huntsman, not some other threat, that took him.

**Retired alternate resolutions (2026-07-15, decided live, grill-canon session):** Dave confirmed the main Act 1.1–3.6 outline as the book's sole canonical plan (see [[Development Notes & Unresolved Questions]]) — the four alternate outlines were a drafting exercise, not competing endings. Three of them had independently landed on a "defanged/starved by belief, not killed" resolution instead: the [[Detective Noir Alternate Outline (What Wasn't and What Was)|Detective Noir alternate outline]] (power cut away via a town-wide belief-in-Caleb mechanic Rudy engineers by re-planting Caleb's flyers, "you can't destroy doubt"); the [[Character-Driven Alternate Outline (What Wasn't and What Was)|Character-Driven alternate outline]] (it "dissolves, not killed, but starved," its story rewritten when Rudy speaks loving, specific memory of Caleb into being rather than fighting); and the [[Mystery Crime Thriller Alternate Outline (What Wasn't and What Was)|Mystery Crime Thriller alternate outline]] (a fifth physical description — "a towering, faceless entity with antlers like dead trees and a cloak made of rotting leaves and stolen names" — plus a stakes rule that killing it outright would let "the Hollow eat what it has," resolved instead by naming truth rather than fighting). All three are retired along with the outlines they came from; the kill-and-name-him-Steven resolution above is the only one that's canon. The three alternates agreeing with each other that belief/memory (not violence) neutralizes the Huntsman was a coincidence of drafting the same climax through different templates, not a signal about the real ending.
