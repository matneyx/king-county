In a traditional three act structure, the b plot is usually resolved at the end of Act 2, right? 

--- ChatGPT ---

Usually, yes—but not always. I think it's more accurate to say that **the B plot reaches its climax before or during the climax of the A plot.**

The reason is structural.

The A plot is the story's central dramatic question.

The B plot exists to:

* develop character,
* reinforce the theme,
* or give the protagonist something emotionally necessary to resolve the A plot.

So it often resolves just before the A plot's final confrontation because it gives the protagonist the emotional or practical tool they need.

For example:

* **Star Wars (1977):** Han's B plot (selfish smuggler → committed friend) resolves when he returns to help Luke. Then Luke destroys the Death Star (A plot).
* **The Lord of the Rings:** Aragorn's acceptance of kingship is largely resolved before Frodo reaches Mount Doom.
* **Jaws:** Brody's fear of the water isn't fully overcome until the shark hunt, so the B plot and A plot resolve almost simultaneously.

## Don't introduce characters physically all at once

You are absolutely right about this feeling amateur.

The classic:

> Rudy Williams was six feet tall, with broad shoulders, gray eyes, a scar on his chin, and a leather jacket.

That reads like a character sheet.

Instead:

Reveal details as they become relevant.

First appearance:

> The man behind the counter looked like he had slept in his clothes, which was probably because he had.

Later:

> The old Marine habit never left him. Rudy still folded his shirts the same way he had overseas.

Later:

> He caught his reflection in the window. Gray had started winning around his temples.

Now the reader builds Rudy.

---

## A trick: describe what is unusual

Readers don't need every detail.

They need the thing that makes this place *this place.*

Compare:

> The room had four chairs, a table, a fireplace, and a bookshelf.

vs.

> The fireplace hadn't been lit since May, but nobody had bothered cleaning the soot from the stone. The inn remembered winters better than the people who stayed there.

Now the place has history.

---

## For your prologue especially

Starting in medias res with Jamie's escape is a good reason *not* to pause for description.

The reader doesn't need:

> Arcadia was a realm of impossible forests, strange creatures, and twisted paths...

They need:

What does Jamie notice while running?

Maybe:

* The trees don't move correctly.
* The moon is wrong.
* The path keeps changing.
* The air smells like something that isn't found in Colorado.
* The Huntsman is behind them.

The setting is revealed through survival.

---

## Westerns often use "arrival scenes"

One of the best places to spend pages describing scenery is when someone enters a place after a period of absence.

Rudy coming back to Shepherd Junction after years away?

Great.

A stranger arriving at Stanton City?

Great.

Someone crossing into King County for the first time?

Great.

Because the character is evaluating the place.

The reader gets the landscape and the character's values simultaneously.

---

## A useful rule for King County:

Don't describe what King County looks like.

Describe **why people who live there stay.**

And describe **why things that hunt there thrive.**

The same canyon can be:

* beautiful to a tourist,
* dangerous to a rancher,
* sacred to a Changeling,
* a hiding place to a monster,
* a memory to Rudy.

That's where your Western voice lives.

And honestly, your instinct about the D&D-style description is a good one. The reason it feels clunky in a novel is because you're already thinking like a novelist: the reader doesn't need a character sheet or a dungeon map. They need to feel like they've been in King County long enough that when Rudy says, "Nobody goes up that road after dark," they understand that the road itself is part of the story.

## The other reason this works: save Arcadia for later

If you show Arcadia in the prologue, you establish a visual language for it.

The reader thinks:

> "Okay, that's what Faerie looks like."

Then when Rudy and Moss go back for Ben, you have less room to surprise them.

By withholding it, you let Arcadia become a question.

The reader has eight years of rumors, scars, and fear before they finally see the place.

When they do, it should not be "the cool fantasy realm."

It should be:

> "Oh. This is the place that made Moss afraid."

One thing I would consider: don't make Jamie's first sight of Colorado *too* beautiful.

It should be beautiful, but alien in its own way.

* the grass is uneven.

* the road is ugly.
* the power lines are ugly.

A telephone pole is boring.

A telephone pole is also something nobody enchanted.
