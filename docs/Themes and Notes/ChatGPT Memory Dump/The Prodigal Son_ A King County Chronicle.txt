Prologue


The worn out shocks squeaked as Mickey rolled his mid-seventies Cadillac through the gutter and up the small driveway into the parking lot.  He drove slowly, not to avoid attracting attention -- though that was a concern -- but because, with his headlights out, he couldn’t see a goddamn thing.  It was a late September night in the small town of Jenson, Colorado, and a thick blanket of clouds blacked out the sky as effectively as ink spilled over a newspaper.  Throughout the town, a few street lights marked the intersections of major roads, but here, behind the Sunshine and Rainbows Day Care, it was darker than the inside of a coal miner's asshole.


“Over there,” said the bleach-bottle blonde in the seat next to him as she sat forward to point at a space he couldn’t see in the darkness ahead of them.  Her voice was honey -- the kind of voice that said its owner was made solely to give pleasure. “Pull in here… no… damnit, Mickey,” she spat, giving her voice the lie, “just stop the car.”


He did as he was told with an acquiescent sigh, parking beneath a weeping willow hanging over from the next lot over, and turned the key to kill the engine.  The old V8 dieseled for a moment, sputtering a few times before it shuttered to a hault.  Without saying a word, Mickey opened his door with a pop and a whine and climbed from the vehicle.  Other than the clicks and creaks from the old car settling, and the other passengers climbing out -- through HIS door, no less -- the place was “small town” quiet.  First, Missy, the blond bombshell that delighted in directing his life, and then Harv, a short round man with a bald spot and a grin on his face that said he only came through Mickey’s door to admire the way Missy’s ass filled out the clinging dark blue evening gown she wore as she bent over to climb out.  Neither of them said anything, but, with her eyebrows lowered and her lips drawn into a tight frown, it was plain on Missy’s face that this wasn’t the parking space she wanted.


Mickey fished a silver case from the inside of his oversized grey sport coat -- oversized on him, anyway… it would fit any average-sized man snugly -- and selected a wrinkled cigarette from inside that he lit and puffed on a few times before replacing the case.  It was a cheap cigarette, a cheaper suit, and it all matched the tired and washed-up rake of a man perfectly.


“Why are you doing this, again?  Wasn’t the point of convincing that kid to do your dirty work to, I don’t know,” Missy said with a sarcastic shrug, “do your dirty work?”


Where her voice was honey, his was ripping cardboard.


“The kid can’t get in here, and we can,” Mickey retorted, as though it should have been obvious.  “Besides, he’s got another job.  C’mon, Harv, let’s get this shit finished.”


“Sure, if you let me,” Harv said genuinely, stepping around MIssy. “When we started this, I offered, but you insisted on doing it all yourself.”


Mickey raised an eyebrow at him incredulously.


“When we started, you were too worried about getting caught -- while I was doing all the work, you hid in the car.”


“All you had to do was ask,” Harv said, raising his hands as if to say it wasn’t his fault.


Mickey snorted out a single-syllable laugh and walked around to the back of the car, with Harv following closely behind, to open the trunk.  Inside, nestled between a gas can, a five-quart jug of oil, and the spare tire lay a grizzled gray-haired man in sweatpants -- Allen, if Mickey remembered correctly.  Allen was breathing, but not conscious, and that was really all that mattered.  Mickey fished around in the trunk for a bit before coming out with an old tarp, which he laid on the ground between he and Harv, and grabbed Allen by the shoulders and began to lift, obviously straining to move the barely-overweight man.


After a few labored moments of grunts, curses, and one dropped cigarette, the old man was laying, still asleep, on the tarp. Mickey recovered his cigarette, took a long drag, and stood up to look at Harv.


“Damnit, I thought you said you were gonna help,” Mickey said, smoke puffing out of his lips with every syllable.


Harv simply shrugged with a quiet, “Sorry,” and Mickey exhaled the rest of the smoke before snuffing out the cigarette on the Cadillac’s bumper.  Without another word, he bent down to grab Allen by his shoulders -- making sure to keep the tarp wedged between them -- and started dragging.


* * * * 




It wasn’t a long distance under any normal circumstances -- the daycare, where the car was parked, was right next door -- but with the tarp’s nearly constant slipping and the weight mismatch between Mickey and the old man, it took over half an hour to drag Allen to the front of Western Slope Nursing Care.  Harv and Missy followed quietly, never once helping drag the dead weight, though they occasionally talked with each other and Missy would sarcastically encourage Mickey. In spite of it all, Mickey didn’t seem out of breath, or even upset he had to do everything..


“Now, you two shut the fuck up, or they’re gonna hear us,” he whispered to them harshly and continued awkwardly jerk-dragging Allen backward toward the entrance, nearly running over his own toes when he wasn’t about to fall on his ass.


As Mickey’s back side crossed into the range of the sensors, the automatic doors slid open -- it was after hours, but the nursing home had a no-locked-doors policy-- then closed and opened two more times before he was actually at the threshold, sliding the old man at a snail’s pace.  There was a security guard sitting at the desk, but he didn’t bother to get up as Mickey dragged the tarped body through the doors, even when the door attempted to close on the body another three times, each time returning to their “open” position after banging into it.  When the doors finally closed, and stayed closed, the guard sighed.


“Better let maintenance know the sensor’s on the fritz, again,” the guard mumbled to himself before returning to whatever game he was playing on his smartphone.  Mickey just kept on sliding.


Things were moving faster now that they were on carpet, which provided little resistance to the tarp’s slick underside, and Mickey only slowed long enough to glance at the inpatient roster as he lumbered down the hallway.  There were nurses walking the halls, doing their rounds and trying to convince patients to go back to sleep, but they completely ignored Mickey’s group as though they didn’t exist -- one nurse even went so far as to step over Allen! However, by the time they reached their destination the hallways were clear.


The door to Room 35 was cracked open but the light was off inside.  Mickey waited a moment to be sure no one was in the hallway, counted to three, then gently nudged the door open and backed Allen in.  Harv and Missy followed, but didn’t close the door, even after a glare from Mickey.  Finally, with another sigh, Mickey irreverently dropped the old man’s shoulders to the floor, stepped over the body, and returned the door to it’s previously cracked position.


The room was small, and sparsely decorated, with only a single chair and table beside the bed and an older tube television on what was probably the dresser in the corner.  The dim light from the hallway leaked under the door to provide some illumination, but Mickey flipped the light switch up anyway.  There was another old man asleep in the bed -- this one showed his age more, with drooping jowls, wrinkles across his forehead, and the type of too-much-skin-and-not-enough-fat double-chin that only the elderly seemed to get -- who peacefully kept sleeping, despite the light.


“You two are useless, you know that,” Mickey remarked quietly as he returned to Allen. He hooked his arms back under the man’s shoulders, and his ankle around the leg of the chair, and, with surprising deftness, had the old man sitting in the chair in one jerked movement.


Mickey stepped back around to arrange the chair so he had a view of both old men, then stood to compare them, just as the door to the hallway opened for a pretty young nurse with spiky black hair and pink scrubs to step into the room.


“Frank, you know you’re not supposed to be… Oh,” she said in quiet surprise, her eyes widening.  Mickey hardly glanced at her as he continued his visual examination but the air between them seemed to… shift.. and the nurse’s eyes -- her whole demeanor -- drooped from “alert with shock” to “placid” when he did. 


“I’m glad you’re here,” Mickey said, finally lifting his eyes to meet hers. “I’ve got a job for you.”


Chapter 1




A small drop of whiskey splashed out of the shotglass as the bartender, Rudy, filled it nearly to the brim. He filled a second glass to closely the same volume and slid it across the worn wooden bar top to the grey-bearded, leather-clad biker leaning heavily against its brass edge.  Rudy, being a short man, had to look up slightly to meet the biker’s eyes -- the biker was easily twenty years Rudy’s senior, but their eyes held a similar depth of wisdom and breadth of experience -- as he lifted his own shot glass with a smile.


“Congrats on the grandson, Gil.”


Gil grunted his appreciation, raising his own glass, and they took their shots together, slamming the empty glasses to the age-stained mahogany.  Rudy swept up the two glasses and dropped them into a grey plastic tub alongside two other shot glasses and four or five pint-sized beer glasses, each showing the tell-tale signs of having been used.  He unconsciously ran a hand through his almost chin-length dark brown hair -- which was usually kept in a slicked-back pompadour, but was currently in dire need of a trim, and no amount of wax seemed capable of holding it in place --  then frowned when he realized what he had done; in any normal bar, he’d have to wash his hands, now, to keep up with food preparation codes.  Luckily, the Shepherd Inn wasn’t any normal bar, and wasn’t registered as such in any of the county records.  No code enforcement officers had ever been through those doors, and never would thanks to the inn’s legal immunity -- a thank you gift from a county official for Rudy saving the the man’s son’s life.  A damn hard-earned gift.


Still, Rudy tried to keep up appearances, and code enforcement or not, his customers wouldn’t want hair in their drinks.  Trying to make the best of a not-so-bad situation, he picked up the grey tub and a damp bar towel and proceeded to make his way around the bar, picking up empty glasses and wiping off tables.


The Inn was small by almost any measure, with a single bar with room for two or three, two standing tables in the middle of the room, and another four tables pressed against the walls for people who intended to sit and stay awhile.  The small rush of oil field workers, who stopped in almost daily to get a quick drink after work before heading home to their families, had just trickled out, leaving only their dirty glasses and peanut shells behind as evidence they were there in the first place.  The Inn was now empty besides Rudy and Gil, just the way they liked it.  Typical Tuesday.


As he rounded, Rudy unplugged the old Twilight Zone pinball machine he kept in the corner, silencing its incessant beeping and ringing, to allow Waylon Jennings’ “Pretend I Never Happened” to be heard from the jukebox next to it.  In the relative quiet the lively Country two-step seemed to fill the place, the slide guitar suiting the old working-man’s bar perfectly,  and Rudy sang quietly along with the music like a man who’d lived out every word.


“Just pretend I never happened, erase me from your mind,” he sang, slightly out of tune. “You will not want to remember, Any love as cold as mine…”


As if on cue, Gil looked over to Rudy and asked for “one more for the road,” to complete the daily ritual, and the only real business the Inn received.  Rudy chuckled at the predictability and returned to his place behind the counter, where he washed his hands and poured Gil another pint of amber.


“That one’s on me, if you stick around to watch the place while I run next door,” Rudy said to Gil while fishing a few wrinkled bills from the old metal cash register. “My ice machine’s broken, and the rush took all I had left.”


Gil raised his glass and nodded, which was all the response Rudy needed, or even expected, from the quiet biker.


“Thanks, Gil.  Oh, there’s a shotgun under the counter -- if anyone tries to get a free drink while I’m gone, shoot ‘em.”




* * * * 


Shepherd Junction, the small glorified-rest-stop-of-a-town Rudy called home, was located at the intersection of US Interstate 70 and Colorado Highway 336 -- locally known as the Shepherd-Jackson Highway -- and consisted of a handful of single- and double-wide mobile homes, a small railroad siding with its own warehouse, Shepherd Inn, and a the newer gas station directly across the Shepherd-Jackson that was Rudy’s destination.


The LeGrand Station, once an actual train station, was purchased and renovated three years prior by Nathan Hale and now provided gasoline and diesel to the interstate-traveling masses, as well as sandwiches, burgers, and shakes through its ‘50s themed deli counter and diner.  Rudy hated it, despite the business it brought in, and despite the food tasting “actually pretty good.”  No, Rudy hated it because Nathan Hale was an “I’d-sue-my-own-mother-if-it-brought-me-a-buck” asshole that had been trying to shut down the Inn since he bought the Station, claiming that the old wood-sided tavern lowered property value, some other bullshit.


Still, the twenty-four-seven mini-mart lived up to its intended purpose, and was a convenient place for Rudy to restock when his small patronage drank up the Coke, or used all his toilet paper.  And tonight, lit up like a three-month-early Christmas Tree with all of its twisted neon-light trim, it was the only place in fifteen miles where Rudy could get a bag of ice. 


Rudy’s wallet chain clinked in time with the click of his cowboy boots on the oil-stained pavement as he swaggered across the Highway, through the fueling lanes between the pumps, and finally up the sidewalk to the red and chrome accented glass doors, which dinged pleasantly when he pushed one open.  The Beach Boys played loudly from speakers throughout the building, reflecting alongside the multi-colored neon lighting off the black and white tiled floor and glass-fronted fridges to overwhelm Rudy’s senses and immediately gave him a headache.


“Hey, Rudy,” called Jenn-with-two-Ns, a pretty hispanic girl in a red polo shirt and khakis, from her spot behind the registers.  “Nathan’s still here -- one of the servers called in sick, so he’s managing the diner -- so you’d better just get what you need before he sees you.  He’s already in a bad mood.”


“Thanks for the heads up… Gimme a pack of Camel Wides and I’ll need two bags of ice” he said, counting out the cash he had taken from the Inn’s register.


She grabbed his money and handed him a key to the freezer outside, which he took and stepped back out into the gasoline-scented night.  When he returned, a bag of ice held under each arm and the key clenched between his teeth, Jenn had his cigarettes and change ready for him.


“Gross… do you know where that key’s been,” she asked as they made their exchange.


“It can’t be any worse for me than these,” he said, indicating the pack of cigarettes. “Or breathing the constant wave of exhaust fumes coming off that street out there.  Hell, against that, smoking may actually be good for me!  Thanks, Jenn.”


Just as Rudy turned to leave, a shout came from the diner.


“I thought I said you weren’t welcome here, Rudy,” came the deep, booming, voice of Nathan Hale.  “Your very presence is losing me business.”


Hale, a large man -- over six foot, over three hundred pounds, three chins on a good day, and a full head of dirty blond hair -- waddled over to the registers from the faux-vintage soda counter.  His red polo shirt was wrapped around his gut, acting as a hammock for his girth, and his khakis had grease stains across his upper thighs.


“You said it, Hale,” Rudy growled mirthlessly, “but you don’t have the spine, or bad enough business sense, to kick out paying customers.”


Hale snorted.


“What do you know about business sense, cowboy?  Your shit-hole over there,” he said, waving a thick hand in the general direction of the Inn, “sees less money in a month than I see in a single night.  The way I see it, your presence is driving away your business as well.”


“Oh, fuck you,” Rudy snarled angrily as he started walking out.  His tone lightened as he said good night to Jenn, but it was clear he was mad.


“‘Night, Rudy,” said Jenn, who offered a small wave.


“Don’t talk to him, Jenn,” snapped Hale. “He and his precious Inn,” he emphasized the word so Rudy wouldn’t miss it, “won’t be here much longer.”


Rudy spun around, his teeth bared like a feral dog.


“Go fuck yourself, Hale,” Rudy snapped, his volume raising with every word. “I was here before you, and I’ll be here long after your wife leaves you and takes the Station as her well-deserved alimony for not slitting your throat in your sleep!”


The shouting drew the attention of every customer in the Station, and Hale immediately jumped to control the damage, spinning away from the fray at the front of the store to offer free meals to all of them. Rudy, on the other hand, stormed out of the front doors, and it was an effort to pass the LeGrand Station trash cans and displays without kicking them over.  By the time he was back inside his Inn, he had calmed down enough to recognize Gil had left, and left a small stack of bills on the counter.  Still, Hale had got to him, and that bothered him more than what he had said -- he knew the Inn wasn’t going anywhere, but as far as he could tell, the fat man’s dick must get hard just thinking about pissing Rudy off.  Disappointed in himself, he dumped the bags of ice into the empty chest below the broken ice maker, lit one of the cigarettes from his new pack, and poured himself another shot.


Chapter 2


Other than three graveyard-shift workers from the nearby warehouse, taking their midnight “lunch” break, the Shepherd Inn had stayed empty since Gil left.[a]  That didn’t necessarily bother Rudy -- what would he expect with a run-down bar at least a half-hour drive away from the nearest small town? -- but it definitely made for a long night.  It didn’t help that King County -- the little patch of Colorado Rudy called home, effectively from Stanton City to Stocton, over to Jackson Ridge, down to Shepherd Junction, and back to Stanton City -- was abnormally quiet, as well.


Rudy had heard a new group of Hunters -- people dedicated to hunting and killing vampires, werewolves, and other things that go bump in the night[b] [c][d]-- had set up in Stanton City, but that wouldn’t explain the lack of news from the rest of the County.  On a usual night, he could expect to hear about dead cattle, high school kids who went missing, mysterious lights near one of the abandoned farm houses in the woods, or any number of other things.[e]  Most of the time, the reports ended up being mundane -- mold spores in the silage killed the cows, and the lights in the woods were flashlights from the “missing” kids -- but it was his job to take the reports and assign them to any other Hunters in the area, local or just passing through.[f]


Dispatch hadn’t always been his job -- when he opened the Inn, he was an active Hunter -- but nearly thirty years of smoking had caught up to him, on top of the bad joints and bad habits that inevitably came with the profession.  There was a reason he set up in a bar -- alcoholism ran rampant among Hunters, alongside depression and lechery.  Suicide, overdoses, and untreated diseases took as many lives as the job itself, if not more.  Rudy was one of the lucky ones -- he was well on his way to dying of old age, something that was practically unheard of among Hunters.  He wasn’t old by typical standards, at forty-three years old, but he was an old man among his peers.  With that age came experience, and all his experience told him that this much quiet meant one of two things -- something big was going to happen, or something big had happened.  Or he had simply been replaced, but he was sure that news would have made it to him -- nothing of note happened in King County without Rudy hearing about it.[g]


Rudy finished the beer he had been nursing and began the process of closing up.  He swept the main room and wiped down the tables, and had begun emptying the register into a safe bolted to the floor below the bar when the door swung open.[h][i][j]


“Bar’s closed,” Rudy said, glancing at the pump action shotgun laying on top of the safe in front of him -- he wasn’t joking when he told Gil to shoot anyone that tried to steal from him, though Gil may have thought he was.[k]


A middle-aged man in a blue tailored suit and a cleanly parted haircut stepped through the door.  Despite its obvious price tag, the suit looked slept in, which matched the man’s five o’clock shadow and tired eyes.[l]


“I’m not here to drink,” he said in a voice that sounded used to giving orders.  “I’m looking for Rudy.  The girl across the street said I could find him here.”


Rudy stood up, but rested his hand on the shotgun’s pistol grip, still hidden behind the counter.


“I’m Rudy,” he said guardedly.  “What can I do for you?”


The suited man stepped forward to the bar with a gait that suggested stiff muscles.


“I’m Jeff Speno,” -- almost as an afterthought, he handed Rudy a business card -- “my son has been kidnapped.”


Rudy briefly glanced at the business card -- lawyer -- before absentmindedly shoving it into his pocket.


“Have you tried calling the cops,” he asked in a tone that suggested he’d gone down this path before. “Kidnappings are their deal.”


“No, I was told to come to you.”


“I’m retired,” the Hunter said, releasing his grip on the shotgun to fish a pair of shot glasses from below the counter. “I can get someone here in fifteen minutes, if you insist this isn’t a job for the police.  That’s your call.”[m][n]


He set the glasses on the counter, then turned to select a bottle of Jack Daniels from the collection behind him.  When he turned back, Jeff had an envelope in his hand and was presenting it to Rudy.


“No,” Jeff said, more insistently. Rudy noticed there were tears in the tired man’s eyes, and an exhausted shake in his voice. “I was told to come to you.”


Rudy set the bottle down next to the glasses and took the envelope from Jeff’s hand, examining it.  “Rudy - Shepherd Junction” was blockily scrawled on the face of the envelope in ballpoint pen, and when he opened it, his breath flew out of him like he was punched in the gut.


* * * * 


It was nice to be doing something for himself, for a change, instead of taking orders.  He had been a good soldier, even during his 16-month deployment in Saudi Arabia, but four years without the ability to make real, life-changing, decisions had beaten Rudy down.  He had heard Ash -- the last surviving of his childhood friends -- had been relocated to a facility in the Rockies, so he drove his beat up Ford F-250 West to Colorado without any real leads.  He was living out of the camper in the bed of his truck for a month, hunting and fishing for food between odd jobs -- some Hunter jobs, but mostly mundane --, when he pulled off I-70 into Shepherd Junction and first laid eyes on the old Shepherd Inn.


It was a worn down, abandoned tavern a few hundred yards away from a railroad siding and it suited his purposes just fine.  It wasn't expensive -- the bank that owned the property was practically giving it away -- and he purchased it outright with the money he had saved while he was enlisted.  He had big plans -- a bar, maybe a gas station -- but what he really wanted was a place to call his own that would bring him news of the outside world.  Still, it didn't take long to develop the habit of parking his truck aimed directly at the freeway onramp, as though, deep down, he feared he would have to drop everything and disappear. Permanently.


He had been working on the Inn all morning, and well into the afternoon.  The old bar stood empty -- Rudy had removed the old, broken furniture weeks earlier -- and a static-laced Guns 'N' Roses was playing on a silver boom-box sitting on the worn mahogany bar top, some “modern rock” station broadcasting from Stanton City. Dust and grease covered him from ankles to wrists, and his two-months-out-of-the-military beard itched like hell. He had just stopped repairing the leak under the bar's sink to find something to eat when the door to the Inn swung open.


"Bar's closed," he called out, pulling himself upright.  "Try back next week -- maybe I'll have my taps in, by then."


He turned to look at the door, hoping to see whoever had opened it was already on their way out.  Instead, there was a middle aged man in a crisp black suit with wispy blond hair that had receded into a respectable widows peak, and lighter patches of almost white-grey at his temples.  Rudy recognized the man from an article he had read in the newspaper a couple weeks prior.


"Executive Dawes," Rudy said in obvious surprise, "I know I'm late on my zoning paperwork, but you didn't have to come down here yourself."


"I'm not here about paperwork," said Dawes. "I'm here because I hear you... you hunt things."


Dawes clenched his jaw, and it appeared as though his outward calm wasn't coming easily.


Rudy shrugged, and quickly glanced around for a towel or something he could wipe his hands on, ultimately settling on his pants.


"I've helped a few farmers with things snacking on their cattle, if that's what you mean."


"My son... My son and his fiance have been kidnapped."


"What about cops?  Isn't that their job?"


Dawes shook his head, lowering his eyes.


"Four days ago, a letter was delivered to my office, demanding I bring some money to one of the old farms up in Boot Knife, insisting I come alone.  Inside the envelope were my son's dog tags and his fiance's ring -- it was his mother's ring, and my mother's before that, I'd recognize it anywhere."  Dawes’ breath shuddered, cracks showing in his strong facade facade.  "The very first thing I did was go to the cops, and they did everything by the book. They wouldn't let me deliver the money unless they were there with me... They were hidden, of course, but they were there.  When I brought the money to the farm, nothing happened. No one was there to meet me, and no one came to get it."


The false front Dawes had been holding suddenly shattered, his face contorting into an ugly cry, and he wrapped his arms around himself in an attempt to find comfort.


"The next morning, I got this."


He reached into his pocket and pulled out an envelope and shakily handed it to Rudy.  It felt padded, but not heavy, and he turned it over in his hands. On the front of the envelope, in ballpoint pen, were the words "I said come alone.  Try again."  No date, no time, just "try again."  He turned the envelope over and flipped open the flap to peer inside, almost dropping it when he saw the contents.


* * * * 
A finger. 


Inside the envelope was a child’s finger.  Last time, the first time, it was an adult’s finger, but even that experience couldn’t prepare Rudy for this.


He practically dropped the envelope to the bar top to free up his hands, which he planted firmly on his edge of the counter, and he took a slow, deep breath.  After an uncomfortable moment of silence, he nodded solemnly.


“I’ll find your son,” he said, his voice hard as an anvil.  “Do you have any idea how he was taken, or who would have taken him?”


“His tutor, Sarah… she picks him up after school, and he stays with her until either I or my wife can pick him up.  We hadn’t heard from her so we assumed she picked him up, earlier today, but no one was home when I went to get him.”


“And that didn’t worry you?”


“No, she’s taken him out to movies, before,” Jeff said, shaking his head. “We assumed she’d drop him off when they were done.  On top of that, my father went into a coma, last night -- I had other things on my mind.  When we hadn’t heard from them by nine, I went over to her house… again, no one was home.  She had a key, in one of those fake rocks, and I went in.. no one was there.”


With a shutter, the man across from Rudy burst into tears.  Heavy sobs shook him, and he leaned heavily against the bar.  Rudy let the man grieve for only a moment before speaking up.

“I get that you’re upset, but crying isn’t going to get your kid back,” Rudy said firmly.  “When did you get the envelope?”


Jeff pushed himself upright, his arms locked against his edge of the counter in a near mirror image to Rudy.  He sniffed heavily, blinking out the tears that were still welling up in the corners of his eyes, and his face assumed look of obviously forced stoicism.  He locked eyes with Rudy, who watched him, assessing, from below his now-lowered brow, before he answered.


“When I got home, after going to Sarah’s -- it was taped to my front door, so I couldn’t miss it when I pulled in.  And, no, that’s not her handwriting.”


Rudy nodded and swallowed.


“I hate to ask, but I need to know… Sarah and your son -- could they have slept together?”


Jeff’s red-rimmed eyes widened in shock.


“No… God, no… He’s twelve, and more interested in video games than girls.”


Rudy nodded and moved on.


“How about you,” Rudy asked, the tone of his voice turning razor sharp. “Did you fuck her?”


Jeff’s eyes went from wide to narrow immediately and he stared raw anger at Rudy.


“No,” Jeff growled. “I did not fuck her.  She’s barely eighteen, a goddamned lesbian, and I’m happily married.  Even if I weren’t, I have more integrity than to have sex with my child’s babysitter.  How is that even relevant?”


“I’m not going to give you details,” Rudy said grimly, shaking his head. “But, it’s relevant.”


He picked up a pen from next to the cash register, pulled the now-wrinkled business card from his pocket, and set them in front of Jeff.


“Gimme your phone number, one you’ll answer no matter what... I’ll call you if something comes up.”


Jeff took the pen and neatly scrawled a number onto the back of the card, a different one than either listed on the front, then slid it back over to Rudy.


“So, what am I supposed to do now,” he asked.  Instead of answering, Rudy picked up the bottle of Jack and poured two shots.


Chapter 3
Mickey sat hunched over an old metal office desk, closely comparing the handwritten notes and diagrams on two yellowed pieces of manuscript under a rusty desk lamp.  A short column of ash perched precariously at the end of the long-forgotten cigarette between the index and middle fingers of his left hand reached critical mass and crumbled to dust onto the old parchment, reminding Mickey of its presence, and he took a long drag off the cigarette before snuffing it out next to a half-pack’s worth of its siblings in the square glass ashtray.


“You’ve looked at those a thousand times, Mickey,” Missy whined from behind him, perching over his shoulder to look at the papers.  “There’s nothing new, even if you could read it.”


He breathed an annoyed sigh and leaned back in his chair, turning to glare at her.
