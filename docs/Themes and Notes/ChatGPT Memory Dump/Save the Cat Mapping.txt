Yes — that’s exactly the needle you’re threading. The Hedge and the Huntsman make a killer horror red herring — visceral, mythic, and genuinely threatening — but the real horror is emotional: the act of unmaking a child through forgetting.

So the trick is:

    Let the Hedge and the Huntsman be terrifying… but never let them be the answer.

They’re louder, scarier, and more genre, but not deeper. They’re the shadow that points to the real wound.

Here’s how you can ramp them up without stealing the climax:
🪓 Make the Hedge and Huntsman Symbolic — Not Structural

Let them represent what’s broken, not be what needs fixing.

    The Hedge = the boundary between denial and memory

    The Huntsman = the consequence of forgetting

They protect the untruth. They’re not the source of it.
🔄 Reverse the Horror Expectations

Instead of:
"Defeat the Huntsman → Save Caleb"

Make it:
"Choose to remember Caleb → Break the Huntsman"

    The moment Leanne remembers Caleb, the Huntsman starts to falter.
    Its shape breaks. Its voice cracks. It loses power.
    Because the thing it guards — the act of forgetting — is unraveling.

This keeps your climax internal, human, and earned.
🧠 Rudy’s Shift Is the Heart of the Reveal

Let Rudy make the same mistake the audience does:

    For most of the story, he’s convinced the Hedge is the cause of all this.

    The Huntsman is the villain.

    Jamie’s trauma is the origin.

But the twist isn’t “the Monster is Leanne.”

The twist is:

    The Monster exists because Leanne couldn’t believe.
    And the only way to win… is to remember. Really remember. At a cost.

🧪 Narrative Trick: Let the Red Herring Pay Off... Emotionally

If you treat the Hedge/Huntsman as a red herring only in terms of plot mechanics, it’ll feel like a bait-and-switch.

Instead, make it emotionally relevant:

    The Huntsman is terrifying because it’s what Jamie believes they let loose.

    The Hedge is dangerous because it mirrors the trauma no one wants to name.

So when Rudy realizes, "This was never about the Hedge," the reader doesn’t feel duped — they feel enlightened. Like they were caught in the same trap Rudy was.

They face the Huntsman, the child’s memory, and the town’s forgetting together.

    Caleb comes home.

    Leanne remembers.

    Jamie leaves. But Rudy keeps their candy wrapper in the drawer.

And Rudy?

    Not because the world is fixed — but because someone was saved.

It’s not romantic.
