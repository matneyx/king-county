I once believed a good mystery story gave enough clues up front for the reader to solve the mystery and then they stick around through the rest of the story to see if they were correct.

How can I do that with What Wasn't and What Was?

That belief is mostly true — and it’s still a good guiding principle, especially if you’re aiming for a story that rewards close reading and emotional investment. In the case of What Wasn’t and What Was, you're already layering metaphor and emotion over a supernatural mystery, so let's walk through how to deliberately plant the right kind of clues that allow a reader to guess the truth early — without ruining the impact of the reveal.
🎯 Your Goal

Let the reader figure out that Caleb was never born — that he's a grief-born thoughtform — but not feel robbed when they’re proven right. Instead, the reveal should feel like confirmation of a deeper emotional truth, not just an answer to a puzzle.
📌 Step-by-Step: Hiding the Truth in Plain Sight
1. Start with the emotional core, not the twist

You’ve already done this — the story is about grief, memory, and belief. The mystery of Caleb’s disappearance is a vessel for that theme. So don’t treat “Caleb wasn’t born” like a Shyamalan twist. Instead:

    Let that theme shape every clue.

    The emotional truth and the narrative truth are the same: he didn’t exist, but he mattered.

2. Offer the clues early — but make them about character, not facts

Readers miss most clues because they think they’re “atmosphere” or red herrings. Use that to your advantage.

Some examples you could use (or may already be using):
❖ Clue: Leanne's memories are soft-edged

    She never quite remembers her water breaking. She knows what the inside of the hospital looked like, but not the name of the nurse who stayed with her. She remembers the drive home, but not the first night.

Let the reader think it's trauma memory. It is — but for a different trauma.
❖ Clue: The missing child report was never filed

    Or it was, and then it was withdrawn. Or it changed. Or the case number doesn’t exist anymore.

It’s chalked up to small-town disorganization. But something is off.
❖ Clue: Jamie/Moss won't say Caleb’s name at first

    They’ll say, “The boy.” Or “the missing kid.” Or “your kid.” When pressed, they say they don’t remember — or “I never heard it.”
    This can feel like a Changeling thing. But it's really because Caleb’s name is fluid, unstable — like a dream you almost forget when you wake up.

3. Use the town as unreliable — not the narrator

Everyone in King County is slightly off. That’s part of the vibe. So let them be the red herring.

    People say, “I could’ve sworn she had a kid.”

    “Didn’t she come back from Salt Lake with a baby?”

    “I thought his name was Jacob.”

These are facts that contradict each other. But they aren’t treated as suspicious — just as the natural fog of memory. That way, when the truth lands, the reader thinks “Oh my god, I should have known,” not “Wait, what?”
4. Use at least one objectively true clue

This is where the detective mystery part kicks in — give the reader one solid, grounded breadcrumb:

    📸 A photo that no longer exists. Maybe Rudy finds a torn picture frame with faded wall shadow but no photo. Or a box of toys labeled with a name that isn't used anymore.

    📝 Medical records that stop short. A prenatal chart with no postnatal follow-up.

    📂 A school application for kindergarten... that was never processed.

This is Rudy's kind of clue — one that points to an inconsistency you can’t explain away emotionally.
5. Reward the reader who guesses it

The reveal should be earned — not shocking.

Let the moment of revelation be about acceptance, not exposition.

Rudy presents the evidence, but Leanne is already crying. She knows. She’s known.

Let the reader feel smart, feel seen, feel heartbroken — because that’s what makes a mystery like this unforgettable.
🔁 Extra: Re-readability Bonus

If you do this right, the second read-through of What Wasn’t and What Was should feel like reading a tragedy you can’t stop. The moment Caleb is first described, the reader should now see it all:

    His existence is never directly confirmed by anyone but Leanne and Rudy.

    Every adult is hesitant or unsure.

    Jamie seems more certain of the not-realness than anyone else, but says nothing.

    Caleb’s favorite book at the library? Donated by Leanne years ago. Never checked out.

All of that only hurts more once you know the truth.
