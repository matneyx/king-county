Are you familiar with the mystery system used in Brindlewood Bay and The Between? 

Yes! Brindlewood Bay and The Between use a unique approach to mysteries that differs from GUMSHOE and traditional mystery games. Rather than pre-determining the "solution," these games provide clues that players uncover and interpret through gameplay, allowing them to create the solution collaboratively with the Keeper (the game master) as the story unfolds. Here's how it works in each:

Core Mechanics for Mysteries
Clues First, Solution Later: In Brindlewood Bay and The Between, you don’t decide the true culprit or exact events beforehand. Instead, you prepare a list of possible clues that can fit the mystery's theme or setting, but the actual solution is left open-ended.
Theory Creation: Once players gather enough clues, they craft their own theory of what happened. They use clues to connect the dots in a way that makes sense for the story, rather than uncovering a single pre-determined solution.
Meddling Move & Complexity: In Brindlewood Bay, players use the “Meddling Move” to find clues. Success means they uncover a clue (from a pre-prepared list, or one the Keeper improvises). The number of clues needed to reach a solution depends on the mystery’s complexity, so the players decide when they’re ready to present their theory.
How This Affects Prep

This approach is great for reducing prep time. You only need to prepare:

A few core NPCs or suspects.
A list of potential clues (themes or locations help guide this).
Possible motives or story elements that can emerge organically based on player theories.

This setup makes Brindlewood Bay and The Between particularly effective for mystery-driven solo games, as it allows the mystery to adapt to your choices. The Keeper (or if you're solo, you as the player) can adjust clues and story elements on the fly without worrying about breaking the plot.

Absolutely! You can definitely adapt Brindlewood Bay’s flexible mystery approach to a solo GUMSHOE game, merging GUMSHOE’s investigative mechanics with the open-ended mystery resolution style. Here’s how you could set it up:

1. Create a List of Clues, Not a Full Solution
Prepare a set of clues for each location or scene, based on the kind of mystery you’re exploring. For example, in a noir setting, clues might include cryptic notes, hidden weapons, or suspicious alibis. Aim for clues that can be interpreted in multiple ways to keep your options open.
Don’t worry about a fixed solution or culprit. Instead, focus on clues that add atmosphere, character motives, and story threads.
2. Gather Clues through Investigative Abilities
In GUMSHOE, you spend points from investigative abilities (like Forensics, History, Streetwise) to uncover clues. Each successful use of a skill reveals a pre-set clue, which you can interpret based on context.
Leave some clues ambiguous so they can fit different narratives. For instance, a partial footprint or a locked door might mean different things depending on how the story unfolds.
3. Use Edge and Problem Cards for Twists
Like in One-2-One, use Edges and Problems (or a similar system) to introduce surprise elements. A successful roll or choice could yield an Edge, giving you a new lead, while a failed roll could add a Problem, like attracting unwanted attention or uncovering a red herring.
4. Theory Crafting as the Culmination
When you feel you have enough clues, step back and craft a theory of what happened based on the clues. You can then roll to see how likely your theory is to be correct or add a “final scene” where you confront the antagonist or reveal the solution.
This adds excitement, as the clues might fit a few possible solutions, and the story can surprise you as it wraps up.
5. Let Your Character’s Abilities and Choices Shape the Mystery
Instead of viewing each clue as a step toward a single solution, think of each as a puzzle piece. As your character follows leads, certain clues may connect, forming a theory that naturally unfolds from the investigation.

This setup brings the strengths of both GUMSHOE and Brindlewood Bay together, giving you the tools to keep mysteries compelling without needing extensive pre-planning, making it ideal for solo play.

What are the steps for creating a One-2-One character? 

Here are the steps for creating a One-2-One character in the GUMSHOE system:

Step One: Concept
Describe the types of mystery-solving characters for the setting. Players create a character concept that fits within the established genre.
You can provide stereotypes to guide players in selecting their character concepts.
Step Two: Assign Investigative Abilities
Investigative abilities are crucial for gathering information and driving the plot. Players should assign points to these abilities based on the number of players in the game.
Each ability has a rating, and you begin with a set number of points to allocate.
Ensure that all necessary investigative abilities are covered among the players.
Step Three: Assign General Abilities
Each player has a pool of points to spend on general abilities that help with survival and interactions beyond gathering information.
Choose abilities wisely to maintain a balance of competence and narrative flow.
Choosing Your Starting Problem
Players begin with at least one Problem card, representing ongoing trouble for their character. This personalizes the character and adds depth to the narrative.
Sources
Players can create Sources, or characters that convey information to the investigator. These relationships can evolve throughout the game.
Character Cards
Information about the character, including abilities and ratings, is consolidated on a character card for ease of reference during play.
Improving Abilities
After each scenario, players can choose to improve their abilities or acquire new ones, reflecting their character's growth and experiences.

These steps ensure that each character is well-rounded and fits seamlessly into the mystery narrative while providing avenues for development and engagement throughout the game!

How do Challenges work? 

In the GUMSHOE One-2-One system, Challenges are structured tests that players use to resolve actions in a narrative-driven manner. Here are the key points on how Challenges work:

Structure: Each Challenge consists of a name, the active ability being used, and outcomes based on dice rolls:
Advance: Indicates success, usually accompanied by a benefit or Edge.
Hold: Indicates a neutral outcome, where the character neither improves nor worsens their position.
Setback: Indicates failure, often leading to negative consequences, such as acquiring a Problem.
Outcomes: Outcomes are based on the player's dice roll, compared to set thresholds:
For a 1-die ability: Typically, an Advance might require a roll of 5+, a Hold 3-4, and a Setback 2 or less.
For a 2-dice ability: An Advance might require 9+, a Hold 4-8, and a Setback 3 or lessal Rolls**: Players can gain extra rolls by spending Edges or taking on Extra Problems, increasing their chances of success but adding potential complications .
**Creating Challee GM, you craft Challenges to create tension and uncertainty in the narrative. They should not only add stakes but also provide interesting story developments based on the outcomes .
Example Challenge Formatn a specific format that includes the Challenge's name, the active ability, and a breakdown of the potential outcomes (Advance, Hold, Setback) .
Contextual Design: Challenges should fit within thet, making them feel natural and relevant to the player's actions and the plot progression .

These mechanics create a framework that allows for dynamic storytelliagency and immersion in the narrative.
