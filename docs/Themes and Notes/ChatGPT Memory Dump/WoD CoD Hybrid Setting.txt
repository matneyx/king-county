I love aspects of both the World of Darkness and Chronicles of Darkness - lore wise, anyway.. CoD is mechanically better, imo - but I don't want to choose one or the other. Can you help me make a hybrid setting?

Absolutely, Dave — blending World of Darkness (WoD) and Chronicles of Darkness (CoD) into a single setting is not only possible but can result in something deeper, grittier, and more flexible than either on its own. Below is a structured way to approach building a hybrid setting, with dials you can tune depending on which themes and tones you favor from each side.
🔧 BASE STRUCTURE: Mechanics First, Lore Modular
1. Use CoD as the Mechanical Backbone

    Why: Streamlined rules, cleaner integrity systems (e.g., Conditions, Beats, Integrity instead of Humanity), and easier cross-template play.

    Keep: God-Machine rules framework, CoD supernatural templates (e.g., Vampire: The Requiem's clans/bloodlines).

    Modify: Plug in lore and concepts from the classic World of Darkness as needed.

🕯️ LORE INTEGRATION: The Core Fusion Pillars
2. Cosmology: Both Can Be True

Blend cosmologies by assigning them layers or epochs of reality:
Theme	Chronicles of Darkness	World of Darkness	Fusion Idea
God(s)	The God-Machine	The True God / Triat / Wyrm	The God-Machine is a shell, a mechanism left behind or hijacked by greater entities like the Wyrm or Weaver.
Underworld	Sinister bureaucracy (Geist)	Oblivion, Tempests	The Underworld is layered: bureaucracy up top (CoD), Oblivion below (WoD), with ancient Spectres seeping upward.
Spirit Worlds	Shadow / Hisil	Umbra (Penumbra, Near Umbra, Deep Umbra)	Hisil = localized spirit reflection; Umbra = a broader metaphysical cosmos above/beyond it. Umbra accessible via deeper Hisil zones.
Vampires	Requiem: No Caine, local myths	Masquerade: Caine, Jyhad, Gehenna	Cainean myth is just one powerful Blood God’s cult, not universal truth. Most vampires follow local origin stories.
Mages	Awakening: Watchtowers, Lie, Exarchs	Ascension: Traditions, Paradox, Horizon	Merge by saying the Exarchs are what became of the Ascension-era Technocracy. The Watchtowers are rewritten versions of the Spheres.
Werewolves	Forsaken: Spirit ecologists	Apocalypse: Rage-filled tribal warriors fighting the Wyrm	Combine into "The Forsaken were the ones who stayed behind." Apocalypse Garou are returning after banishment. Or vice versa.
🧬 SUPERNATURAL TEMPLATE BLENDING

Use CoD character creation mechanics but give the option to select Origins and Belief Systems from either line.
Vampires

    Use Requiem's Clans and Covenants as default, but:

    Include Masquerade's Camarilla/Sabbat as rival political orders — perhaps even remnants from a previous age.

Werewolves

    Base on Forsaken mechanics.

    Add tribes and spiritual enemies from Apocalypse as legacy spirits or “old blood” — e.g., Silent Striders are exiled Forsaken with deeper umbral ties.

Mages

    Mechanically use Mage: the Awakening but:

    Include the Ascension War as part of the setting’s history.

    The Technocracy = an ancient, failed attempt to control the Lie.

Ghosts, Spirits, Demons

    Let Wraith: The Oblivion be “deep” ghosts.

    Geist: Sin-Eaters handle surface-level, present-day ghostly concerns.

    Combine Demon: The Fallen with Demon: The Descent as two aspects of the same metaphysical rebellion — the Fallen are old Descenters who stayed behind.

🏙️ SETTING IDEAS: WHERE IT ALL MEETS

Here are some potential in-world justifications for why the two lores are colliding:
1. The Shatter

A recent cosmological event (or the slow failure of the God-Machine) is allowing deep Umbra to leak into the Hisil. The Gauntlet weakens. Old gods return. Cainean vampires awake. The Ascension War was just the beginning.
2. The Lie is Fraying

Mage: Awakening’s “Lie” starts collapsing — and in the vacuum, truths from the old World of Darkness seep in. Caine. Antediluvians. Apocalypse prophecies. The Exarchs and the Wyrm are not so different.
3. Multiple Realities

The Chronicles world is a copy of the original WoD world — the God-Machine rebuilt the broken world from before the Time of Judgment. But something got loose from the old version…
🛠 TIPS FOR IMPLEMENTATION

    Start Local, Think Global

        Begin with a single city or region where the bleed-through is most intense.

        Create localized myths — the Camarilla exists here because of something specific to the area.

    Write with Mystery

        Don’t over-explain. Use conflicting myths, incomplete truths, and unreliable narrators.

    Use Themes from Both Lines

        WoD: epic tragedy, cyclical destruction, cosmic horror.

        CoD: intimate horror, surveillance, entropy, moral decay.

    Make Cross-Line Factions

        A Banishers/Wyrm-corrupted Pack.

        A Kindred cult of the Abyss with ties to old Tremere.

        A Sin-Eater who hears the voice of Oblivion.

📘 Epilogue Scene: Jamie Leaves

Rail siding outside Shepherd Junction.
Dawn’s just starting to smear light across the high desert sky. The air smells like creosote and diesel.

A freight train idles, groaning low as if it hates to move.

Jamie’s there — crouched on the gravel, tying a rag around their wrist, tucking loose strands of dark hair under their hood. They look smaller in the morning light. Younger. But the scars on their arms are visible in the growing sun, pale like old spiderwebs.

Rudy pulls up in the truck, lets it idle. He doesn’t get out right away.

When he finally does, he walks over and offers Jamie a beat-up thermos and a brown paper sack.

Rudy:
“Didn’t know what you’d want. It’s probably all melted, but there’s ice in the bottom.”

Jamie takes it and sniffs the thermos.

Jamie (softly):
“Sweet tea?”

Rudy:
“It’s not good.”

Jamie gives a tiny smile, just the corner of their mouth.

Jamie:
“Guess that makes it authentic.”

They tuck the thermos into their pack.

Jamie:
“Tell her thank you. For believing.”

Rudy:
“She’d thank you, too. If she knew.”

Jamie (quiet):
“She doesn’t need to know.”

The two stand in silence as the train jolts. A steel groan rolls down the line.

Rudy:
“You’ll call? If it… starts again?”

Jamie:
“You’ll hear from me before that.”

Jamie climbs the ladder up into the open boxcar — graceful, like they’ve done it a hundred times. They don’t wave. But they pause in the doorway.

Jamie:
“Not everyone who’s forgotten disappears. Not forever.”

Rudy:
“Let’s hope so.”

And then Jamie is gone. The train begins to move, slow and steady, metal grinding against metal as it pulls away from town. Dust swirls behind it — thick at first, then thinning.

Rudy watches until the last car vanishes into the horizon.

Absolutely. Here’s a full character profile for Jamie based on everything we’ve established:

Name: Jamie (no known last name)
Alias: Jamie the Lost (informal), “that street kid” (by locals), “Witness” (to Huntsmen)
Age: Physically around 16; chronologically unknowable (possibly centuries)
Gender: Non-binary
Pronouns: They/Them
Heritage: Ethnically ambiguous; often described as androgynous
Appearance:

    Tall, slender, graceful with an uncanny presence

    Long, straight dark hair

    Big, expressive dark eyes – always a mix of curious and haunted

    Elvish in form and manner, like an eerie fairytale illustration

    Pale skin with faint scars, especially on their forearms

    Usually dressed in layers of secondhand goth-punk clothing, with a long coat and patched-up boots

Voice:
Smooth and low; sits ambiguously between a soft masculine and husky feminine tone
They speak deliberately and often cryptically, but rarely unkindly

History:

    Jamie was Taken by the Gentry (True Fae) as a small child, possibly an infant or toddler.

    Lived in Arcadia for decades or centuries — time flows strangely there.

    Escaped the Hedge around age 12 with a small group of fellow Changelings, but Jamie was the only survivor.

    Found alone and terrified by Rudy on a back road in King County, bleeding and running from a Huntsman.

    Rudy saved them, possibly killing or banishing the Huntsman — cementing Jamie’s loyalty.

    Repeated attempts to place Jamie in foster care failed; they kept running back to Rudy.

    Eventually, Jamie disappeared into the world — hopping trains, living off-grid, returning occasionally to Shepherd Junction.

Personality:

    Old-soul wisdom in a childlike shell

    Grounded but otherworldly; doesn’t always react to things “normally”

    Resilient, skeptical of institutions, fiercely loyal to those who show them kindness

    Deeply traumatized but rarely speaks directly about Arcadia

    Has a dry, poetic sense of humor

Powers / Traits (Changeling: The Lost, implied):

    Mask and Mien: Jamie’s Mask (what mortals see) is their human form, but their Mien (true appearance) might be even more alien and striking.

    Wyrd: Likely has a strong Wyrd rating; the Hedge still clings to them.

    Seeming: Likely a Darkling or Fairest — shadowy, elegant, elusive.

    Kith: Possibly Mirrorskin, Reflections, or Palewraith (suggesting their obsession with memory and loss).

    Can’t sleep: Jamie is unable to sleep naturally. They rely on drugs to shut their body down, which do not provide rest or dreams.

    Self-harm scars: They cut themselves occasionally as a way to feel real, to test the boundaries of their Mask and Mien.

    Drug use: Drugs also serve as a way to suppress memories of Arcadia. Rudy knows and hates it — but understands.

Role in Story (B Plot):

    Jamie provides thematic contrast to Caleb — someone who was lost, forgotten, and returned changed.

    Their story layers a red herring: what if Caleb was taken the same way?

    They remind Rudy (and the reader) that belief shapes reality — Jamie’s very existence is proof.

    May confront the returning Huntsman again if it begins hunting Rudy or threatens Caleb.

    In the epilogue, Jamie hops a train and leaves town once Caleb is safe — closure without permanence.
