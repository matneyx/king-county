A lot of these clues all seem like weird-for-weird-sake, without any real cause or payoff.  That seems like terrible writing... Shouldn't a clue lead somewhere, even if it's never followed?

---

### **ChatGPT**

You’re dead-on — yes, absolutely, a clue should always lead somewhere, even if the character doesn’t follow it. Otherwise, it's not a clue. It's just set dressing.
🚫 “Weird for weird’s sake” = lazy writing.
It’s the narrative equivalent of a jump scare that doesn’t matter. You don’t want that.

✅ A real clue serves at least one of these purposes:

Pushes the plot forward (it reveals a lead, opens a door, makes someone act).

Alters the character’s understanding (even if they can’t act on it).

Changes the emotional or thematic weight (builds dread, twists meaning, recontextualizes earlier scenes).

Creates dramatic irony (the audience sees the meaning, even if the character misses it).

Foreshadows future action or resolution (especially in stories with supernatural or memory-based unreliability).

🧭 A good rule of thumb:

A clue should either be followed, haunt the character for not being followed, or return later with consequence.

📌 Let’s fix an example:

❌ “There’s a drawing of a symbol in Caleb’s backpack. It looks weird. No one knows what it is. The story moves on.”

That’s flavor text — not a clue.

✅ “There’s a symbol in Caleb’s drawing. Moss flinches when he sees it. Says he’s seen it carved into the trees in the Hedge. Rudy doesn’t pursue it — but later, he finds the same symbol carved into the doorframe of a missing girl’s bedroom from 1979.”

Now it’s:

Emotionally charged

Specific

Connected to other events

A ticking bomb

Even if the plot never explains it 100%, it echoes through the story.

🔄 Clue Echo Example:
You don’t have to resolve a clue immediately — or even fully — but it should land:

🔍 Rudy finds a lock of hair with blood on the root. The DNA is human — but from two different people.

That’s a clue.

If he ignores it, it should haunt him.

If he follows it, it should reveal something: a fetch, a bloodline, a ritual.

If he forgets it? It should come back later — maybe that hybrid DNA matches another missing kid. Or him.

🧠 TL;DR:

A clue with no consequence is just confetti.

A clue that opens a question, even if unanswered, must matter.

Make every weird thing a mirror, map, or match — or cut it.

### **ChatGPT**

Great question — and it depends on your goals and where you're at in the process. Here's a breakdown:

✅ When It's Absolutely Worth Having a Partial Proofread:
1. You're testing the waters
You want to see if the proofreader is a good fit before committing to a full manuscript. Send them:

A representative 10–30k chunk

Ideally one with both action and quiet moments, narration and dialogue

2. You're polishing for agents/publishers
If you're submitting a sample (usually the first 3 chapters or first 50 pages), getting that section professionally proofread is totally worth it. It’s the first impression.
3. You're releasing episodically (e.g. web serials)
For writers publishing in parts (like Kindle Vella, Substack, etc.), proofing each 10–30k episode makes sense.

🤷‍♂️ When It's Less Worth It:
1. You want comprehensive story-level feedback
Proofreading focuses on grammar, typos, and mechanics — not plot, pacing, or character arcs. If you're still revising story elements, consider editing (developmental or line) instead of proofreading.
2. The rest of your manuscript is rough
If you polish only one section while the rest is still messy, it can throw off your flow and consistency when you go back to revise.

💡 Smart Strategy:
If you're cost-conscious but serious:

Pick the first 10k-15k of your book

Hire a proofreader on Fiverr with a good track record

Evaluate their work (and maybe test two at once)

Ask if they'll do a bulk discount or custom gig for the whole novel in parts

Many will say yes if they like your writing and know you’re organized.

Final Thought:

It’s absolutely worth it to proofread part of a book — if you’re strategic about which part and why.
