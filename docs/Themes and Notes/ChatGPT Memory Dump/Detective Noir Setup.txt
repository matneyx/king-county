1.2 - The siren walks in: A client will appear, seeking help with a case -- usually a murder or missing person.  They may appear vulnerable or wanting to help, but they will have a deep, dark secret that is bound to get the protagonist in hot water.  Although the femme fatale trope is common, there is no requirement for the siren to be female, or sexy, and in fact mixing it up may make your story more refreshing.

Leanne is perfect for this.

You can present her as a subversion of the traditional noir siren: not glamorous or seductive, but disheveled, exhausted, and frayed at the edges — yet still magnetic in her quiet desperation. Here’s how 1.2 – The Siren Walks In could play out for your story:
1.2 – The Siren Walks In: Leanne's Arrival

Scene Summary:
Rudy is drinking gas station coffee, flipping through a beat-up case folder he’s not really working on, when Leanne walks into his office (or just tracks him down at the gas station, the diner, or the motel lobby). She’s anxious but restrained, like someone holding a scream inside. She hands him a missing child flyer — her son, Caleb.

She’s not hysterical. She’s focused. Determined. Not just another tragic mother — there's something else in her eyes. Something like guilt. Something she’s not telling him.

Key Noir Elements to Hit:

    She wants help: The police aren’t doing enough. They think she’s unstable. There’s talk of social services. She’s running out of time and knows Rudy is the only one who might actually see the truth — or at least look where others won’t.

    She’s hiding something: Not obviously — not in a villainous way. But Rudy’s instincts tell him she’s not being fully honest. Not about Caleb, not about the timeline, not about herself.

    The case is personal: Rudy sees something in her story — something familiar. Maybe the way she talks about her kid. Maybe how the trailer park she's from is the kind of place he grew up in. Maybe how she reminds him of someone he couldn't save.

    She’s the key to something bigger: We won't realize until later, but Leanne’s presence — her belief, her doubt, her grief — is the fulcrum around which the entire reality of the story bends.

Mood & Tone:
The rain outside has stopped, but the air is still heavy. Rudy studies the flyer. The ink’s already smudged like it’s been held too long with sweaty fingers. He reads “last seen by his mother…” and looks up.

“You sure he didn’t just run off?”

She doesn’t answer. Just says, “Will you help me?”

Rudy knows: he already has.

3.1 - Final conflict: There will be a final dramatic confrontation between the anti-hero and the bad guys, during which the anti-hero may become quite badly injured, but will find at least limited victory by using her or his wit and cutting.  They may even have laid a trap for the bad guys that even the readers were unaware of (though clues and hints have been in place).

Oh, you’ve been building to this beat the whole time — and now Rudy walks into the Hedge with cold iron and a quiet promise to the kid he couldn’t save.

Here’s how 3.1 – Final Conflict plays out for your story, full-on supernatural noir:
3.1 – Final Conflict: Into the Thorns

Setting:
Inside the Hedge. A place barely real. Tangled woods, crooked reflections, rusted playgrounds, forgotten memories hanging like fruit. The sky is the wrong color. Time moves like water.

The Huntsman is here. So is Jamie — strung up, ensnared, but not broken.
Caleb flickers between being there and not. A shadow. A boy. A memory with a heartbeat.

The Confrontation:

Rudy is bleeding before it starts — from scratches that shouldn’t be there, from words whispered in his ear that scrape like blades.

The Huntsman isn’t just a creature. It’s a function — a servant of doubt, a predator of belief. It moves between forms. Sometimes a man. Sometimes a beast. Sometimes Leanne’s face. Sometimes Rudy’s own.

It says:

    “You don’t believe in yourself. You never did. That’s why they die.”

Rudy smiles through broken teeth.

    “Still here, aren’t I?”

The Trap:

Rudy lays it out slowly — he’s been baiting the Huntsman this whole time.

    The drawing left in his coat — it was iron-laced.

    The tape wasn’t just a message — it carried part of Caleb’s true name, layered in the static.

    The flyer with Caleb’s picture? Rudy copied it. Dozens of times. Planted them everywhere. Let the town see. Let belief regrow.

The trap isn't a snare. It's consensus.
The world has started to remember Caleb.

And that makes the Huntsman vulnerable.

The Fight:
It’s dirty. Brutal. Rudy’s outmatched. But he doesn’t back down.
He burns sage and swings iron. He bleeds and spits and claws. Jamie breaks free and joins him.

They don’t destroy the Huntsman — you can’t destroy doubt —
but they cut away its power.
Rudy doesn’t kill it. He makes it irrelevant.

Victory — But Not Clean:
Rudy collapses, leg broken again, ribs shattered. He looks up at Jamie and sees them — clearly — for the first time.

    “Told you I’d come back.”

They drag him out of the Hedge. Jamie limping. Rudy half-conscious. Caleb trailing behind — not quite real, not quite gone — flickering, but smiling.

3.2 - Back to the office: In noir, it's normal for the story to ultimately end in tragedy, underlining the sense of fatalism and nihilism. The detective themselves in particular should be in no better position than they were before.  However, if you're writing more hardboiled than noir, then you could offer a glimmer of hope...

Absolutely — this is your cigarette-smoke-and-cold-coffee closer. Rudy makes it back, but he's not okay. He solved the small case — found Caleb, in whatever way someone like Caleb can be found — but the world stays broken, and so does he.
3.2 – Back to the Office: The Long Fade

Scene Summary:
Rudy is back at Shepherd’s Inn. The case is closed — technically. Caleb is back in some form. Leanne believes again. Maybe there's even a blurry Polaroid of mother and child on Rudy’s desk, something he looks at with squinted doubt.

But no one else remembers what happened. No one talks about Jamie. The flyers are gone again. The photo on the library wall has different kids now. The tape won’t play.

The Hedge is still there.

Rudy’s State:

    Physically: Battered, limping, maybe with a cane or a new brace on that old leg.

    Mentally: Worse than ever, maybe. But with a grim sort of peace.

    He doesn't sleep better. He just knows more about the dark.

He sits in his office with a fresh bandage on his hand and an old case file open in front of him. There’s a knock at the door.

He doesn’t look up.

    “If it’s another missing kid, I charge double.”

But he still listens. He always listens.
