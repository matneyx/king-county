If I use third-person omnicscient, is it okay that I don't try to describe things as absolutely foreign?  "A large blocky monstrosity with glowing yellow eyes barreled toward Jamie..." just feels dumb.

Yes, you're absolutely right to trust your instincts on this. If you're writing in third-person omniscient, you do not need to describe the mundane world through the lens of total alienation unless that disorientation is part of your narrative goal.

In third-person omniscient, you’re free to name things what they are—even if the character wouldn't. The narrator knows what a truck is, even if Jamie doesn’t. You might briefly hint at Jamie's unfamiliarity for emotional or tonal effect, but you don’t need to fully immerse the prose in their confusion. That kind of thing works better in limited third-person, where you're tightly bound to the character’s perception.
Here's a quick comparison to clarify:
✅ Third-person omniscient (clear, clean, and focused):

    Jamie stepped out of the Hedge and into a chill wind that tugged at their stolen coat. A field sloped gently toward a trailer park lit in dull orange. They crossed it barefoot, trembling, and ducked into the nearest barn. A pickup truck idled nearby, door open, engine rumbling. They climbed inside without hesitation.

🚫 Overwritten for unfamiliarity (awkward and distancing):

    The creature—a blocky metal beast with glowing eyes—snorted fumes and roared. Jamie froze. It stood on black stone hooves and waited, rumbling. A hole opened in its side. Jamie leapt into its belly.

This latter style only works well if your goal is horror, satire, or extreme fish-out-of-water storytelling. And even then, it wears thin fast unless used with intent.
Your Best Approach

Use Jamie’s emotional dissonance, not their literal ignorance, to guide the tone. Jamie recognizes things instinctually, even if they don’t remember them—because Arcadia twisted their memory, not their entire wiring as a person.

    They didn’t know why they trusted the shape of the truck, the hard plastic of the dashboard, or the rattle of keys left dangling from the ignition. But they did. Somewhere deep inside the years of thorns and lies, this place still made sense.

Let the omniscient voice do the heavy lifting for clarity, and let Jamie's trauma, instincts, and unease bring the weird.

Here’s why:
🕸️ Arcadia Isn’t a Vacuum

Arcadia warps reality, but it’s deeply obsessed with mirroring, mocking, and manipulating the real world. The Gentry often steal people, ideas, language, objects, and culture—not to preserve them, but to repurpose them for their own alien aesthetics.

So it’s entirely plausible that:

    Jamie was made to build trucks out of bones and smoke for some cruel reenactment.

    They were told stories about what trucks used to be, filtered through fae nonsense.

    Their Keeper’s domain included twisted reflections of modern towns.

    They overheard other Changelings or captured mortals reminiscing.

    Time is warped there—Jamie could have witnessed glimpses of the real world through a cracked mirror or bleeding television set.

🧠 Memory vs. Recognition

Even if Jamie can’t remember life before, they might still carry instinctual knowledge:

    The sound of an engine.

    The smell of exhaust.

    The shape of a steering wheel or a seatbelt.

Just like someone with amnesia might know how to play piano without knowing their name, Jamie might leap into a truck and know how to start it, even if they couldn’t tell you why.
👁️ Narrative Benefit

This lets you:

    Avoid clunky “alien observer” narration.

    Reinforce the theme that Jamie isn’t fully human or fully fae anymore.

    Build unease—because Jamie shouldn’t know this stuff, but they do. It raises the question: What did Arcadia give back to them that it didn’t take?

When addressing an alien world -- Kameron Hurley has quite a few things unique to her Bel Dame setting but she never takes the time to explain them.  Instead, she names and interacts with them just as the people in that world do.  It's weird at first but it's fast and lets you focus on Nyx's story and not how different the world is from our own.

Yes, exactly—and that’s one of the core strengths of immersive speculative fiction. What Hurley does in the Bel Dame books is a masterclass in confidence through context. She trusts the reader to keep up, and more importantly, she keeps the story moving. That’s key.

Instead of saying:

    "A bindlecat—a creature native to the dunes, known for its poisonous spines and aggressive mating rituals—skittered across the rocks."

She just says:

    "The bindlecat hissed. Nyx kicked it."

No hand-holding. Just momentum and character.
Why It Works:

    Worldbuilding is baked into action — You learn what things are by how they’re used, not through exposition.

    It centers the character's perspective — If Nyx doesn’t stop to explain what a bindlecat is, then neither should we.

    It creates texture and immersion — The world feels bigger than the page, like there’s more there than you’re being told. Which there is.

Applying It to Your Work:

You’re already doing this by skipping “blocky glowing monstrosity” and just saying “truck.” Same principle. You don’t need to explain every bit of the Hedge or Arcadia. Let it feel lived in. Let Jamie interact with things as if they’re normal to them—and let readers catch up.

You could write:

    “The Huntsman came from the crook-path, a tangle of time-rotted hedge-thorns wrapped in glinting iron, jaw unhinged from years of quiet service.”

And never say what a “crook-path” is. Because Jamie knows. And if Jamie knows, so does the story.

You’ve got the emotional instincts. Add in the confidence to let readers live in your world without a tour guide, and you’re in Hurley territory.
