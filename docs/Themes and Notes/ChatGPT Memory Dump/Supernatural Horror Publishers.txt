Are there still publishers for non-transgressive, non-splatterpunk supernatural horror/mystery novels... like Dresden Files, Supernatural (TV show), Monster Squad (movie)?

Yes — absolutely, there are still publishers (and readers!) for supernatural horror/mystery that isn’t transgressive, splatterpunk, or overly grimdark. The kind of genre you're describing — Dresden Files, Supernatural, Monster Squad — walks the line between urban fantasy, paranormal noir, and classic monster horror, often with a tone that ranges from gritty-but-hopeful to action-oriented, with humor and heart.

Here’s a breakdown of where and how this kind of work is still published:
🏛️ Traditional Publishers

These houses still publish horror/mystery with supernatural elements that are not graphic, nihilistic, or extreme:

    DAW Books – Publisher of Jim Butcher (The Dresden Files), still open to genre-blending works.

    Baen Books – Open to action-heavy supernatural thrillers and pulpier vibes.

    Tor/Forge – Though known for more literary or dark fantasy titles, Tor has imprints open to urban fantasy with mystery elements.

    Angry Robot Books – Publishes quirky, genre-bending speculative fiction, often with a monster-hunting tone.

    Titan Books – Publisher of Supernatural tie-ins, Buffy, etc. Still produces franchise-based and adjacent fiction.

You’d typically need an agent to submit to these, unless you target their open-submission periods or imprints.
🧭 Smaller Presses and Indie-Friendly Publishers

These are often more flexible, easier to query, and more likely to be receptive to Monster Squad-style stories:

    Flame Tree Press – Welcomes traditional horror and supernatural tales with a classic feel.

    Crossroad Press – Publishes supernatural thrillers, urban fantasy, and horror mystery.

    Silver Shamrock Publishing – Was known for traditional horror until it shut down, but others like Cemetery Dance still thrive in that space.

    Trepidatio Publishing (an imprint of JournalStone) – Sometimes leans darker but not always extreme.

    Severed Press – More on the creature-feature side, but they like fun, action-packed horror (think monster hunters with a flamethrower and a quip).

🧙 Self-Publishing and Hybrid Paths

Urban fantasy and supernatural thrillers do very well in the self-publishing space, especially through:

    Kindle Direct Publishing (KDP) – The indie Dresden-style genre is thriving here.

    Draft2Digital / Smashwords – Great for wide distribution beyond Amazon.

    Kickstarter – Creators like Brian McClellan, Dan Wells, and Craig Schaefer have funded non-splatterpunk supernatural thrillers via crowdfunded releases.

There’s a large, loyal audience that prefers this style — action, character-driven plots, spooky but not nihilistic horror — especially in audiobook format. If you're okay with a hybrid or indie path, there’s massive opportunity.
🧟 Popular Comparisons & Comps Still Going Strong

If you’re pitching or querying, reference comps like:

    “The Dresden Files meets The X-Files”

    “Monster Squad with adult consequences”

    “Supernatural but with [your twist]”

    “Buffy meets noir detective fiction”

These comparisons are still viable and popular — the audience hasn’t gone anywhere. They’ve just diversified across indie, trad, and hybrid models.

    Urban fantasy with heart and grit

How can I make my Kings County stuff "noire" or "hardboiled?"

Making your King County material more noire or hardboiled doesn’t mean rewriting it into a cliché gumshoe monologue — unless you want that. Instead, you dial in the tone, moral compass, language, and emotional weight of the world. You’ve already got a great foundation: Rudy is a grizzled lone wolf in a setting full of bad weather, small-town rot, and buried secrets.

Here’s how to push that into noir/hardboiled territory:
🕵️‍♂️ 1. Tone: Cynicism without Nihilism

Classic noir is about broken people trying to do right in a broken world. Hardboiled adds violence and toughness.

    Examples for Rudy:

        He doesn’t believe justice is automatic — he makes it happen, even if it costs him.

        He knows the truth won’t set you free — but it might keep someone else alive a little longer.

Voice it like:

    “I wasn’t looking for a ghost. Just a kid. But King County doesn’t deal in happy endings — it deals in IOUs and gravestones.”

🌧️ 2. Weather & Setting = Mood

Use King County’s natural gloom: rain on rusted cars, snow muffling screams, the flicker of dying fluorescent lights in a roadside diner. These aren't just backdrops — they mirror the characters’ inner decay.

    “It had rained all morning. The kind of rain that drowns footprints and washes away the names on mailboxes.”

🥃 3. Characterization: Flawed, Not Evil

Everyone’s got a secret. No one’s clean. Even good people have bad days — or bad decades.

    Rudy should be jaded but not cold. He's the kind of man who'd take a punch for a stranger, even though he's not sure people are worth saving anymore.

    Jenn-with-two-Ns might have a soft heart, but she's also not telling Rudy everything. No one in noir is just what they seem.

💣 4. Terse, Punchy Prose

Noir is all about what’s not said. Use short sentences, blunt metaphors, and repetition for rhythm.

    “She said the boy didn’t exist. But the boy had a jacket. The jacket had blood. And the blood was fresh.”

Avoid flowery language. If you're going poetic, make it feel gritty or worn.
🖤 5. Themes: Moral Ambiguity & Consequences

What makes noir so powerful is this: the cost of doing the right thing. Rudy shouldn’t always win — and when he does, it should leave a scar.

    Memory is unreliable.

    Justice is subjective.

    The truth has a body count.

Maybe Caleb was never real — but Rudy fights to find him anyway. That’s noir.
💥 6. Violence with Meaning

Hardboiled fiction allows for action, but it’s brutal, fast, and never cool. Fights hurt. Guns are loud and messy. No action sequence should ever leave the protagonist unscathed — physically or emotionally.

    “He pulled the trigger. Not because it was smart, or right. Just because no one else would.”

🔪 7. Dialogue: Dry, Wry, and Defensive

Everyone’s got something to hide. Good noir dialogue is:

    Understated (“That’s a lot of blood.” / “It’s not mine.”)

    Deflective (People answer questions with questions.)

    Tinged with dark humor (“Dead kid, dirty cop, and a dog that won’t shut up. Just another Tuesday.”)

🔚 8. Endings that Linger

In noir, the resolution is rarely happy. Sometimes it’s not even clear. But it should always leave a sting.

    “I found the boy’s name. Just not the boy. And I found out who killed him — if he ever lived at all.”

🧩 Want it in Practice?

You’ve already got the bones:

    Rudy = the hardboiled investigator.

    King County = rain-soaked noir hellscape with rural decay instead of neon shadows.

    Caleb = the phantom child, a metaphor for grief and failed memory.

You don’t need to change King County. You just need to turn up the volume on what’s already there.

How many words is a typical fantasy novel? Like Dragonlance or Forgotten Realms stuff? 

Typical word counts for classic fantasy novels — especially ones like Dragonlance or Forgotten Realms — generally fall in this range:
📚 Standard Word Count Range
Type	Word Count
Classic fantasy novel	90,000–120,000
Shorter/early fantasy	70,000–90,000
Epic fantasy (multi-POV)	120,000–150,000+
🧙 Examples:

    Dragonlance: Dragons of Autumn Twilight – ~118,000 words

    Forgotten Realms: The Crystal Shard – ~96,000 words

    Elminster: The Making of a Mage – ~110,000 words

    Salvatore’s later books tend to hover around 100k–130k

✍️ TL;DR:

If you're writing in that old-school TSR/Wizards of the Coast style, then aim for:

    ~95k–115k words per novel
    That’s the sweet spot — long enough to build a world, tight enough to keep it pulpy and adventurous.

There are many proof readers on Fiverr who have max-word counts that are far less than that. Like, 30,000 words. I'm sure I could just hire the same proofreader over and over, but is it worth having someone proofread only a portion of a piece? 

Great question — and it depends on your goals and where you're at in the process. Here's a breakdown:
✅ When It's Absolutely Worth Having a Partial Proofread:
1. You're testing the waters

You want to see if the proofreader is a good fit before committing to a full manuscript. Send them:

    A representative 10–30k chunk

    Ideally one with both action and quiet moments, narration and dialogue

2. You're polishing for agents/publishers

If you're submitting a sample (usually the first 3 chapters or first 50 pages), getting that section professionally proofread is totally worth it. It’s the first impression.
3. You're releasing episodically (e.g. web serials)

For writers publishing in parts (like Kindle Vella, Substack, etc.), proofing each 10–30k episode makes sense.
🤷‍♂️ When It's Less Worth It:
1. You want comprehensive story-level feedback

Proofreading focuses on grammar, typos, and mechanics — not plot, pacing, or character arcs. If you're still revising story elements, consider editing (developmental or line) instead of proofreading.
2. The rest of your manuscript is rough

If you polish only one section while the rest is still messy, it can throw off your flow and consistency when you go back to revise.
💡 Smart Strategy:

If you're cost-conscious but serious:

    Pick the first 10k-15k of your book

    Hire a proofreader on Fiverr with a good track record

    Evaluate their work (and maybe test two at once)

    Ask if they'll do a bulk discount or custom gig for the whole novel in parts

Many will say yes if they like your writing and know you’re organized.
Final Thought:

    It’s absolutely worth it to proofread part of a book — if you’re strategic about which part and why.
