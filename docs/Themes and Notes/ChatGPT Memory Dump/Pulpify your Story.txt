How can I make What Wasn't and What Is more Pulp? 

To make What Wasn't and What Was more Pulp, you don’t have to abandon the emotional depth or slow-burn mystery — you just need to inject a little more grit, sweat, and punch-per-paragraph. Here's how you can do that without losing the heart of your supernatural noir:
🔥 1. Up the Stakes, Earlier

Pulp stories waste no time. Introduce danger fast, even if it's subtle:

    A child disappears — but leave behind a bloody toy.

    Rudy’s first clue is delivered with a threat — like a knife stuck in his motel doorframe.

    Jamie doesn’t just return — they crawl out of the Hedge bleeding, whispering “it found me again.”

🕶️ 2. Harder Prose, Sharper Dialogue

Adopt the style of a trenchcoat-wearing bastard with a half-smoked cigarette:

    Cut anything soft unless softness is deliberately jarring.

    Use hardboiled metaphors: “The trailer smelled like grief and cheap laundry detergent.”

    Rudy shouldn’t just "notice" something — he clocks it, pegs it, feels it like a punch behind the eyes.

☠️ 3. Make the Threat Physical

Even metaphysical horror can throw a punch.

    Let the Hedge spit out something you can shoot — even if bullets don’t work.

    The Huntsman? Make him hunt. Rip out a secondary character’s eyes, just to watch Rudy flinch.

    Jamie can’t sleep — maybe they scream in withdrawal if they try, shaking with too many days of consciousness.

🏚️ 4. Wider World, Stranger Rules

Pulp worldbuilding thrives on rumors, half-truths, and dangerous knowledge:

    There's a bar Rudy goes to where the drinks don’t stay down if you lie.

    A conspiracy of missing kids tied to a defunct department store — and its unlit third floor.

    Mickey’s blood magic could involve pulp-y ingredients: animal tongues, mirror shards, gasoline-soaked prayer books.

💔 5. Tragic Archetypes, Pulpified

Let characters lean into pulp tropes, even as you subvert them:

    Rudy becomes the "cursed gumshoe" — every case takes something from him.

    Jamie is the "lost kid with a thousand-yard stare" — mysterious, maybe dangerous.

    Jenn is the "good woman working nights," unaware she's being dragged toward something old and ugly.

🕳️ 6. Corruption Runs Deep

Noir and pulp revel in systemic rot.

    The cops aren't just indifferent — they’re in on it. One used to hunt things, like Rudy.

    The town library burned its occult section in 1972... or so they say.

    A local pastor used to run exorcisms — now he drinks turpentine and hums nursery rhymes.

🔚 7. End Every Act With a Gut Punch

Keep your cliffhangers visceral:

    Rudy finds Caleb’s shoe — and it’s still warm.

    Jamie says, “I remember who I was. I think he’s still in there.”

    The case files are empty — except one: Rudy’s own childhood photo.

Not quite yet. What about Gothic horror? How can I shift toward that? 

Ah, Gothic horror. Now we’re talking crumbling places, decaying minds, inherited grief, and monsters that wear the faces of those we love. Shifting What Wasn't and What Was toward Gothic horror means emphasizing atmosphere, ancestry, decay, and dread, without sacrificing your core story.

Here’s how to twist the lens and let the shadows stretch longer:
🕯️ 1. The Past Is Never Dead

    Gothic horror is obsessed with inheritance — emotional, spiritual, architectural.

    The trailer park isn’t just poor, it’s cursed — maybe the ground was once a churchyard, or there’s an old well sealed in concrete behind Leanne’s unit.

    Rudy’s old case? Echoes of his past are in the new one. Maybe he failed someone once. Maybe they whisper in his dreams.

    Jamie didn’t just escape Arcadia. They brought something with them. A voice. A look in their eyes that isn’t always theirs.

🕳️ 2. Make the Setting a Character

    In Gothic horror, places breathe. Places remember.

    The Hedge should feel like a rotting manor — overgrown, crumbling, filled with familiar furniture in the wrong places.

    The library? Built in 1904, flooded in 1931, burned in 1952, reopened in 1970 — and it still has all the original books.

    The barn Jamie hides in might have carvings in the beams — names, sigils, dates that haven’t happened yet.

😶 3. Dread Over Shock

    Pulp hits fast. Gothic lingers. Let fear ferment.

    Swap out jump scares for long silences, flickering memories, and slow realizations.

    Instead of the Huntsman appearing in full — we see his hat, his shadow, his fingertip brushing the edge of a mirror.

    The townsfolk know something is wrong, but won’t say it out loud — just glance nervously at Caleb’s mother, or spit on the ground when Jamie walks by.

🩸 4. Body and Mind Are Fragile Things

    Madness is as terrifying as monsters.

    Jamie can’t sleep. But maybe they start to hallucinate sleep — waking dreams. Murmured lullabies. Phantom bruises.

    Rudy finds his own notes rewritten in a hand that isn’t his.

    Leanne’s grief becomes ritualistic — she lays out Caleb’s shoes every night, facing the door. Sometimes they’re moved in the morning.

🖤 5. Emotion Is the Monster

    At its heart, Gothic horror is about suffering you can’t put down.

    The story becomes less about solving a case and more about bearing witness to a wound that keeps reopening.

    The fear isn’t that Caleb is gone. The fear is that he was never real, and that you can feel a child's absence in your arms and still be told you're insane.

    The Hedge becomes not just a place, but a metaphor for grief — tangled, consuming, filled with memories that aren’t yours anymore.

🩶 6. Symbolism Everywhere

    Gothic horror loves mirrors, birds, blood, weather, rot.

    Mirrors crack when Jamie passes.

    Rudy bleeds every time he reads a certain case file. Just a little. Just enough to stain the page.

    Caleb’s old room smells like wet leaves and milk teeth.

    Thunder doesn’t bring rain — it brings silence.

🪦 7. No Happy Ending. Just Bittersweet Survival

    If the mystery is solved, it’s not without a cost. The cost is usually hope.

    Rudy saves the kid, but can’t remember his name afterward.

    Jamie survives, but has to leave. Permanently. They’re too close to fading.

    Leanne chooses to believe in Caleb again — and forgets Rudy entirely.
