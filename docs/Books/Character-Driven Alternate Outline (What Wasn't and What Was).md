Another alternate pass at outlining [[What Wasn't and What Was]], this time using a "Character-Driven Plot Outline" framework (also from the Novel Factory), centered on Rudy's want vs. need. Distinct from both the main Act 1–3 outline and the [[Detective Noir Alternate Outline (What Wasn't and What Was)|Detective Noir alternate outline]].

**Status: confirmed non-canon as an outline (2026-07-15, decided live, grill-canon session).** Like the other three Novel Factory passes, this was a drafting exercise, not a competing draft — the main Act 1.1–3.6 outline is the sole canonical plan. That said, Dave's own verdict on this specific pass survives that decision:

**User's own verdict on this one (important — treat differently from a pure exercise):** "This one isn't perfect for the story... but it revealed some things that I'll definitely include." So while the beat framework itself isn't canon, specific pieces below are more likely to be real intended material than pure what-if brainstorming — just not marked which ones specifically. The Huntsman resolution below, however, is retired along with the outline — see [[The Huntsman]].

**Rudy's want vs. need (likely a keeper — strong, clean character-arc analysis):**
- **Want:** Closure — solving the next case, believing each solved mystery brings him closer to silencing the ghosts of [[The California Incident]].
- **Need:** Connection and belief in redemption — reconnecting with his own humanity, believing again in hope/love/change, that someone like him can still matter to someone else. Manifests through his relationship with [[Jamie]], and ultimately [[Leanne]] and [[Caleb]].

**1.1 – Incomplete:** Rudy living in a trailer at the edge of King County (in tension with his established Winnebago-behind-Shepherd's-Inn home — same kind of conflict flagged on the Detective Noir outline). Draft prose beats: a visor photo of "him and a bunch of kids, way back... a girl's scribbled name on the back. Ashley." A voicemail from [[Jenn-with-two-Ns|Jenn]] just checking if he's sleeping, deleted without listening all the way through. Line: "One day I'll just say no. One day. But he won't. He never does."

**1.2 – Unsettled:** [[Detective Marcus Cole|Marcus Cole]] calls about a missing kid — the mother's story doesn't add up, half the town swears the kid never existed. Line: "You ever heard of a kid named Caleb Raines? 'Cause half the town swears he never existed — and his mom's losing her mind saying otherwise." (Note: this outline calls him "Caleb Raines," not the established "Caleb Thomas" — see the discrepancy note on [[Caleb]]'s page.) A mysterious clue: a blank envelope with no address or stamp arrives in Rudy's mail, containing only a child's crayon drawing — "two stick figures holding hands beneath a crooked sun." Rudy doesn't know where it came from.

**3.1 – Decisive (a fourth climax draft):** In the Hedge, the Huntsman (wielding "a blade of bark and rusted bone") is about to move on Jamie, half-rooted; Caleb stands nearby under a thorn tree, flickering between there and not. The Huntsman taunts Rudy to end it the way he always does — with violence. Instead, Rudy chooses *not* to fight: he turns to Caleb and speaks loving, specific memory into being — "You're Caleb Raines. You like sour ropes and drawing monsters. You used to check out the same damn Berenstain Bears book every week. You are loved." (Note: "checked out... every week" recurs here too, reinforcing the tension with the established "never checked out" library clue — see [[What Wasn't and What Was]].) The Huntsman screams as the world tries to erase Rudy in turn, but Jamie stirs and Caleb looks up: "You came." / "I never stopped." Outcome: the Huntsman dissolves — "not killed, but starved," its story rewritten rather than destroyed by force. Jamie returns to their full self, changed but whole; Caleb becomes solid and undeniable. "He doesn't walk out of the Hedge a warrior. He walks out a man holding a child's hand."

**3.2 – Complete (a fourth ending draft):** Caleb exists again, gradually remembered by others too — a school photo, a library card, a shared memory between two kids that shouldn't be there but is. Jamie is whole — still Moss, still changed, but no longer lost — and **sleeps sometimes now, not much, but enough**; stays a little while longer before hopping trains again. Rudy is "not cured, not redeemed, just better" — gets coffee with Jenn, returns a crumpled drawing to Leanne with "Caleb Raines" etched in crayon. Draft prose: a photo arrives in the mail two weeks later, no return address, just "Thanks" in a kid's blocky handwriting on the back — the first thing Rudy's pinned to his fridge in years. A porch scene with Jamie over coffee: "You sure you're leaving?" / "Don't belong anywhere too long." / "You could." / [no answer, just a smile] / "You're not who I thought you were." / "Neither are you." That night, Rudy doesn't watch the train disappear — sits on the porch, whispers the names of every kid he's tried to save, and ends with one that finally sticks: "Caleb." "And this time, the world didn't flinch."
