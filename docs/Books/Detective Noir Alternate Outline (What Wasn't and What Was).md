An alternate pass at outlining [[What Wasn't and What Was]] using a "Detective Noir Plot Outline" framework (from a tool called the Novel Factory) — a different beat structure than the Act 1–3 outline already documented on the book's main page. **Status: confirmed non-canon (2026-07-15, decided live, grill-canon session).** This was a drafting exercise — running the story through the Novel Factory tool's genre templates — not a competing draft. The main Act 1.1–3.6 outline is the sole canonical plan; where the two conflict, the main outline wins. Preserved here for the record, not as a live alternative. See [[Development Notes & Unresolved Questions]].

**Known conflicts with the main Act 1–3 outline:**
- Rudy is described here as living in "a barely functional motel room" and drinking too much/talking to people who aren't there — this contradicts his established home (a Winnebago behind [[Shepherd's Inn]]) and his established sober, controlled characterization (a "drunk" framing was explicitly rejected once before for a different scene — see [[Out of Scope Ideas]]).
- The Leanne meeting scene here happens over gas station coffee / at his office / the diner / the motel lobby, rather than her coming to the Inn (established Act 1.4).
- The ending here is much bleaker: nobody but Rudy remembers what happened, Jamie's fate is left ambiguous ("headed west, still alive" is only an optional glimmer), and Caleb is only "back in some form" rather than the fuller resolution in the main outline's Act 3.

## 1.1 – Start Low: Establish Rudy as the Fallen Hero
Rudy as ex-military, "ex-some-kind-of-law," a haunted blue-collar PI. Wakes with joint pain — an old injury, a plate in his leg. A brick-like Nokia phone full of ignored voicemails (consistent with the setting's intentionally tech-dead/anachronistic feel). Alienation: not trusted by police, avoided by townsfolk; only Marcus Cole and Jenn treat him with wary respect or pity. Hints of past heroism: medals in a drawer, a newspaper clipping, someone sarcastically calling him "hero." Internal conflict: sleep eludes him, he drinks too much, talks to people who aren't there (see conflict note above). Mood: fall sliding into winter, harsh grey light, an early Caleb flyer already blowing down the street, ignored. Optional emotional hook: a little boy waves at him from a school bus stop; there's no one there.

## 1.2 – The Siren Walks In: Leanne's Arrival
Leanne cast as a subversion of the noir siren — not glamorous, but disheveled, exhausted, magnetic in quiet desperation. She hands Rudy a missing-child flyer for Caleb. Not hysterical, focused; something like guilt in her eyes. Noir elements: police think she's unstable, there's already talk of social services (consistent with the CPS worker established elsewhere); Rudy senses she's not being fully honest; the case feels personal to him (the trailer park reminds him of where he grew up — possible new backstory detail, see [[Rudy Williams]]); she's "the fulcrum around which the entire reality of the story bends." Sample dialogue: Rudy: "You sure he didn't just run off?" Leanne says nothing, just: "Will you help me?" Rudy already knows he has.

## 2.4 – On the Run: The Turn
Rudy wakes hungover, motel trashed, still trying to forget everything — until the threat hits home. Two stackable triggers:
- **Jamie disappears:** their things left behind, a lingering "root-cellar smell" (echoes the Act 2.1 title, "The Root Cellar and the Thorn"), a note in slashing childlike handwriting on the mirror: "He's not yours to protect." Rudy realizes the Huntsman took Jamie — not just Jamie, but their belief in him: "If Jamie stops believing in Rudy… Rudy won't come back." A mirrored belief-mechanic to Leanne/Caleb's.
- **The tape:** a VHS or burned CD Rudy doesn't remember acquiring plays a grainy home video — a birthday party, a kid laughing, Leanne's voice — then static, "a face with no face," then silence. He throws it: "Fuck this."

Acceleration: digs up iron bullets and an old cold iron knife "from his war days"; goes to Marcus for old files; breaks into a church; "steals from the dead" (implies grave robbing); returns to the barn/creek/the Hedge — "Jamie said it only opens if you're ready to be changed." Rudy is ready. Closing hook: at the edge of the Hedge, crowbar in one hand, "a drawing" in the other (same ambiguous-whose-drawing object flagged on [[Caleb]]'s page), lighter clicking open: "You want me forgotten? Gonna have to earn it."

## 3.1 – Final Conflict: Into the Thorns
Inside the Hedge — tangled woods, crooked reflections, rusted playgrounds, wrong-colored sky, time like water. The Huntsman is there; Jamie is strung up but not broken; Caleb flickers in and out of being there — "a shadow, a boy, a memory with a heartbeat." **The Huntsman's confirmed nature:** not just a creature but a function — "a servant of doubt, a predator of belief" — shape-shifting between a man, a beast, Leanne's face, and Rudy's own face. This directly parallels the "villain is disbelief itself" theme already established for the Caleb plot (see [[What Wasn't and What Was]]) — the Huntsman may be a literalization of that same force. Its line: "You don't believe in yourself. You never did. That's why they die." Rudy: "Still here, aren't I?"

**The trap (confirmed mechanic):** Rudy has been baiting the Huntsman the whole time — the drawing in his coat was iron-laced; the tape carried part of Caleb's true name layered in static; Rudy secretly copied and planted Caleb's missing flyer all over town to "let the town see, let belief regrow." The trap is consensus: the town's collective belief in Caleb regrowing is what makes the Huntsman vulnerable — a direct payoff of the collective-belief mechanic established in [[Project Chronicle Summary]]'s notes.

The fight is dirty and brutal; Rudy is outmatched but doesn't back down, burning sage and swinging iron; Jamie breaks free and joins him. They don't destroy the Huntsman — "you can't destroy doubt" — they cut away its power and make it irrelevant rather than killing it. Victory isn't clean: Rudy collapses, leg broken again, ribs shattered, and sees Jamie clearly for the first time. Jamie: "Told you I'd come back." They drag him out — Jamie limping, Rudy half-conscious, Caleb trailing behind, flickering but smiling.

## 3.2 – Back to the Office: The Long Fade
Rudy back at Shepherd's Inn; the case is closed "technically." Caleb is back in some form; Leanne believes again — maybe a blurry Polaroid of the two of them on Rudy's desk. But no one else remembers what happened; no one talks about Jamie; the flyers are gone again; the photo on the library wall has different kids on it now; the tape won't play. The Hedge is still there. Rudy is physically battered (a cane or brace on the old leg) and mentally worse, but with a grim peace — he doesn't sleep better, he "just knows more about the dark." A knock at the door: "If it's another missing kid, I charge double" — but he still listens.

Optional, unconfirmed glimmers of hope (hardboiled-style, pick one or neither): Jenn brings him coffee and mentions seeing Jamie pass through town again, headed west, still alive and real. Or: the gas station clerk leaves a candy bar on Rudy's dash (a callback to Jamie's candy habit) wrapped around a tiny hand-drawn flyer reading "Missing: Caleb. But not gone." Either way, Rudy lights a smoke as the sun barely comes up and mutters: "Good enough." The door closes.
