## Save the Cat! Beat Sheet
1. **Opening Image:** Rudy wakes alone at Shepherd's Inn — a world where belief shapes reality but no one believes in anything anymore.
2. **Theme Stated:** "Sometimes forgetting is worse than losing" — spoken by Jamie or Jenn. A strong candidate thematic tagline (see [[What Wasn't and What Was]]).
3. **Set-Up:** Rudy, Jamie, and the town introduced; Rudy gets the Caleb call; Leanne's story has holes; locals remember Caleb "kind of."
4. **Catalyst:** The Caleb/Jacob flicker; Rudy starts to suspect Caleb may never have existed, "and yet... he did."
5. **Debate:** Rudy doubts himself; consults Jamie, who reveals their Hedge connection; wonders if he's "going to stir up old magic again" — implies Rudy swore off his old methods/magic at some point before the story starts (undeveloped).
6. **Break into Two:** Rudy chooses to enter the Hedge with Jamie — choosing to believe again.
7. **B Story:** Rudy and Jamie's bond deepens ("Rudy has never been friends with a monster before"); Jenn offers grounded emotional support.
8. **Fun and Games:** Investigation through the Hedge and memory; they rescue the second missing child (see the confirmed addition on [[What Wasn't and What Was]]), who confirms Caleb was never there.
9. **Midpoint (False Victory):** Definitive proof that Caleb was never born; breaks Rudy; still time to get him back.
10. **Bad Guys Close In:** Leanne's doubt weakens Caleb's presence further; the Huntsman comes after Rudy; Rudy gets hurt and **starts to forget Caleb's face himself** — in tension with the established "Rudy is the only one who still says Caleb's name" (Act 2.7 on the main outline).
11. **All Is Lost:** Caleb disappears completely, no one remembers him, not even Rudy; the photograph goes blank, the poster tears, the name fades. Leanne "had started to forget on purpose" — framed as an active, self-protective choice rather than passive drift.
12. **Dark Night of the Soul:** Rudy, broken: "What's the point of saving a ghost?" Jamie: "He was real. I remember." Rudy drinks bad tea: "It's not good." Jamie: "It's good enough." — **note: this is the tea/"good enough" exchange recurring at a different point in the story than the epilogue version documented elsewhere; may indicate the exchange is meant to recur more than once, not just at the very end.**
13. **Break into Three:** Rudy chooses belief; creates a trail of memory by visiting people who saw Caleb, reawakening belief (echoes the flyer-consensus mechanic from the Detective Noir outline).
14. **Finale:** Rudy presents evidence; Leanne chooses to believe; the town remembers; Caleb returns.
15. **Final Image (an eighth ending draft):** A photo arrives — Leanne and Caleb together. Rudy tapes it to the mirror, then burns a second copy and scatters the ashes into the wind. "Some things aren't meant to last. But belief? That's enough."

## "Stage 12: The Fallout" (a ninth, notably darker epilogue draft)
- **Caleb comes home:** Leanne opens the door; Caleb asks for cereal "like he never left." The town acts like of course he was always there — no fanfare, no police, no celebration. Leanne alone remembers the hole and the day she chose to believe again — "that is what makes him real."
- **Rudy is not the same:** Sits on the porch with a photo taped to the window — a crayon drawing inscribed "To Mom" (another version of the recurring ambiguous "drawing" object, this one explicitly tied to Caleb via the inscription). Hand trembles lighting a cigarette; doesn't fully trust the world to stay solid, doesn't know if he's really out of the Hedge — but he remembers Caleb, "and that means he won," even if no one else believes him and even if he doubts again.
- **Jamie leaves:** The day after Caleb returns. Bed made, a single train ticket on the nightstand, a candy wrapper in the sink, a smudged message on the mirror: "Good enough." No one else remembers them clearly, but Rudy does: "They kept the door open, so we didn't have to."
- **The Huntsman is gone but not destroyed:** Optional coda images (none confirmed as chosen) — hoofprints in the snow outside the barn, one set then nothing; a missing pet poster with the name scratched out; a new kid drawing a shadow with antlers in crayon; or unnatural quiet in the woods behind the creek, "like something is waiting for a name to vanish." "The Huntsman is not dead. It never really was. It's a consequence. And forgetting? That never goes out of style." Closing question: not "will it come back," but "who will remember next time?"

## Craft Discussion: Keep the Huntsman/Hedge Symbolic, Not Structural
Raised as a real concern (not just brainstorm) — ramping up the Hedge/Huntsman as scary risks making the actual emotional reveal feel weaker by comparison. Resolution principle, which reinforces "the villain is disbelief itself" already established on the main outline: **the Hedge = the boundary between denial and memory; the Huntsman = the consequence of forgetting. They protect the untruth — they aren't its source.** Reverse the expected horror-genre logic: not "defeat the Huntsman → save Caleb," but "choose to remember Caleb → break the Huntsman" (the moment Leanne remembers, the Huntsman's shape/voice starts to fail, because the forgetting it guards is unraveling). This keeps the climax internal and earned rather than a bait-and-switch — the reader should feel enlightened when the Hedge turns out not to be the answer, not duped. The user found this compelling but didn't develop a scene from it in this chat.

## Romance-Structure Reframing of Rudy & Jamie
Explicitly non-romantic — "devotion, healing, chosen family" mapped onto a standard romance arc:
1. **Meet Cute:** Rudy rescues young Jamie from the Hedge years ago — not love at first sight, but responsibility, an unsolved mystery. They separate; when the Caleb case starts, Jamie returns, and "this time they need each other."
2. **Push & Pull:** Jamie: "This isn't your kind of fight." Rudy: "You're still in it whether you like it or not." They fight like siblings/survivors but don't walk away.
3. **Forced Proximity:** They investigate together, enter the Hedge together, face the Huntsman/the town's forgetting together — "no one else will ever understand this but us."
4. **The Rift:** After a failed confrontation with the Huntsman (a beat not otherwise documented — implies a mid-story defeat/setback), Jamie blames themself for the Monster, Rudy blames himself for believing he could fix it. They don't say goodbye, just stop speaking; each thinks the other is better off without them.
5. **The Grand Gesture:** Rudy realizes it's about belief, not control. Jamie returns — "not for the town, not even for Caleb, but for Rudy." They enter the Hedge together one last time. Jamie: "If you believe in him, I believe in you." Rudy: "I believe in both of you."
6. **The Sacrifice:** To bring Caleb back, Rudy is willing to stay behind; Jamie is willing to give up their last memory of Arcadia — "one of them must pay, or both must risk paying." Not about proving love, but "being willing to be forgotten so someone else can be remembered."
7. **HEA/HFN:** Caleb comes home, Leanne remembers, Jamie leaves — but Rudy keeps their candy wrapper in his drawer. Rudy finally sleeps, "not because the world is fixed, but because someone was saved."
